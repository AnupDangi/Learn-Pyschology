# PSYC 110 - Lecture 14 - What Motivates Us: Sex

Source: https://oyc.yale.edu/psychology/psyc-110/lecture-14

## Overview

Professor Paul Bloom frames sex as both an oddly minor time commitment (an average of four minutes and three seconds per day for the average American) and an extraordinarily important force from which "everything in life follows" — marriage, family, children, aggression, competition, and much of art. Before presenting biological material, he addresses two prefatory concerns: morality (nothing moral necessarily follows from the facts of biology) and inevitability (genetic origin does not entail unchangeability). He then walks through the evolutionary logic of why two sexes exist, parental investment theory and its predictions across species, and applies it to humans: sex differences in size, aggression, sexual choosiness, and mate preferences. The lecture next examines physical beauty as a marker of youth and health (including the attractiveness of average faces), discusses possible social contributions to sex differences (empathy, math/science aptitude debates), and ends with the evolutionary puzzle of exclusive homosexuality.

## Key Concepts & Definitions

- **Male / Female (biological definition)**: Males have a small sex cell (sperm — genes only). Females have a big sex cell (egg — genes plus food and protective cover).
- **Parental investment** (Robert Trivers): "Any investment that's going to increase the offspring's chance of survival at the cost of the parent's ability to invest in other offspring."
- **Serial monogamy**: The pattern attributed to American culture — a series of pair-bondings rather than mating for life.
- **Coolidge Effect**: A preference for sexual variety, named after the Calvin Coolidge anecdote about the rooster and different hens.
- **Sexual choosiness**: The degree to which an individual is selective about (or receptive to) sexual partners, including anonymous sex.
- **Beauty (as signal)**: A marker of (a) youth (sexually mature but young) and (b) health (absence of deformities, clear eyes, unblemished skin, intact teeth, average face).
- **Average face hypothesis**: Average faces are attractive because averaging factors out deviations/deformities.
- **Gender self-segregation**: Developmental tendency (roughly age four to eleven) of males to group with males and females with females, potentially amplifying sex differences (Eleanor Maccoby).
- **Sexual orientation (vs. behavior)**: Orientation concerns what one wants (whom one would be sexually/romantically involved with all things equal), not whom one has actually had sex with.

## Detailed Notes

### Chapter 1. Addressing Morality and Inevitability on Evolution, Sex and Gender

- Opening puzzle: People (especially college students) name sex as their favorite activity, but data summarized in James Gleick's book show the average American spends **four minutes and three seconds per day** on sex — about the same time spent filling out IRS tax forms.
- Gleick passage lists what the four minutes excludes: "flirting, dancing, ogling, cruising the boulevard, toning up in gyms, toning up in beauty parlors, rehearsing pick up lines, showering, thinking about sex, reading about sex, doodling pornographically, looking at erotic magazines, renting videos, dreaming of sex, looking at fashion magazines, cleaning up after sex, coping with the consequences of sex, building towers or otherwise repressing, transferring, and sublimating."
- Two takeaways: (1) we don't spend much brute time on sex; (2) it remains extraordinarily important — marriage, family, children, aggression, competition, art, music, creativity all follow from it.
- Some creatures reproduce by **cloning**. The existence of two sexes is itself an evolutionary mystery: each offspring carries only half a parent's DNA, so sexual reproduction "tosses away" half the genes. (Not the puzzle of today's class.)
- Today's questions: theoretical and empirical sex differences; sexual attractiveness; origins of sexual preference (straight, gay, bisexual, harder to classify).
- Sex is "dicey" emotionally and fraught with moral implications. Bloom flags two issues at the outset:

**Morality** — Quote from Steve Pinker, *How the Mind Works*:
> Nature does not dictate what we should accept or how we should live our lives. Well into my procreating years, I am so far voluntarily childless, having squandered my biological resources reading and writing, doing research, helping friends and students, and jogging in circles — ignoring the solemn imperative to spread my genes. By Darwinian standards, I am a horrible mistake, a pathetic loser, but I am happy to be that way, and if my genes don't like it they can go jump in the lake.

- If "right" = leads to gene reproduction, you wouldn't think much of birth control or non-procreative sex. If "wrong" = unnatural, you wouldn't think much of planes, refrigeration, or surviving infections. Bloom: "Nothing moral necessarily follows from the facts of biology."

**Inevitability** — Quote from Richard Dawkins:
> If a child has had bad teaching in mathematics, it is accepted that a resulting deficiency can be remedied by extra-good teaching in the following year. But any suggestion that the child's deficiency might have a genetic origin is likely to be greeted with something approaching despair. If it's in the genes, it is determined and nothing can be done about it. This is pernicious nonsense on an almost astrological scale. Genetic causes and environmental causes are in principle no different from each other. Some may be harder to reverse, others may be easy. What did genes do to deserve their sinister, juggernaut-like reputation? Why are genes thought to be so much more fixed and inescapable in their effects than television, nuns or books.

- Examples:
  - **Bloom's poor eyesight**: genetically determined, but easily corrected with glasses/contact lenses.
  - **Society's treatment of the obese**: views about thin/fat vary cross-culturally (not hard-wired), but once in a culture they are "almost impossible to shake."
- Conclusion: "Genetic does not mean inevitable, and cultural does not mean easy to fix."

### Chapter 2. Basic Sex Ed

- Don't define male/female by penis/vagina — many animals have neither. Biological definition rests on sex cell size:
  - **Male**: small sex cell (sperm) — genes only.
  - **Female**: big sex cell (egg) — genes plus food and protective cover.
- Puzzle: if males have the smaller cell, why are males often larger and more aggressive?

**Parental investment theory (Robert Trivers)**:
- Definition: investment increasing offspring's survival at the cost of investing in other offspring.
- Within a species, females typically have higher parental investment: big sex cells, often internally incubated; eggs may be sat on; carrying one offspring blocks another.
- Males with small sex cells can sometimes invest only "a few moments of copulation."
- Consequences:
  - A single male can fertilize multiple females; some males go mate-less → male-male competition.
  - Females can always find mates, so numbers don't matter — they compete to mate with the *right* males (best chance of surviving offspring).
  - Explains evolution of larger males, special weapons (for male-male combat), and male display structures (e.g., peacock plumage) to woo choosy females.
- Causal chain: sex cell size differences → parental investment differences → differences in psychological/physiological mechanisms.

**Predictions and species evidence**:

| Species | Parental investment pattern | Size / behavior |
|---|---|---|
| Pipefish | Male takes eggs into a pouch, plugs into bloodstream; females shoot off | Females larger; females fight other females; females compete for male attention |
| Penguins (March of the Penguins) | Both parents must protect egg or it dies — equal investment | Males and females about the same size |
| Elephant seals | Males compete for harems — winner-take-all | Males four times bigger than females |
| Gibbons | Pretty monogamous; raise children together | About the same size |

- Equal-investment scenarios arise when offspring need both parents and effort can't be shared with another family. Hard to find perfect cases; some primates are close.

### Chapter 3. Sex Differences among Humans

- Humans are **relatively polygamous**; most cultures are polygamous. American culture = "serial monogamy" — a series of pair-bondings, not lifelong mating.
- **Size**: human males average about **15% larger** than human females, implying some male-male competition history → parental investment is not fully equal.
- **Testicle size**: smaller than chimpanzees, larger than gorillas and gibbons — intermediate sperm competition. Suggests women in evolutionary history were "not wantonly promiscuous, but were not entirely monogamous either."

**Aggression**:
- "Males are meaner" (not technical). Males are more physically violent:
  - More violent in utero (kick more).
  - More violent as children (play fighting, combat-like sports).
  - More violent as adults (prisons mostly hold men; more likely to kill/harm).
- Male hormones like **testosterone** increase aggressiveness in humans and other primates.

**Sexual choosiness / anonymous sex**:
- Parental investment theory predicts males more receptive to anonymous sex (pre-birth-control logic).
- Cross-cultural / behavioral evidence:
  - **Prostitution**: near-universally a male interest. Male prostitutes mostly cater to male customers.
  - **Pornography**: universal — males in every society depict naked females for arousal (sometimes carved into trees, sculptures).
  - **Monkey porn (Duke study)**: monkeys "paid" in fruit juice to look at pictures of female hindquarters or socially dominant ("celebrity") monkeys — like "some sort of combination of People Magazine and Penthouse."

**Coolidge Effect**:
- Anecdote about President Calvin Coolidge and Mrs. Coolidge being shown around a farm; the rooster has sex many times a day — different hen each time. Mrs. Coolidge: "Well, be sure to tell that to the president." President Coolidge, on hearing the rooster mates many times a day: "Same hen every time?" — "No, different hen every time." — "Tell that to Mrs. Coolidge."

**Survey data on desired sexual partners**:

| Time frame | Women's typical answer | Men's typical answer |
|---|---|---|
| Next month | < 1 | 2 |
| Next 2 years | 1 | 8 |
| Lifetime | 4–5 | 18 |

**Would you have sex with a very desirable partner known for…**

| Known for | Women | Men |
|---|---|---|
| 1 year | Yes | Yes |
| 6 months | Unsure | Yes |
| 1 week or less | No | Yes (majority say yes down to 5 minutes) |

**Field experiment** (~10 years ago, "wouldn't do nowadays"): Attractive male and female confederates approached strangers on campus.
- Of women approached by an attractive man: over half agreed to "go out with me tonight"; very few agreed to come to his apartment; **nobody** agreed to go to bed with him.
- Of men approached by an attractive woman: "go out" ~50%, "come to apartment" ~69%, "go to bed" ~75% agreed.
- The 25% of men who said no "apologized profusely" (e.g., fiancée in town).

**Behavior — same-sex couples as natural test**:
- Heterosexual frequency is a compromise between male and female priorities. Gay couples provide a "pure reflection" of one-sex desire.
- Data are messy but consistently in the expected direction: lesbians tend to be more monogamous than gay men. Pre-AIDS studies found some gay men extremely promiscuous (over a hundred or over a thousand partners). Such promiscuity not found in females.
- Interpretation: gay men do what the average heterosexual man would do if presented with equally willing women.

**Mate preferences / attractiveness — Cross-cultural study**:
- 10,000 people, 37 countries — asked who they want to be with (marry, mate, sexual partner).
- Universals: everyone values **kindness and intelligence** (or says they do).
- Sex differences:
  - **Females** focus more on power, status, and the male's interest in investing in children. Evolutionary rationale: a man's age (15 vs. 25 vs. 35 vs. 45) matters less for sperm; what matters is his being a good father, protector against predation/murder/assault.
  - **Males** focus on those traits too, but also on **ability to have children** — large male-perceived difference between a 20-year-old and 50-year-old female.

### Chapter 4. Beauty: An Average Face

- Beauty signals two things:
  1. **Youth** — sexually mature but young. Cues: large eyes, full lips, smooth tight skin.
  2. **Health** — absence of deformities, clear eyes, unblemished skin, intact teeth (very important), **average face**.
- Why average faces? Deformities = deviations from the average; averaging factors out anything bad (distortion, asymmetry, age-related changes).
- **Empirical evidence**:
  - Researchers morph faces together. As more faces are averaged, observers rate them as better-looking — "subtle, but it's actually not so subtle that babies don't notice it."
  - Same researchers showed averaged Caucasian male and female composites to **babies**, who preferred to look at average faces → preference not purely cultural; partly hard-wired.
  - Composite male and female faces "don't exist" — pure computer composites with hair held constant; they look attractive.
- **Beyond average**:
  - A **feminized** version of the average female face (certain prototype features exaggerated as more feminine) tends to be preferred → cues a stronger "sexual object."
  - For male faces: most people prefer the less-chiseled face B (looks healthy, young, strong, good provider). However, **women who are ovulating** prefer the more chiseled, exaggerated face A (replicated in three labs). Interpretation summarized: face A "may not be a good provider… but I'm sure he has wonderful genes" — have sex with him, have face B raise the kids.

### Chapter 5. Social Factors for Sex Differences

- Some sex differences (aggression, mate preference) appear universal across cultures and are good biological-adaptation candidates. Others are debated.
- **Differential treatment of babies**: Studies — swaddle a baby in blue and call it a boy vs. swaddle in pink and call it a girl, and people treat it differently.
- **Name effects on adult treatment**: Sending an email, job application, or paper to a scientific journal as **John Smith vs. Joan Smith** elicits different reactions and expectations. Anecdote: Bloom's friend Lynn is often mistaken for female.
- **Gender self-segregation** (Eleanor Maccoby): Children self-segregate by sex roughly from **age 4 to age 11**. May exaggerate small initial differences — e.g., slightly more aggressive boys become more aggressive in all-boy groups, while girls' non-aggressive behavior is reinforced in girl groups.

**Empathy (Simon Baron-Cohen, *The Essential Difference*)**:
- Claim: men are by nature less empathetic; women more empathetic — a core sex difference.
- Evidence cited:
  - Murder = the ultimate act of lack of empathy; men more violent.
  - Testosterone ↑ → less social.
  - Boys less empathetic than girls; some (debated) evidence boys do worse on social cognition / theory of mind tasks.
  - Disorders of empathy/social cognition (autism, Asperger's Syndrome, conduct disorder, psychopathy) are predominantly male.
- Baron-Cohen's slogan: "To be a man is to suffer from a particularly mild form of autism."
- Trivia: Simon Baron-Cohen is a famous developmental psychologist; his cousin Sasha Baron Cohen is more famous.

**Math and science aptitude (Larry Summers controversy)**:
- Larry Summers, then president of Harvard, suggested under-representation of women in science/engineering was partly due to "issues of intrinsic aptitude, and particularly of the variability of aptitude."
- Summers agreed average skills are identical; claim was that males show more **variability** → more retarded males and more male geniuses.
- Highly controversial. Bloom directs students to the **Pinker–Spelke debate** at Harvard (Steve Pinker vs. Liz Spelke, a major infant cognition researcher), available on video at The Edge (http://www.edge.org/3rd_culture/debate05/debate05_index.html).

### Chapter 6. Sexual Orientation

- Approximate (hard to pin down) figures: ~98% of women are sexually attracted to men; ~96% of men are sexually attracted to women.
- Some proportion is exclusively homosexual; some bisexual (varies cross-culturally).
- **Orientation ≠ behavior**: People may have same-sex sex out of boredom or experimentation. Orientation = what you want, if all things were equal.
- The "real puzzle" is **exclusive homosexuality**.

**Why? What's probably NOT the explanation**:
- Not a **conscious choice**: gay people often face severe discrimination and may even believe being gay is morally wrong — implausible they would choose it.
- Not **post-pubertal seduction/experience**: signs of orientation (sexual and romantic fantasies) appear long before puberty.

**Genetic / built-in?** — Partly.
- Behavioral genetics: compare monozygotic vs. dizygotic twins, adopted vs. biological siblings.
- Concordance: if one identical twin is gay, the other is gay about **50%** of the time — high vs. population baseline, but not 100% as it would be if purely genetic.
- So some experience (possibly **prenatal**) also contributes.

**Why is exclusive homosexuality an evolutionary puzzle?**
- Not why some men sometimes have sex with men (recreation, pair bonding).
- The puzzle: why are there men who don't want to have sex with women (and women who don't want to have sex with men)? Evolutionarily, the underlying genes should be weeded out because such individuals typically (pre-modern technology) don't reproduce.
- Reading response assignment for the week: "solve that puzzle." Account should bear some relationship to the facts discussed in lectures and readings.

### Chapter 7. Question and Answer on Sex and Gender

- Student: "I like your leather jacket." — Bloom: "Thank you very much."
- Student question: Is there similar data on other species?
  - Bloom: Good question — cross-species data should inform any account of human sexual preference origins.
  - Many animals engage in homosexual behavior (sex with members of their own sex).
  - Bloom does not know the rate of **exclusive** homosexual behavior in nonhuman primates (i.e., primates that do not want to have sex with members of the opposite sex).
