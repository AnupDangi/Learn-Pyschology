# PSYC 110 - Lecture 16 - A Person in the World of People: Self and Other, Part I

Source: https://oyc.yale.edu/psychology/psyc-110/lecture-16

## Overview

This lecture opens a two-lecture sequence on social psychology, covering how we think about ourselves, other people, and groups of people. Professor Bloom argues that the most interesting aspect of our evolved minds is our capacity to understand and deal with other people, and he uses our fascination with celebrity lives (e.g., the 2005 Aniston–Pitt separation) as an entry point. The lecture works through individual differences in social connectedness (connectors and the Six Degrees concept), three core findings about the self (the spotlight effect, transparency effect, Lake Wobegon / better-than-average effect, self-serving bias, and cognitive dissonance), the fundamental attribution error and cross-cultural variation in it, and the basic determinants of liking other people (proximity / mere exposure, similarity, and physical attractiveness). Two summary morals are drawn: "enhancement of the self" and "oversimplification of the other."

## Key Concepts & Definitions

- **Connectors (Gladwell):** People who are social "hubs" — they know unusually large numbers of people. Analogous to air-traffic hubs like Chicago O'Hare or Newark.
- **Six degrees of separation:** Idea, derived from Milgram's package study in Omaha, Nebraska, that people are connected to one another via short chains (max six steps in that study).
- **Bacon number:** Number of movie-collaboration steps from an actor/actress to Kevin Bacon.
- **Spotlight effect (Gilovich):** We systematically overestimate how much other people notice us.
- **Transparency effect (Gilovich):** We believe our inner states (e.g., that we are lying) are more visible to others than they actually are.
- **Lake Wobegon effect:** Systematic bias to see oneself as better than average (named after Garrison Keillor's story of a place where all children are above average).
- **Self-serving bias:** Asymmetry in attributions — successes attributed to one's own abilities, failures to external factors.
- **Cognitive dissonance (Festinger):** Unpleasant emotional state caused by inconsistency in one's beliefs/actions; we act to reduce it.
- **Confirmation bias:** Seeking out information that confirms existing beliefs (e.g., right-wing readers reading right-wing magazines).
- **Insufficient justification effect:** When external justification for behavior is small, people adjust beliefs to justify the behavior internally.
- **Attribution (Heider):** A claim about the cause of someone's behavior.
- **Person bias / Fundamental attribution error:** Tendency to over-attribute behavior to personality/disposition and under-attribute it to the situation.
- **Proximity:** Tendency to like people we are physically/spatially close to.
- **Mere exposure effect:** Simply seeing something more often makes it more likable.
- **Similarity:** Tendency to like people similar to ourselves; predicts marriage success.
- **Matthew effect:** Developmental-psychology term (from the Bible) — "to him that hath shall be given" — the advantaged get more advantages.

## Detailed Notes

### Chapter 1. Social Psychology and Connections between People

- Two-lecture sequence on social psychology: how we think about ourselves, other people, and groups.
- Most interesting aspect of evolved minds: capacity to understand and deal with other people.
- People are intensely interested in others' lives; the 2005 Aniston–Pitt separation cited as illustration. Psychologists must "question the natural."
- **Individual difference in social nature** introduced via a test from Malcolm Gladwell's *The Tipping Point*.
- **Stanley Milgram package study:**
  - Gave a package to 160 randomly chosen people in Omaha, Nebraska.
  - Task: get the package to a stockbroker who worked in Boston but lived in Sharon, Massachusetts.
  - Most succeeded; max six steps → origin of "six degrees of separation."
  - About **half** of the successful packages passed through only **two** people — evidence for connector "hubs."
- Analogy: air traffic hubs (Chicago O'Hare, Newark).
- **Gladwell's name test:**
  - 250 names randomly chosen from a Manhattan phone book, covering many ethnicities/national origins.
  - Rule: to "know" someone, they must know you back (Bloom can circle Marcia Johnson, department chair, but not Magic Johnson).
  - Circle a name twice if you know more than one person with that last name.
  - Bloom reports class distribution; **average among a college crowd is 21**; some score over 100.
  - Higher scores correlate with age, longer time in country, being a journalist (academics not so high).
- **Six Degrees of Kevin Bacon:**
  - Average Bacon number across ~250,000 actors on IMDb = **2.8**.
  - John Wayne (180 movies over 60 years) is not well-connected — mostly westerns, repeat collaborators.
  - Meryl Streep also not well-connected — only "good movies," no overlap with Adam Sandler or Jean-Claude Van Damme.
  - **Most connected: Rod Steiger** — *On the Waterfront*, *In the Heat of the Night*, *Carpool*, across dramas, crime serials, thrillers, westerns, horror, sci-fi, musicals.
- Sociological implication: a reason to attend Yale is the **acquaintance network** (not just close friends) — connections matter for jobs, etc., above and beyond intellectual benefits.
- Roadmap for next ~1.5–2 lectures: self → self vs. other → other → groups (e.g., Harvard students, gay people, black people).

### Chapter 2. Aspects of the Self: The Spotlight and Transparency Effects

- Bloom's anecdote: rushed mornings; once had shaving cream in ear (noticed by a homeless man), once had a seriously misaligned shirt at a party — feels humiliating, like everyone notices.
- A *Simpsons* episode illustrates the spotlight effect (clip played in class).
- **Tom Gilovich's T-shirt experiment:**
  - Subjects asked to wear T-shirts with the most embarrassing pictures.
  - Worst T-shirt picture: **Hitler tied with Barry Manilow**. Best: **Martin Luther King Jr. and Jerry Seinfeld**.
  - Subjects estimated how many noticed; experimenters then asked observers.
  - Subjects overestimated by roughly a **factor of two** (e.g., thought 100 noticed; actually ~50).
- **Why this matters — psychology of regret:** When elderly/dying people are asked what they regret, they regret things they **didn't try**, and the reason given is "I would look silly." Knowing the spotlight effect: people don't care as much as you think.
- **Transparency effect demo:** Student volunteer answers three yes/no questions ("Have you been in London? Do you have a younger sibling? Do you like sushi?") and lies on a designated one. Class vote is split between two answers; student reveals she lied on #3.
- Two takeaways:
  1. People are actually quite good liars — most can succeed at this task.
  2. We feel our secrets "bleed out," so we overestimate how much others detect.
- Implication: makes teaching and storytelling hard because we overestimate how much others already know.

### Chapter 3. Aspects of the Self: You're Terrific!

- If self-ratings were unbiased, average percentile self-rating should be 50%; instead, people rate themselves above average as students, teachers, lovers, and **especially drivers**.
- **Lake Wobegon effect** (Garrison Keillor) — systematic bias to view self as better than average.
- Possible explanations:
  - **Nature of feedback:** Healthy environments give more compliments than criticism → inflated self-esteem.
  - **Different criteria for goodness:** People pick the aspect they're good at (parallel parking, carefulness, risk-taking) and judge themselves on that.
  - Above and beyond these, there is a motivation to feel good about yourself.
- **Self-serving bias:**
  - Good midterm grades attributed to being smart, hardworking, brilliant.
  - Poor grades attributed to unfair test, being busy, having better things to do.
  - Same asymmetry in professors (accepted papers = brilliance; rejected = conspiracy by jealous editors).
  - Found in athletes, CEOs, accident reports.
- Three self-related findings so far: you think you're important (spotlight), you think your thoughts bleed out (transparency), and you think you're terrific.

### Chapter 4. Aspects of the Self: Cognitive Dissonance

- **Leon Festinger's cognitive dissonance theory:** Inconsistency in the head produces an unpleasant emotional state ("dissonance"); we act to reduce it.
- **Confirmation bias:** Right-wing people read right-wing magazines; left-wing read left-wing. Bush supporters look for good news about Bush; opponents look for bad.
- **Louisa Egan's M&M study (Yale):**
  - Pretest ensures equal liking of three M&Ms.
  - Subject chooses between two; eats the chosen one (e.g., red).
  - Red is removed; subject now chooses between the remaining two.
  - Subjects strongly prefer the one **not** previously rejected — denigrating the rejected option to justify the choice.
  - Same effect found in undergraduates, four-year-olds, and monkeys.
- **Insufficient justification effect (Festinger classic):**
  - Two groups do a really boring task; one paid **$20** (real money at the time), the other **$1** (insultingly small).
  - When asked later how fun the task was, the **$1 group rated it more fun**.
  - $20 subjects can explain their behavior with the money; $1 subjects rationalize: "It wasn't that bad really, it was kind of interesting, I learnt a lot."
- Dilbert cartoon illustration: dog explains cognitive dissonance — "When people are in an absurd situation their minds rationalize it by inventing a comfortable illusion." The character concludes, "I must work here because I love this work, I love this job."
- ***When Prophesy Fails* (Festinger):** Group convinced world would end; went on a mountain, gave away families, houses, possessions, money. When the world didn't end, instead of saying "I'm such a moron," members said going to the mountain had **delayed** the end of the world — proof they were doing the right things. People who invest heavily are extraordinarily resistant to disconfirmation.
- **Real-world applications:**
  - **Hazing** (fraternities, med schools): humiliation → infer "the group must be really good." (Bloom notes hazing is illegal.)
  - **Political volunteers:** Unpaid volunteers are more committed because they must justify their work to themselves ("I must think very highly of you").
  - **Free therapy tends to be useless** — without paying, you don't ascribe value.
  - **Children's rewards:** Two groups of kids draw pictures; one rewarded (sticker/toy), one not. The **rewarded group later values the activity less** and is less likely to do it without a reward — they explain their behavior by the reward; the unrewarded children infer intrinsic value. Caution against over-rewarding children.
- **Subtlety:** Cognitive dissonance is **not** simple inconsistency. Both $1 and $20 subjects had *some* justification (money). The motivation is to make ourselves look more **moral and rational** than we are — to preserve the belief that we are terrific.
- Summary of three findings about the self:
  1. You believe everyone notices you (you're the hero of your story).
  2. You're terrific — better than average in every way.
  3. What you do makes sense — if it doesn't, you'll distort it so it does.

### Chapter 5. Self and the Other

- **Attribution (Heider):** Claim about cause of someone's behavior.
- Multiple kinds of attributions possible (personality, situation, stress, ulterior motive).
- **Person bias / fundamental attribution error:** We over-attribute behavior to personality/disposition; under-weight situation/context. One of the core ideas in psychology.
- **Examples:**
  - **Professors seem smart** because they only talk about what they know; by semester's end Bloom will have told the class everything he knows.
  - **Quiz show study:** Coin flip assigns one person quiz master, the other contestant. Quiz masters ask idiosyncratic questions ("What was my dog's name?"). Observers rate the quiz master as more intelligent than the contestant — failing to discount the situation.
  - **Job talks:** If a slide projector breaks, observers blame the speaker, not the projector ("the person looked kind of upset … they seemed like a nervous type").
- **Actors confused with their roles (extreme cases):**
  - **Robert Young** played Marcus Welby in *Marcus Welby, M.D.* — compassionate, made house calls, saved lives. Received thousands of letters asking medical advice; later exploited the FAE in a Sanka decaf coffee ad with the famous line "I am not a doctor but I play one on TV."
  - **Sylvester Stallone** viewed as a Vietnam War hero due to Rambo; actually was in a Swiss boarding school teaching girls aged 12–15 during the war.
  - A gay-character role (movie 20 years before the lecture) was turned down by Harrison Ford, Michael Douglas, Richard Gere because they didn't want to be thought gay; eventually taken by Harry Hamlin, "kind of a B-list sort of guy." (IMDb cited.)
  - **Leonard Nimoy / Spock:** Repeatedly treated as an actual Vulcan; wrote *I Am Not Spock*, then later, after his career stalled, wrote *I Am Spock*, conceding to the FAE.
- **Cross-cultural variation:**
  - FAE is not a universal — research compared the United States and India.
  - At about age eight, children in both cultures do **not** commit the FAE.
  - In Western cultures (ideology of personal control of destiny), the error develops.
  - Some Eastern cultures emphasize faith and situational attributions.
  - **Newspaper reports of murders:** US reports emphasize personal characteristics of the accused; Indian reports emphasize the situation that drove the person.
- **Summary morals:**
  - **Enhancement of the self:** when things go badly, blame the situation; when they go well, credit yourself.
  - **Oversimplification of the other:** we are far less forgiving of others. "You do something stupid, that's — you're a stupid person. I do something stupid, it's an off day."

### Chapter 6. How We Think about Other People

- Why we like people — partly covered in Peter Salovey's earlier ("Dean Salovey's") lecture on sexual attractiveness. Beyond honesty, kindness, intelligence, humor, three fundamental processes:

#### Proximity

- We like people physically/spatially close to us and people we spend time with.
- **Manhattan housing project study:** 90% said best friend lives in the same building; 50% said same floor.
- Class poll: best friend at Yale tends to be in same college; many on same floor. Joke: "If you were going to marry somebody from this class, it is the person you are sitting next to?"

#### Mere Exposure Effect

- Simply seeing something more often makes it more likable — becomes comfortable, safe.
- **James Cutting study:** In Intro Psych course, flashed paintings as screen-saver before lectures; said nothing about them. End of semester: subjects rated previously seen images as more likable, even without conscious memory of having seen them.
- **Mirror-image preference:** You prefer photos of yourself that match your mirror image (what you see every day); your best friend prefers the non-mirrored version (what they see every day).

#### Similarity

- We like people similar to us; friends and spouses are highly similar.
- Hard to separate from proximity (Yale friends similar partly because Yale students are similar to each other), but similarity has effects beyond proximity.
- Similarity predicts marriage success; couples become more similar over time (mechanism unclear).

#### Physical Attractiveness

- Attractive people are judged smarter, more competent, more social, nicer.
- Possibility: good-looking people actually become better because of advantages — teachers more responsive, more opportunities, more money, more access.
- **Matthew effect** (developmental psychology, from the Bible): "For unto everyone that hath shall be given and he shall have abundance … but from him that hath not shall be taken away even that which he hath." Rich get richer; poor lose what they have.
- Studies cited:
  - Teachers rate attractive children as smarter and higher-achieving.
  - When an ugly child misbehaves, it's an "ugly soul"; an attractive child is "that little scamp, somebody must have been bothering him."
  - Bloom's neighbor's cute son in Arizona was named **Adonis**.
  - **Mock trials:** Judges give longer prison sentences to ugly people.
  - **Supermarket parking-lot study:** Parents observed to be rougher with ugly kids than good-looking kids — interpreted as the ugly child mattering less to the parent.
- Bloom critiques the parking-lot study: parents of ugly children are likely to be ugly themselves, so the finding may just be that ugly people are more violent.
- Poker quote heard on TV: "They beat me like an ugly stepchild." Bloom: the fate of the ugly stepchild is, in fact, not a very good fate.
- Lecture ends here; social psychology to continue Wednesday.
