# PSYC 110 - Lecture 11 - Evolution, Emotion, and Reason: Emotions, Part I

Source: https://oyc.yale.edu/psychology/psyc-110/lecture-11

## Overview

This lecture, delivered by Professor Paul Bloom, applies the evolutionary perspective introduced previously to the study of emotion. Bloom argues that emotions, which seem natural and self-evident, must be examined scientifically because they emerge from contingent aspects of our biological nature, not from logical necessity. He attacks the "Star Trek" view that emotions are detrimental to rational functioning, showing through cases like Phineas Gage and Damasio's patient Elliot that without emotions people cannot set goals or prioritize. The lecture surveys facial expressions (with a focus on smiles), the non-social emotion of fear, and begins an analysis of social emotions and altruism grounded in evolutionary biology and the "selfish gene" perspective. Bloom previews how kin-directed feelings (cuteness responses, distress calls, attachment) can be explained by genetic relatedness, ending with an introduction to Harlow's "wire mother" and "cloth mother" experiments.

## Key Concepts & Definitions

- **Emotions**: Mechanisms that set goals and establish priorities; without them no action would occur.
- **Phineas Gage**: 1848 construction foreman whose frontal cortex was damaged by an iron rod, illustrating loss of emotional/character function.
- **Duchenne smile**: A real happiness smile, named after a neurophysiologist; involves movement around the eyes; only about one in ten people can fake it.
- **Pan Am smile (greeting smile)**: A smile to communicate hello; mouth moves but eyes do not.
- **Coy smile (appeasement smile)**: A specialized smile of embarrassment/stress involving gaze aversion; appears in other primates and human infants.
- **Emotional contagion**: The phenomenon by which one's face molds in accord with the emotional facial expressions of another.
- **Pre-potent stimuli**: Things (e.g., snakes, spiders) that naturally elicit fear without learning.
- **Altruism**: The biologists' term for kindness/generosity.
- **Selfish gene theory** (Richard Dawkins): Animals are the vehicles through which genes reproduce; "an animal is just the gene's way of creating another animal."
- **Cupboard Theory** (B.F. Skinner): Babies attach to parents because parents provide food (operant conditioning).
- **Bowlby's alternative**: Babies are drawn to mother for comfort and social interaction, plus fear of strangers.
- **Wire mothers / Cloth mothers** (Harlow): Experimental surrogates—wire mothers provide food via nipple; cloth mothers provide warmth and comfort.
- **Baby-face bias**: Tendency to perceive adults with baby-like features as naive, helpless, kind, and warm.

## Detailed Notes

### Chapter 1. The Different Functions of Emotions

- Evolutionary psychology forces scientific scrutiny of things we take for granted.
- William James quotation (cited verbatim in transcript): asks why we smile when pleased, why we can't talk to crowds as to a single friend, why a particular maiden turns our wits upside down; notes the lion finds the lioness made to be loved, the broody hen finds eggs fascinating.
- Bloom notes the passage is (a) "incredibly sexist," (b) "beautifully written" and (c) makes the terrific point that these natural-seeming facts emerge from contingent biological nature.
- Examples of questions psychology must ask:
  - Why does poop smell bad? "To a dung beetle poop smells just fine."
  - Why does chocolate taste good?
  - Why do we love our children?
  - Why do we get angry when slapped (rather than sleepy, nostalgic, or craving cold soup)?
  - Why do we feel good when someone does us a favor?
- Parallel to other sciences: Why do things fall down? Why is our flesh warm? Why does water turn solid when cold?
- **Wrong theory of emotions**: Illustrated by Star Trek — Mr. Spock (half Vulcan) and Data (android lacking emotion chip) are portrayed as competent because they lack emotions.
- **Steven Pinker** in *How the Mind Works* refutes this: Spock must have been driven by motives — intellectual curiosity, solidarity with allies, fear (to flee predators/Klingons), modesty (not walking naked), ambition (getting out of bed), sociability. Without emotions to drive us, "we would do nothing at all."

### Chapter 2. Phineas Gage and the Loss of Emotional Capacity

- **Phineas Gage**, 1848, construction foreman; iron rod (13 pounds) propelled by explosion went under his eye, popped out the top of his head, landed about 100 feet away covered in blood and brains.
- Gage was briefly knocked unconscious, got up, was taken to hospital (stopped at a tavern for cider on the way), had an infection, had surgery.
- Outcome: not blind, not deaf, not aphasic, no paralysis, no retardation — but **lost his character**.
- Description from Damasio's *Descartes' Error*:
  > He used to be a really responsible guy, a family man, very reliable, very trustworthy. But after the accident he was fitful, irreverent, indulging at times in the grossest profanity, manifesting but little deference for his fellows, impatient of restraint or advice, a child in his intellectual capacities and manifestations. He had the animal pleasures of a strong man. His foul language is so debased that women are advised not to stay long in his presence.
- Gage couldn't hold a job, lost his family, ended up in the circus traveling with his iron rod.
- **Elliot** (Damasio's pseudonymous patient): tumor in frontal lobe removed along with part of frontal lobe.
  - Not blind, deaf, retarded, or profane.
  - Lost ability to prioritize and set goals.
  - Quoted description: would read and fully understand significance of work material but suddenly turn to something else and spend an entire afternoon deliberating which principle of categorization to apply to files (date, size, pertinence, etc.).
  - Couldn't keep a job or deal with people.
- Take-home: "Emotions set goals and establish priorities. And without them you wouldn't do anything, you couldn't do anything."
- Themes for the unit:
  - Emotions as mechanisms for goal-setting/prioritization.
  - Universals vs. cultural differences (America vs. Japan, American South vs. North differ in emotional triggers/baselines).
  - Darwin's point: emotions have universal roots shared across humans and many animals.
- Agenda: (1) facial expressions, especially smiling; (2) fear as a nonsocial emotion; (3) feelings toward kin; (4) feelings toward non-kin (next class).

### Chapter 3. Facial Expressions and Smiles in Particular

- **Paul Ekman**: leading scholar of facial expressions; trains police and Secret Service to detect honesty/dishonesty; profiled by Malcolm Gladwell in The New Yorker.
- **Anger face demo**: lower brows and draw them together; tense lower and upper eyelids; stare (eyes can bulge); press lips together with corners straight or down.
- Universal facial-expression databases now exist across genders and national origins (example shown: Japanese women's facial expressions). Subtle cross-cultural differences exist alongside deep universals.
- **Smile demo**: raise corners of lips back and up; raise cheeks; raise lower eyelids.
- Yale experts on smiling:
  - **Angus Trumble**, curator at the British Art Gallery, wrote *A Brief History of the Smile* (smiles in art).
  - **Marianne LaFrance**, Bloom's colleague, studies smiling in adults, children, across cultures, and its social uses.

**Findings about smiles**:

- Smiles are universal — young children smile; even children blind from birth smile appropriately (so smiling is not learned by watching faces).
- Smiles are not uniquely human; nonhuman primates smile.
- Smiles are social signals: people smile when they want to **communicate** happiness, not merely when happy.
  - **Bowler studies**: bowlers who get a strike don't smile after knocking down pins; they turn around to friends and grin.
  - **Olympic gold medal winners**: don't grin on the podium; smile when they face the crowd.
  - Bloom's aside: ask whether people smile during sex — discoverable with a partner or a mirror.
- **Types of smiles**:
  - **Pan Am smile (greeting smile)**: named for the now-defunct airline whose stewardesses produced big fake smiles; communicates "hello."
  - **Duchenne smile (real happiness)**: involves the eyes; only ~1 in 10 people can fake one.
  - Politicians often use Pan Am smiles, especially when attacked/ridiculed.
  - Anecdote: **John Boehner** vs. **Roy Blunt** — a few years ago, Republican John Boehner won the House majority leader race; a photo of Roy Blunt stepping out after losing showed an unhappy expression vs. a real smile.
- Real psychological validity of the distinction:
  - **Ten-month-olds** give a real happiness smile when mother approaches, a greeting smile for strangers.
  - **John Gottman** studies married couples by film clip:
    - Death knell of marriage is **contempt** — not fighting, screaming, or hate.
    - True happiness smile when partner enters lab bodes better than a Pan Am smile.
  - **College yearbook photo studies**: a reliable correlation between current happiness and the type of smile in a yearbook photo from 30 years ago.
- **Coy smile / appeasement smile**: third type; smile of embarrassment or stress; gaze averted; head turned; appears when wanting to be liked under high-stress/risky social situations.
  - Shown in other primates: a rhesus monkey bites her own infant; the infant screams then gives a submissive coy smile.
  - Shown in human infant clip: gaze locked, brows raise, smile starts, smile widens, head turns and averts.

### Chapter 4. Question and Answer on Smiles

- **Q**: Do nonhuman primates give the same kinds of smiles humans do (Pan Am vs. genuine)? **A**: Bloom doesn't know; will check. There is evidence the coy smile appears in nonhuman primates.
- **Q**: Why are some people's smiles "better" than others? **A**: Trivially, some people are better looking; more deeply, some people are better at producing cues of genuine happiness.
- **Emotional contagion demo**: students paired off, designated smiler smiles at partner; difficult not to smile back. Holds for virtually every other emotion — facing someone in rage molds your own face.
- **Q**: Difference between teeth-showing vs. closed-lip smiles? **A**: Probably exists; not a main smile difference but likely shows regional/cross-country variation (e.g., England vs. US). Every culture has Pan Am, happiness, and coy smiles, but variants depend on upbringing/culture.

### Chapter 5. Non-social Emotions: Fear

- Why study fear:
  - It is basic and universal in humans and most nonhuman species.
  - Connects back to behaviorism/classical conditioning.
  - Nonsocial (you can fear a cliff fall, not just a person).
  - Has a distinctive, universally recognizable facial expression — illustrated by photo of the detective standing as **Jack Ruby assassinated Lee Harvey Oswald** (mixture of fear and anger).
- **What we are afraid of** (universal fear elicitors): spiders, snakes, heights, storms, large animals, darkness, blood, strangers, humiliation, deep water, leaving home alone.
- **What we are NOT very afraid of**: guns, cars, electrical outlets.
- Explanation: these elicitors were dangerous in our ancestral environment; chimpanzees develop phobias of similar things (spiders, snakes).
- **Urban Chicago study**: inner-city children asked what they fear most — top fears were snakes and spiders, even though many had never seen them outside a zoo. "These are natural fears."
- **Judy DeLoache** (University of Virginia): studies babies' fears of spiders and snakes using indirect methods (cannot ethically attempt to construct phobias). Finds these are **pre-potent stimuli**.

### Chapter 6. Social Emotions and Altruism

- Two categories of social emotions: those toward **kin** (genetic relatives) and those toward **non-kin** (people you interact with but aren't related to).
- Focus on emotions generating kind/altruistic behavior.
- "Altruism" = biologists' term for kindness/generosity.
- **Wrong old view**: animals are merely survival machines; under that view altruism is mysterious.
- **Simple counterexample**: imagine Gene A (animal cares for offspring) vs. Gene B (animal cares only for itself). Gene A wins out across generations. The animal that eats its offspring is a "biological dead end." **What matters is reproduction, not survival per se.**
- **Selfish gene perspective** (Richard Dawkins): animals are the vehicles through which genes reproduce.
  - **Cold virus**: you sneeze not just to expel germs; the cold virus uses your body as a tool to replicate. "A person is just a germ's way of making other germs."
  - **Toxoplasmosis**: parasite in rats, passed when cats eat rats and excrete it; rewires rat brain to be less afraid of cats — an adaptive strategy.
  - Hypothetical maximally powerful virus would skip the respiratory system and take over the brain to make people seek sex and kiss on the mouth; some evidence that **syphilis** arouses libido for this reason.
  - A virus that made hosts run out and bite other animals would be called **rabies**.
- **Implication for altruism**: selfish genes lead to altruistic animals because, at the gene level, there is no hard and fast distinction between your body and someone else's.
- **Haldane**: Asked if he'd lay down his life for his brother — "No, but I would gladly give my life for three brothers or five nephews or nine first cousins."
  - Joke that captures the ultimate causation: genes propagate at higher rate if a body sacrifices itself for sufficient genetic relatives. Three brothers = 50% more genes replicated.
  - Selfish gene theory, often seen as cold-blooded, provides a scientific basis for **real, genuine altruism**.
- **Lion takeover example**: New male lion taking over a pride kills remaining cubs; lionesses undergo spontaneous abortions.
  - Cubs are genetic competition; only by removing them can the new male reproduce.
  - Females' spontaneous abortion is adaptive: existing cubs would not survive anyway, so best strategy is to start anew.

**Mechanisms of kin care (especially in birds and mammals)**:

- Birds and mammals invest in **quality, not quantity** (unlike fish/reptiles); few offspring, big psychological investment.
- Offspring are vulnerable for long periods and need resources.

**Distress calls** (baby cry example played):

- Small animals chirp, mew, bleat, cry.
- "Delicate high-wire act" — annoying enough to elicit help but not so annoying that caretakers kill the infant.
- Cry is annoying because our brains are wired to respond, not because of volume or pitch.

**Cuteness**:

- Babies are cute because of how our brains are wired, not because of "metaphysical" cuteness; Martians wouldn't say "Oh, cute baby."
- Cute features (mammalian universal): big protruding foreheads, upturned little nose, chubby cheeks, big eyes.
- **Stephen Jay Gould** essay: evolution of **Mickey Mouse** — started as an ugly little rodent, became cuter as artists converged on baby-like features.
- **Baby-face bias**: same studies in US and Asia; baby-faced adults (example: **Leonardo DiCaprio**) judged as naive, helpless, kind, warm. In mock trials, baby-faced individuals more likely to be found innocent than non-baby-faced (example: **Ben Affleck**).
- Class poll (men only) on attractiveness between baby-faced vs. testosterone-faced; women's preferences are more complicated and depend on menstrual cycle phase (to be discussed in sex lecture).

**Babies' responses to caregivers**:

- **Cupboard Theory** (B.F. Skinner): attachment is operantly conditioned via food (breast milk or bottle).
- **Bowlby**: babies seek mother for comfort and social interaction, plus fear of strangers.
- **Harlow's experiments** with nonhuman primates:
  - **Wire mothers**: built with a nipple providing food.
  - **Cloth mothers**: provide no food but provide warmth and comfort.
  - Aside about Yale psychology department: one professor was nicknamed "the cloth mother" (supportive, not warm); another, productive but loveless, was called "the wire mother."
- Bloom warns the third Harlow film is an example of why this research is not currently done but illustrates an important scientific point.
- Lecture ends with the promise to wrap up Harlow and move to altruism toward non-kin next class.
