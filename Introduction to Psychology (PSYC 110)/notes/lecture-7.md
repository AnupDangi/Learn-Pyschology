# PSYC 110 - Lecture 7 - Conscious of the Present; Conscious of the Past: Language (cont.); Vision and Memory

Source: https://oyc.yale.edu/psychology/psyc-110/lecture-7

## Overview

This lecture wraps up the topic of language and transitions into perception, attention, and memory. Bloom argues that nonhuman animals possess communication systems but not language in the technical sense (phonology, morphology, syntax, recursion), and that attempts to teach apes human languages have largely failed and may be misguided. He then introduces perception as a hard problem: the retina is a 2D surface from which the mind must infer a 3D world, and it does so through unconscious assumptions and Gestalt cues, illustrated by color/brightness, object segmentation, and depth illusions. Finally, he frames attention and memory together, demonstrating that attention is a narrow spotlight (change blindness, the gorilla demo, the Stroop effect) and that memory comes in many kinds (sensory, short-term/working, long-term; implicit/explicit; semantic/episodic) with encoding, storage, and retrieval stages. The overarching moral: "We are often wrong about our experiences, both of the present and of right now."

## Key Concepts & Definitions

- **Language (technical sense)**: a communication system with phonology, morphology, syntax, combinatorial systems including recursion, and arbitrary names.
- **Animal communication categories**: (1) a finite list of calls (e.g., vervet monkey warnings for snake vs. leopard), (2) a continuous analog signal (e.g., bee dance where intensity corresponds to richness of the food source), (3) random variation on a theme (e.g., birdsong).
- **Linguistic relativity / Sapir-Whorf hypothesis**: the claim that the language you know affects how you think.
- **Reading**: a cultural invention, not an instinct; acquired with tremendous difficulty over many years; distinguished by Darwin from things that come naturally.
- **Perception (problem)**: getting from a 2D array of neural firings on the retina to objects, people, actions, and events in a 3D world; "from a mathematical point of view, impossible" without unconscious assumptions.
- **Gestalt principles** (cues to object segmentation): proximity, similarity, closure, good continuation, common movement, good form.
- **Kanizsa Triangle / Kanizsa Square**: illusory shapes induced by incomplete circles ("Pacmen") or regularities of form.
- **Depth cues**: binocular disparity, interposition, relative size, texture gradient, linear perspective.
- **Binocular disparity**: the only depth cue that involves two eyes; the images differ more for close objects and become almost identical for far ones.
- **Mueller-Lyer illusion**: arrow-end lines that appear different lengths but are equal.
- **Ponzo illusion**: two converging lines (like train tracks) make a horizontal line at the top appear longer than an equal one at the bottom.
- **Shepard tables**: two tables that look very different in proportion but are the same shape and size (developed by Roger Shepard).
- **Sensory memory**: a residue in your senses (e.g., afterimage of lightning; echoic memory for sounds for a few seconds).
- **Short-term memory / working memory**: spans for a few minutes.
- **Long-term memory**: the store you walk around with (e.g., knowing Elvis, your name, where you live).
- **Implicit vs. explicit memory**: explicit = conscious access (what you had for dinner); implicit = unconscious (word meanings, how to walk, how to ride a bicycle).
- **Semantic vs. episodic memory**: semantic = facts (capital of Canada, "Yale is in New Haven"); episodic = autobiography (you went on vacation away from New Haven last week).
- **Encoding / storage / retrieval**: getting the memory in, holding it, getting it out.
- **Recall vs. recognition**: recall = pulling it out; recognition = recognizing a match to something in the past.
- **Attention**: "a flashlight, a spotlight on experience that willingly zooms in on something and makes it memorable."
- **Stroop effect**: expert reading subverts the task of naming font color because attention to word meaning cannot be blocked.
- **Change blindness**: a narrow focus of attention causes obliviousness to huge changes in the environment.
- **Two types of amnesia**: (1) losing memory of the past (Matt Damon amnesia); (2) losing the ability to form new memories.

## Detailed Notes

### Chapter 1. Non-human Communication [00:00:00]

- Two follow-ups from Monday's lecture:
  - **Preference for own language in development**: studies of newborns show that "pretty much the moment they pop out they favor their own language over other languages," suggesting they listen in utero to the rhythms of the language.
  - **"Let him have it!" court case**: the defendant was tried and found guilty of murder, executed in 1957, then pardoned in 1988. A movie was made about it.
- Roadmap recap: covered universals of language, phonology, morphology, syntax, arbitrary symbols, combinatorial systems including recursion, and development from babies (good at language) to adults (whose "language capacities are dead").
- **Animals and language**:
  - Not controversial that animals have communication systems; if "language" = communication, then dogs, bees, monkeys have it.
  - In the technical/narrow sense, the answer is almost certainly "no."
- **Three categories of animal communication systems**:
  1. Finite list of calls — vervet monkeys have warnings like "attack from a snake" / "attack from a leopard."
  2. Continuous analog signal — bee dance: intensity corresponds to the richness of the food source (no syntactic structure).
  3. Random variation on a theme — birdsong.
- None show phonology, morphology, syntax, combinatorial systems, or arbitrary names.
- **Trained primates (Kanzi, Nim Chimpsky, etc.)**: this is controversial. Bloom thinks Gray's textbook is "a little bit too credulous."
  - Even if they learn words, they learn very few, over years of extensive training.
  - A normally developing child can learn "a word in a day or a word in an hour."
  - Utterances have limited order and lack recursion (not controversial).
  - Trained chimpanzee utterances are extremely repetitive. Example sample: "Nim eat, Nim eat. Drink, eat, me Nim. Me gum, me gum. Tickle me, Nim play. Me eat, me eat. Me banana, you banana, me you give. Banana me, me eat. Give orange, me give, eat orange, me eat orange."
  - **Lila Gleitman**: "if any normally developing child spoke like this, his parents would rush him screaming to a neurologist."
- **Why expect chimps to learn human language at all?**
  - "Chimps are smart" — but smartness isn't enough (smart children with language deficits exist).
  - "Chimps are our nearest evolutionary relatives" — but we split a long time ago; five million years either way is more than enough for language to evolve.
- Bloom's personal view: attempts to teach apes ASL are "misguided" — "as if a team of monkeys kidnapped a human child and tried to train him how to hoot like a monkey." Studying animal communication systems in the wild (vervet cries, bee dance) using the linguistics program would be more interesting.

### Chapter 2. The Relationship between Language and Thought [00:09:16]

- Intro Psych skips topics that fuller courses cover:
  - Language in the brain.
  - Language disorders: aphasias, specific language impairment, dyslexia.
  - Language perception and production (understanding and producing words in a fraction of a second).
  - Reading: a cultural invention, not universal; Darwin distinguished language as instinct from reading; reading "intersects with language" — moving from speech to writing.
  - Bilingualism and multilingualism.
- **Language and thought** — Bloom once taught a full seminar on this. Two general questions:
  1. **Is language necessary for abstract thought?** Look at creatures without language (babies, chimpanzees) and see how smart they are.
  2. **Does the structural properties of language affect the way you think?** = linguistic relativity / Sapir-Whorf hypothesis. Research compares speakers of e.g. English vs. Korean.
- The Gray textbook and selections from The Norton Anthology address this; it is the basis of a reading response.

### Chapter 3. Question and Answer on Language [00:12:39]

- **Why do some people learn languages more easily?**
  - Applies to both first and second language learning.
  - Einstein anecdote (didn't speak till four, then "The soup is too hot," "Well, up to now everything's been fine") — "It's not a true story."
  - Slight advantage for females, but small (need ~100 people to see it statistically).
  - Big genetic factor: if your parents learned languages quickly, you're more likely to.
  - Brain, cognitive, and social bases largely an open question.
- **Bilingual parents**: more the norm worldwide than the US single-language situation. Children learn both languages, distinguishing them by sound system and rhythms, and don't typically confuse them.
- **Hemispheric specialization**: if right-handed, language is probably in the left side. For left-handers, it varies — left side, right side, or diffuse. Why people are right- or left-handed is also an open question.
- **Does learning more than one language slow each down?** Common sense says yes (finite resources), but data say no — bilingual children reach milestones in each language at the same time as monolinguals. No cognitive deficit either (cited the Quebec debate). "There appears to be, as far as we know, no down side to learning many languages when you're young."

### Chapter 4. Introduction to the Complexity of Perception and Expectation [00:18:06]

- Perception, attention, and memory treated as one connected story: you perceive a scene, hold it in memory, and a week later recall it.
- **Claims to be argued**:
  - Perception is hard; successful perception involves educated and unconscious guesses.
  - We attend to some things and miss a surprising amount.
  - There are many types of memory; the key is organization and understanding; some memories cannot be trusted.
- **9/11 memory study**: psychologists on Sept 12 asked people where they were when they heard the news; later checks (a year, two years later) often showed the recalled story was wrong.
- **Big moral**: "We are often wrong about our experiences, both of the present and of right now."
- **Marvin Minsky / MIT story**: A.I. guru; thought building a robot that could see and recognize objects would "take a graduate student a whole summer." Computer vision has been a "profound failure" — no machine recognizes objects at the level of "a really dumb one-year-old."
- **Why perception is hard**:
  - The "little man in the head watching a TV monitor" model just pushes the question back.
  - The Terminator's gauges/numbers view doesn't solve it either (he still has to "read" them).
  - Real picture: retina = nerve cells firing or not firing; you must infer the world from an array of firings.
  - Retina is 2D; the world is 3D. Mathematically, for any 2D image there is an indefinite number of 3D images that correspond to it (e.g., a square shape could be a square head-on or a tilted square).
- The mind has **unconscious assumptions** that yield educated guesses.
- Three examples to be covered: **color** (conflated with brightness), **objects**, **depth**.

#### Color / brightness

- Coal vs. snowball: not simply read off from retina. Object color depends on illumination as well.
- As Bloom walks across the stage into shadow and light, no one screams that he is changing colors — we automatically factor out the change in illumination.
- **Two-blocks demo**: a block under a table appears lighter than one out in the open, but they are the same. Removing the surrounding cues (covering the background) reveals they are identical.
- Logic: "We know shadows make surfaces darker… wired up in our brains." A surface seen as in shadow is assumed lighter than it looks, and seen as lighter.

#### Objects

- A scene is intuitively segmented into man, house, birds, trees. Programming a computer to do this is "hugely difficult."
- **Gestalt cues**:
  - **Proximity**: things close together belong together.
  - **Similarity**: same-texture items group as one object.
  - **Closure**: a closed square reads as one object overlapping a circle.
  - **Good continuation**: overlapping lines AB and CD are seen as A-to-B and C-to-D, not A-to-C and D-to-B.
  - **Common movement**: things moving together are one object.
  - **Good form**: two perpendicular overlapping rectangles read as a plus sign; non-perpendicular ones read as two things lying on each other.
- **Illusions**: Kanizsa Triangle (illusory triangle from three Pacmen — cover the Pacmen and the triangle disappears); Kanizsa Square (no square there; "very Matrix").

#### Depth

- Looking at a flat picture, you still perceive depth (man in front of house; podium with Bloom walking behind it). If neurologically abnormal, you'd see a "strange creature" merging Bloom and the podium.
- **Depth cues**:
  - **Binocular disparity**: only depth cue using both eyes; close objects yield very different left/right images, far objects yield nearly identical images.
  - **Interposition**: object that blocks another is in front (Bloom walking behind a podium; the man blocking part of the house).
  - **Relative size**: judgments about size dictate judgments about distance. If Bloom looked 50 feet tall, you'd assume he's farther. Empire State Building / tower example.
  - **Texture gradient**: textures get smaller with distance; receding dots cue receding depth.
  - **Linear perspective**: parallel lines converge with distance.
- **Mueller-Lyer illusion**: lines with inward vs. outward arrowheads look different lengths but are equal.
- **Ponzo illusion**: two converging "train track" lines crossed by horizontal segments — the upper line looks longer; it isn't.
- Explanation: arrow/track cues suggest one line is farther away; for equal retinal size the "farther" one must be physically bigger, so the brain sees it bigger.
- **Shepard tables** (Roger Shepard): two tables — one looks longer/leaner, one thicker — but the rectangles are identical. Bloom spent ~seven hours on the computer demo proving it; a paper cut to one shape fits the other perfectly. Same explanation: depth cues and the visual system's correction for depth.

### Chapter 5. Linking Attention and Memory [00:39:15]

- Memory fascinates especially when it goes wrong (amnesia).
- **Volunteer demo**: a "pretend amnesia" patient says "I don't know." Bloom: wrong answer — you still remember English. A second volunteer "still maintains bowel and bladder control so he hasn't forgotten everything."
- **Memory is broad**: autobiographical memory (the movie kind, "Where am I?") but also language, standing, chewing, swallowing — all learned and shaped by experience.
- **Two types of amnesia**:
  1. Losing memory of the past (Matt Damon amnesia).
  2. Losing the ability to form new memories.
- **Choir director case** (film): suffered viral encephalitis; brain damage destroyed most of his temporal lobes, hippocampus, and a lot of his left frontal lobe. Retains speech, not intellectually impaired, but can't form new memories — lives in a perpetual "now," "continually reborn at every moment."
- **Basic distinctions in memory**:
  - **Sensory memory** — residue in senses (lightning afterimage; echoic memory of last few seconds of speech, as when "you're not listening" but can replay).
  - **Short-term / working memory** — spans for a few minutes.
  - **Long-term memory** — knowing Elvis, your name, your address.
  - **Implicit vs. explicit** — conscious recollection vs. unconscious know-how (word meaning "had," walking, riding a bicycle).
  - **Semantic vs. episodic** — facts (capital of Canada; Yale in New Haven) vs. autobiography (your vacation last week).
  - **Encoding, storage, retrieval** — getting it in, holding it, getting it out.
  - **Recall vs. recognition** — pulling out vs. matching. ("What color tie I had on two days ago?" — impossible to recall; "purple or orange?" much easier.)
- **Stage theory**: sensory → short-term → long-term.
- **Attention as gatekeeper**: not everything gets into memory (the seat pressing your butt, your neighbor's smell). Attention "is a flashlight, a spotlight on experience."

#### Attention demos

- **Automatic visual search**: finding the one green X in black Xs, or the one O among Xs, is effortless and fast. Finding the red O among black Os and red Xs is harder.
- **Stroop effect**:
  - Naming colors of colored rectangles — easy.
  - Naming colors of color-words written in matching color — easy.
  - Naming font color of color-words written in mismatching color — slow and error-prone.
  - "Being an expert reader… your attention to what the words meant, subverted your desire to do the task. You couldn't make that go away even if you wanted to."
- **The gorilla demo** (basketball passing): white vs. black T-shirt teams pass balls; subjects count white-team passes. A gorilla-suited person walks through. "About 50% of people when counting, who have never seen this before don't notice anything." This shows a "very small window of attention."
- **Change blindness demos**:
  - Conversation movie where camera cuts change the scarf, plate color, food, etc. — most people miss the changes.
  - Two pictures flickering back and forth — subjects must clap when they see the difference; many never do.
  - Work by **Dan Simons**.
- **Cornell campus experiment (Dan Simons)**: a confederate stops a passerby asking for directions; two construction workers carry a door between them; while passing, the original confederate is swapped for a different person. Subjects typically don't notice. They do notice sex changes ("Didn't you used to be a woman?") and race changes; most other changes go unnoticed.
- **Consent form experiment** (Brian Scholl or Dan Simons): a subject signs a human-subjects form; the experimenter goes "down here" and a different person pops up. People don't notice.
- "What's weird is we don't see — we don't think we are. We think we see the world as it is and we don't know — notice that when we're attending to something; everything else gets blanked out."

### Chapter 6. Question and Answer on Attention and Memory [00:58:04]

- **Why do practiced things become automatic and involuntary?** Bloom doesn't know. Reading, listening to speech, and seeing all become hard to shut off ("You could put your fingers in [your ears] but you can't shut off your [hearing]"). Over-practice → involuntary.
- **Did the choir director remember things before his accident?** Yes — he had some amnesia of events before the accident but knew his name and other things about his life.
