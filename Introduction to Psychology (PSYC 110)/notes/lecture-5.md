# PSYC 110 - Lecture 5 - What Is It Like to Be a Baby: The Development of Thought

Source: https://oyc.yale.edu/psychology/psyc-110/lecture-5

## Overview

This lecture, delivered by Professor Paul Bloom, surveys the development of thought from infancy through childhood. It begins with Jean Piaget's foundational stage theory and its mechanisms (assimilation and accommodation), then moves to the modern science of infant cognition, which uses clever non-verbal methods (looking-time, habituation, surprise, sucking) to test what babies actually know. Bloom argues that, contrary to Piaget's claims, babies appear to possess significant knowledge of the physical world (e.g., object permanence, rudimentary arithmetic) and possibly the social world (helping/hindering, mimicry) from very early on. The lecture also explores explanations of developmental change — neural maturation, inhibition problems, and learning — and considers the modular view of cognition (Chomsky, Fodor), illustrated by autism as a disorder selectively affecting social understanding. It closes with the broader idea that studying development can illuminate questions about adult cognition.

## Key Concepts & Definitions

- **Genetic epistemology**: Piaget's discipline — the origins of knowledge; he studied individual children to learn about the development of knowledge more generally.
- **"Ontogeny recapitulates phylogeny"**: The idea that the development of an individual mimics or repeats the development of the species. Bloom notes it is "entirely not true, but it's a beautiful phrase," and Piaget was committed to it.
- **Assimilation**: "The act of expanding the range of things that you respond to." Example: a baby used to sucking on a breast may come to suck on a bottle or a rattle.
- **Accommodation**: "Changing how you do it." Example: a baby forms his mouth differently depending on what he is sucking on. More psychologically, changing one's system of knowledge itself.
- **Sensorimotor stage / period**: Piaget's first stage. The child is "purely a physical creature" with no real understanding of the external world — no past, no future, no stability, no differentiation. The child touches and sees but doesn't yet reason. Object permanence is gradually acquired during this stage.
- **Object permanence**: The understanding that things exist when you no longer see them. Piaget claimed children before six months lack it — when an object is hidden, "it's like it's gone."
- **A-not-B task** (Peter Gray's "changing hiding places" problem): An object is repeatedly hidden at location A (a cup) and the nine-month-old reaches for it. When moved to a new location B in plain view, the child still reaches at A.
- **Preoperational stage**: The capacity to represent the world internally emerges, but with striking limits — egocentrism and failure of conservation.
- **Egocentrism** (Piaget's technical sense, not "selfish"): Children "literally can't understand that others can see the world differently from them."
- **Three mountains task**: Demonstration of egocentrism. A child can draw three mountains from his own viewpoint but finds it "extraordinarily difficult" to draw them as they appear from the other side.
- **Conservation**: The notion that "there's ways to transform things such that some aspects of them change but others remain the same" (e.g., pouring water into a different-shaped glass, spreading out pennies). Preoperational children fail to recognize this.
- **Concrete operations (age 7–12)**: Children can solve conservation but are still limited in abstract reasoning (e.g., mathematical infinity, logical entailment are beyond them).
- **Abstract / scientific reasoning stage** (around age 12): Capacity for abstract and scientific reasoning emerges.
- **Habituation**: "A fancy word for boredom." Showing a baby something repeatedly until the baby stops attending; then a new stimulus is presented to see if the baby perks up.
- **Surprise method**: Showing babies something that shouldn't happen; longer looking is taken as evidence the baby also finds it surprising. "Scientists do magic tricks to explore this very thing."
- **Neural maturation**: Most neurons exist already in utero; development largely involves pruning excess neurons, growth of synaptic connections (peaking around age 2), and myelination (continuing through teenage years, especially in frontal lobes).
- **Inhibition**: Capacity to stop a prepotent response; tied to the frontal lobes, not fully active in babies. Offered as an alternative explanation for A-not-B errors.
- **Modules** (Chomsky, Jerry Fodor): Separate pre-wired systems for reasoning, each with built-in knowledge and its own learning pattern. Opposed to a single across-the-board developmental story.
- **Autism**: A disorder striking about one in a thousand people, mostly boys; characterized by lack of social connectedness, problems with language, problems dealing with people — what Simon Baron-Cohen calls "mind blindness." Autistic individuals show no impairments on the physical world, math, or spatial skills.
- **False-belief task**: A test of understanding others' minds. A doll named Maxie hides a ball; a second doll moves it; the child is asked where Maxie will look. The correct answer is the original (cupboard) location because Maxie has a false belief. Three-year-olds fail; four- and five-year-olds pass; autistic individuals often fail even when otherwise high-functioning.
- **"Extreme male brain" theory** (Baron-Cohen): Social abilities are "more sequestered" in females; autistic individuals have extreme male brains. "To be a man is to suffer from a very mild form of autism."
- **Autistic savants**: A very small minority of autistic individuals who have extraordinary mathematical, artistic, or musical abilities (e.g., the Rain Man character).

## Detailed Notes

### Chapter 1. Jean Piaget, Stage Theory and Its Limits

- Piaget's influence shapes most current methods, theories, and ideas in cognitive development.
- Piaget viewed children as "active thinkers… little scientists" who construct schemas — "miniature theories of the world."
- His ambition was not children per se but **genetic epistemology** — the origins of knowledge. He believed studying the individual child would reveal the development of knowledge more generally.
- He endorsed the phrase "**Ontogeny recapitulates phylogeny**" — development of an individual mimics development of the species. Bloom: "It's entirely not true, but it's a beautiful phrase."

**Two mechanisms of learning:**
- **Assimilation** — expanding the range of things you respond to (baby sucks on breast → bottle → rattle).
- **Accommodation** — changing how you respond (baby shapes mouth differently for different objects). Psychologically: changing your system of knowledge.

**Method**: ask children to solve problems and ask them questions. Different answers at different ages led to the Stage Theory.

**Stages:**

| Stage | Approx. Age | Key Features |
|---|---|---|
| Sensorimotor | Before ~2 | Purely physical; no past/future/stability; gradually acquires object permanence |
| Preoperational | ~2 to 7 | Internal representation present but limited by egocentrism and failure of conservation |
| Concrete operations | 7 to 12 | Solves conservation, but limited in abstract reasoning (infinity, logical entailment) |
| Abstract / scientific | ~12+ | Abstract and scientific reasoning |

**Object permanence demonstrations:**
- Adult example: ducking behind the lectern doesn't make the audience think he's gone.
- Before six months, hide a rattle and "it's like it's gone." Babies surprised by peek-a-boo because they think you actually disappear.
- **A-not-B task** ("changing hiding places," Peter Gray's name): Nine-month-old reaches at A repeatedly; when the object is moved to B in plain view, the child still reaches at A. Piaget took this as evidence babies don't fully understand objects.

**Preoperational limits:**
- **Egocentrism** — Three mountains task: child can draw the mountains from his own side but fails when asked to draw them from the other side.
- **Conservation failures**:
  - Two equal rows of checkers; one is spread out — child says the spread row has more.
  - Pouring water into a taller/shallower glass — child says the amount changed.
  - Bloom: "Any of you who have access to a four- or five-year-old… Do not take one without permission… you can do this yourself."

**Why Piaget fared better than Freud or Skinner:**
- Interesting and falsifiable claims.
- Rich theoretical framework with many books and articles.
- "Really striking findings" — before Piaget, no one noticed conservation failures or babies' object-tracking problems.

**Limitations:**
- *Theoretical*: Does he really explain how a child moves from concrete to abstract thought, or from lacking to having object permanence?
- *Methodological*: Heavy reliance on question and answer; children aren't good with language, especially when young, which may underestimate what they know.
- *Factual*: Methodological limits may have led Piaget to systematically underestimate infant/child knowledge.

**Aside on methodology**: "Often 90% of the game is discovering a clever method through which to test your hypotheses." Bloom cites Henry Gleitman at University of Pennsylvania, who built a tickle machine — "this box with these two giant hands that went 'r-r-r-r.'" It failed because people convulsed in laughter before reaching it.

### Chapter 2. The Modern Science of Infant Cognition

- Long-standing consensus (captured by an Onion headline): "babies are stupid… don't know much about the world."
- Modern research suggests "babies might be smarter than you think" — but discovering this requires clever methods.

**Methods (babies can't talk or move skillfully):**
- **Brain waves** (with EEG-style electrodes — Bloom jokes about a child "crushed by the weights of the electrodes").
- **Pacifier sucking**: machines deliver music or language contingent on sucking; sucking rate measures preference.
- **Looking times** (most influential): pioneered by **Elizabeth Spelke**, a developmental psychologist.

**Two looking-time paradigms:**
- **Preference**: Place two images side by side (e.g., dog vs. cat; pretty vs. ugly face) and see which the baby looks at more.
- **Habituation and surprise**: Show the same stimulus repeatedly until the baby is bored, then show a different one. Longer looking = perceived difference. Used, e.g., for color vision: green, green, green, then red.
- **Surprise**: Show something that shouldn't happen; longer looking suggests the baby finds it surprising. "Scientists do magic tricks to explore this very thing."

**Studies revisiting object permanence:**
- **Spelke and Baillargeon — bar/block study**: Babies see a bar moving behind a block. Adults assume a complete bar in the middle. When the screen drops, babies look longer at a broken bar than a complete one — i.e., they expected a whole bar.
- **Rene Baillargeon — screen and block**: A block on a stage is occluded by a rising screen. In one condition the screen stops where the block should be; in the other (via a trap door) the screen rotates through. Babies look longer at the impossible event.
- **Karen Wynn's lab at Yale — addition/subtraction**: With real objects and animated versions. Babies expect 2 − 1 = 1; when shown 2 − 1 = 2, 3, or 0, they look longer. Even six-month-olds are sensitive to "rudimentary facts of arithmetic" and to object continuity out of sight.

**Things babies don't know:**
- Show a block on a surface and another block floating in midair. Six-month-olds find floating in midair surprising (violates gravity).
- A block stuck protruding off the edge: six-month-olds aren't surprised; even 12-month-olds aren't surprised; adults recognize it as an unstable configuration that should fall.
- "Although some things are built in, some things develop."

**Explanations of development:**
1. **Neural maturation**: Most neurons exist already in utero. Development largely involves **pruning** (getting rid of excess neurons), **synaptic growth** (peaks at about two years), and **myelination** (continues through development — even teenagers' frontal lobes aren't fully myelinated; frontal lobes underlie restraint and willpower).
2. **Inhibition problems**: A-not-B may reflect not lack of knowledge but inability to stop a prepotent response. **Adele Diamond**'s evidence: kids reach for A but *look* at B, "as if they know it's there but they can't stop themselves from reaching." Bloom illustrates with a Simpsons clip.
3. **Learning**: Some things must be learned — true across social, economic, political, and physical domains.
   - **Michael McCloskey** study with college students: A ball is shot through a curved transparent tube; subjects asked whether it exits in a straight line (A) or continues to curve (B). At Johns Hopkins, 50/50; at Yale, students get it right. With *water* squirted through the tube, nobody chooses B — everyone knows water goes straight. "When you have experience that helps you out, but in absence of experience you're kind of lost."

### Chapter 3. Babies in the Social World

- Newborns have social preferences. Newborn research is hard "because of the consent procedure and everything, so most of this work is done in France." Newborns prefer face-like configurations over scrambled ones. Humans and other primates favor faces.
- Babies are natural mimics. **Andrew Meltzoff**: stick out your tongue at a newborn, and the newborn more often than not sticks its tongue back out — "some sort of social connection from one person to another."
- The nature of face preference and mimicry is debated.

**Helping/hindering study (Bloom, Valerie Kuhlmeier, Karen Wynn):**
- Tested nine-month-olds and twelve-month-olds with movies of a ball being helped or hindered by other characters.
- Controls: shapes, positions varied across versions.
- Result: Babies look statistically longer when the ball approaches the *hinderer* than the *helper*, suggesting a social interpretation of the events.

**Follow-up (Kiley Hamlin, graduate student):**
- 3-D scenes; baby is offered the characters to reach for.
- "The person giving a choice is **blind** to the study" — she didn't know what the baby had seen, to avoid intentional or unintentional bias.
- Suggests some social understanding may be present from very early.
- Bloom notes the evidence is "tentative, very controversial."

**Developmental puzzle**: In some respects, young children are "very clueless" about people. Two film clips were shown demonstrating young children's ignorance of others' minds.

### Chapter 4. Question and Answer on Learning and Development

- **Q: ages of children in failure/success videos?** Bloom: Typically three-year-olds and young four-year-olds fail; around age four to five children make the transition.
- **Q: has discriminant conditioning been used with babies?** A graduate student (Koleen) answers: it's "not as effective as other methods." Operant conditioning is hard with babies because they don't behave systematically; looking-time measures are more subtle.
- **Q: why did the kids choose the rocket ship sticker over the Raphael one?** Bloom: not the experiment's focus; researchers assumed boys' sticker preferences to get the study off the ground. Sex differences in preferences would be revisited later.

### Chapter 5. Review of Studies Presented in Class; Autism

**Two interpretations of children's failures on deception/false-belief tasks:**
- The psychologist in the video argued children "need to know more about minds" — they don't understand intent to deceive.
- **Alternative**: They have the right knowledge but suffer from **inhibition** problems.

**Examples:**
- **Big shoes / little shoes deception task**: The child, asked who the mother would think stole the food, says the real thief. Possible "pull towards the right answer" overwhelms the deception logic.
- **Mean monkey / rocket ship task**: The boy keeps pointing to the rocket ship he wants, even though the mean monkey then takes it. Could be Homer-Simpson-like failure of inhibition — "the extra work it takes to lie was beyond him." Supporting this, even adults are slower and more error-prone on tasks involving lying and deception.

**Relationship between different domains of development:**
- Piaget (and Freud) believed in across-the-board general changes.
- **Alternative: modularity** (Noam Chomsky, Jerry Fodor) — separate pre-wired systems with built-in knowledge and distinct learning patterns.
- Reason to take modularity seriously: developmental disorders affecting one system but not others.

**Autism:**
- Strikes about 1 in 1,000 people, mostly boys.
- Symptoms: lack of social connectedness, language problems, problems with people, "mind blindness" (**Simon Baron-Cohen**).
- No impairments on physical, mathematical, or spatial tasks necessarily.
- Many autistic children have no language; even high-functioning ones suffer severe social impairment.
- Bloom personally entered psychology working with autistic children.

**Baron-Cohen — Charlie and chocolates task**: Four candies are arranged; "Charlie" is in the middle looking toward one. Asked "Which chocolate will Charlie take?" most three- and four-year-olds answer correctly (the one he is looking at). Autistic children often "just shrug, say, 'How could I know?'" — they don't appreciate that desires track gaze.

**False-belief task (Maxie):**
- Maxie puts a ball in the cupboard, leaves. A second doll moves the ball under the bed. Maxie returns.
- Question: Where will Maxie look?
- Correct: the cupboard (Maxie has a false belief).
- Three- and two-year-olds fail. Four- and five-year-olds and normal adults pass. Autistic individuals — even high-functioning — often fail, saying "He's going to check under the bed."

### Chapter 6. Question and Answer on Autism

- **Q: Is the false-belief task different from the deception task?** No — both tap appreciation of false belief; the Maxie task is a "stripped-down version" of the deception study.
- **Q: Why mostly boys?** "Nobody knows," but Baron-Cohen's theory: certain abilities are more sequestered by sex; social abilities are more female. Provocatively: "**To be a man is to suffer from a very mild form of autism.**" Autistic individuals have "extreme male brains." To be revisited under sex differences.
- **Q: Severity of autism vs. developmental stages?** Surprisingly, autism can't really be thought of as a developmental delay. "Somebody with autism isn't like any child at all, any normally developing child at all." However, severity is partly indexed by how much language the person has.
- **Q: Can someone with autism overcome their deficiencies?** Media tends to showcase exceptional cases like **Temple Grandin**, who wrote books on her experience but is very unusual. Depends partly on whether **Asperger syndrome** (milder) is included. Majority of people with autism have severe problems and will not lead a normal life.
- **Q: Rain Man / autistic savants?** Rain Man (Dustin Hoffman) had extraordinary mathematical abilities. Some autistic individuals have extraordinary artistic, mathematical, or musical abilities — "amazing" but a very small minority.

**Candidate modules raised:**
- **Physics** (object module) and **people** (social module) — covered in the developmental data.
- **Artifacts** — for tables, chairs, cars, forks.
- **Sociology** — for human groups, races, classes.
- **Intuitive biology** — common-sense biological understanding; defended by **Frank Keil**, Master of Morse College at Yale.

**Beyond modules — general differences:**
- A "very, very big difference between a creature that doesn't have language and a creature that does." Learning to speak may reconfigure the human brain in a way unparalleled in other species. To be revisited in the language lecture.

**Closing — Stephen Jay Gould's example:**
- Even if you hate babies and development, developmental data can inform questions about adults.
- Gould asked: "Is a zebra a black animal with white stripes or a white animal with black stripes?" Looking at adult zebras won't answer it — but watching embryological development of a zebra will.
- Final quote from biologist **D'Arcy Thompson**, author of *On Growth and Form*: "**Everything is the way it is because it got that way.**"
