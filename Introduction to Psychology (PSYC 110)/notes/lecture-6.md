# PSYC 110 - Lecture 6 - How Do We Communicate?: Language in the Brain, Mouth and the Hands

Source: https://oyc.yale.edu/psychology/psyc-110/lecture-6

## Overview

This lecture by Professor Paul Bloom introduces the scientific study of language, restricting "language" to systems like English, Dutch, Warlpiri, Italian, Turkish, Urdu, and sign languages, in order to make scientific generalizations possible. Bloom argues that any adequate theory of language must account for both deep universals shared across languages and striking differences between them. He frames language as plausibly part of human nature, citing Darwin's claim, the universal presence of language in human societies, the creation of creoles from pidgins within a single generation, neurological dedication, and possible genetic bases. He walks through the three core structural levels of language — phonology, morphology, and syntax — and then turns to Chomsky's claim that language acquisition is more like growth than learning, surveying the developmental time course from prenatal melody preferences through the post-puberty decline in language-learning ability.

## Key Concepts & Definitions

- **Language (scientific/narrow sense):** Systems like English, Dutch, Warlpiri, Italian, Turkish, Urdu, American Sign Language, Navajo. Bloom restricts the term in order to find "interesting generalizations" and "do good science."
- **Linguistics:** An entire field devoted to studying the nuances and structures of different languages.
- **Pidgin:** A makeshift communication system developed when speakers of different languages (e.g., enslaved people from different language backgrounds, mixed deliberately in the slave trade to avoid revolt) need to talk to one another. "Strings of words borrowed from the different languages around them and put together in sort of haphazard ways." Not a language.
- **Creole:** A full language with rich syntax, morphology, and phonology, developed within a single generation by children raised in a pidgin-speaking society.
- **Aphasia:** Language deficit resulting from damage to dedicated language parts of the brain; may include losing the ability to understand or create language.
- **Phonology:** The system of sounds (or signs) of a language. English has about forty phonemes.
- **Phoneme:** A basic sound unit. Example: English has phonemes "l" and "r," so English speakers hear "lip" vs. "rip" as different words.
- **Morphology:** The system of words/morphemes.
- **Morpheme:** "The smallest meaningful unit in a language." Often the same as a word ("dog"), but words can contain multiple morphemes ("dogs" = "dog" + plural 's'; "complained" = two morphemes).
- **Arbitrariness of the sign (Ferdinand de Saussure):** Languages map arbitrary sounds/signs to ideas. Most concepts (e.g., "country," "idea") cannot be iconically represented.
- **Syntax:** Rules and principles that combine words into phrases and phrases into sentences. Same as "grammar" (syntax is the more technical term).
- **Infinite use of finite media (Wilhelm von Humboldt):** Going from a finite vocabulary to an infinite number of sentences via a combinatorial system.
- **Recursion:** The infinity mechanism in language — one rule invoking another rule (e.g., a sentence expanding to noun + verb + sentence), allowing potentially infinite embedding.
- **Top-down processing:** "When you know what something is you hear it that way." Fills in gaps in sound (e.g., hearing "sentence" through a cough).
- **Segmentation:** Determining word boundaries from a continuous sound stream. The "pauses" between words in your own language are a psychological illusion inserted by the mind.
- **Specific language impairment:** Genetic disorder where children, though smart, socially motivated, and wanting to communicate, cannot learn language normally.
- **Subject-verb-object (SVO) vs. subject-object-verb (SOV):** English is SVO ("Bill hit John"). The majority of languages are SOV ("Bill John hit").
- **Linguistic isolate:** A person (e.g., a deaf person in a society where no one signs to them) who grows up without language input.

## Detailed Notes

### Chapter 1. The Scientific Notion of Language and Structure

- Language is a battleground for theories of human nature. Every major thinker about people has had to make claims about it: Aristotle, Plato, Hume, Locke, Freud, Skinner; modern computational theory, cognitive neuroscience, evolutionary theory, cultural psychology.
- Bloom restricts "language" to systems like English, Dutch, Warlpiri, Italian, Turkish, Urdu, etc. Using "language" too broadly (e.g., for traffic signals, music, dog communication) makes scientific generalization useless.
- After defining narrowly, we can later ask whether animal communication systems share the same properties.
- Framing questions for lecture: basic facts about language; what languages share; how language develops; language/communication in nonhumans.
- Pre-class demonstration with bilingual students illustrated two facts:
  1. Languages share deep, intricate universals — every language can convey abstract notions about thoughts, propositions, and spatial relations in objects.
  2. Languages differ greatly — sounds, words, look unusual (for sign language); knowing one does not mean knowing another.

#### Darwin's claim

> "Man has an instinctive tendency to speak, as we see in the babble of our young children, while no child has an instinctive tendency to bake, brew or write."

- Claim: language is special — a propensity, capacity, or instinct.

#### Evidence that language is part of human nature

- Every human society has language. No group encountered without one in human history.
- This alone doesn't prove it's built in — could be a useful cultural innovation (like utensils for eating: knife/fork, chopsticks, spoon).
- **Pidgin → Creole case:** Slave trade (tobacco, cotton, coffee, sugar) deliberately mixed laborers from different language backgrounds to prevent revolt. Adults developed pidgins. Children raised in this society did not speak pidgin; within a single generation they produced a creole — a real language with rich syntax, morphology, phonology.
- **Nicaragua sign language case:** Children acquired sign language from adults who were themselves second-language learners. Rather than copying the makeshift adult system, the children "creolized" it into a full-blown language.
- Every normal human has language; not everyone can ride a bicycle or play chess. Exceptions are due to brain damage.
- Neurological evidence: dedicated brain parts for language; damage produces aphasias.
- Genetic evidence: some people have point mutations in genes implicated in language and cannot learn/use language normally.

#### Creativity of language (Descartes)

- Descartes argued language is the best evidence we are more than machines — language is unbounded and free; we have free will.
- We can produce a virtual infinity of sentences. Grammatical English sentences under twenty words: "a lot."
- Comprehension cannot be a list — we understand sentences never said before. Bloom's invented examples:
  - "It's surprisingly easy to get a purple tie on eBay if you don't care much about quality."
  - "I am upset that one cannot easily download 'Buffy the Vampire Slayer' through iTunes."
- Rules in our heads are abstract, automatic, unconscious — similar to rules of visual perception.

#### Examples of implicit syntactic knowledge

- "The pig is eager to eat" — pig is the eater.
- "The pig is easy to eat" — pig is being eaten.
- "Bill knew that John liked him" — "him" can refer to Bill or to a third party (Fred), but not to John. Natural reading: him = Bill.
- "Bill knew that John liked himself" — only means John liked John.
- Bloom credits Steven Pinker's *The Language Instinct* for examples.

#### Three core levels of language

| Level | Definition |
|---|---|
| Phonology | System of sounds or signs |
| Morphology | System of words/morphemes (basic units of meaning) |
| Syntax | Rules and principles combining words/phrases into meaningful utterances |

### Chapter 2. Phonology: A System of Sounds

- English has about forty phonemes; native monolingual English listeners categorize sounds into these.
- "l" vs. "r" example: English speakers hear "lip" vs. "rip"; speakers of languages lacking the distinction struggle to learn it.
- **Segmentation problem:** must figure out word boundaries from sound signal.
- The pause between words is a psychological illusion — an oscilloscope shows no pauses. Your mind inserts pauses where you already know the boundaries are.
- Demonstrations of segmentation:
  - French: "Je ne sais pas" — non-French listeners hear no pauses.
  - Hebrew: "Sleecha, eypho ha-sheeruteem" (a request for the bathroom).
  - When bilingual students gave demos, they sounded like "blublublublub" because the listener didn't know the language.
  - Imagined properly-segmented English from another's perspective: "Glorp [pause] fendel [pause] smug [pause] wuggle."
- Children make segmentation mistakes — visible in misheard song lyrics:
  - "I'll never be your pizza burnin'" → "Beast of burden."
  - "A girl with colitis goes by" → "A girl with kaleidoscope eyes."
  - "The ants are my friends; they're blowin' in the wind."
  - Religious example: "Our father with Bart in heaven; Harold be they name… Lead us not into Penn Station…"
- **Top-down processing:** what you know shapes what you hear.
  - Rick James "Super Freak" — many hear "the kind of girl you read about in Newsweek magazine"; actual lyric: "in new wave magazines." Once told, you hear it that way.
  - Coughed-over "s[cough]entence" is perceived as "sentence" — gap is filled.
  - "Get Crunk" example: even the clean (censored) version still sounds obscene because of top-down processing.
- Theme to be revisited with vision: prior knowledge clears up confusing perceptual input.

### Chapter 3. Morphology: A System of Words

- Saussure's **arbitrariness of the sign:** any arbitrary idea can be linked to a sound/sign.
  - "Dog" could be "woof woof" (somewhat iconic), but "country" cannot sound like a country.
  - In sign language, "drink" can resemble drinking; "country" or "idea" cannot be iconic.
- Vocabulary = the set of these arbitrary mappings (technically: morphemes).
- A morpheme is the smallest meaningful unit. "Dog" = one morpheme; "dogs" and "complained" = two morphemes each. Knowing "dog" + plural 's' lets you form "dogs" without ever having heard it.
- **Vocabulary size:** Average speaker knows ~60,000 words (low-ball); Bloom's estimate closer to 80,000–100,000. Starting around age 1, children learn roughly **nine new words per day** on average (varies by age).
- Multilinguals may carry 200,000–300,000 words, accessed in fractions of a second — "one of the most astonishing things that people do."

### Chapter 4. Syntax: Communicating Complicated Ideas

- Syntax combines words into phrases into sentences.
- **Humboldt:** "infinite use of finite media." Achieved via a **combinatorial system**.
- Other combinatorial systems: music (finite notes, infinite compositions); DNA (finite bases/amino acids, possibly infinite strings).
- **Recursion** as the infinity mechanism.

#### Toy grammar example

- Vocabulary: 3 nouns (Fred, Barney, Wilma) + 2 verbs (thinks, likes).
- Rule: sentence = noun + verb + noun.
- Total sentences: **18** (3 × 2 × 3). E.g., "Fred likes Wilma," "Fred thinks Barney," etc.
- Add recursion rule: sentence → noun + verb + sentence.
- Yields e.g. "Fred thinks Barney likes Wilma" — and a potential infinity of sentences.
- Real-life recursion example (escalating embedding):
  - "John hates cheese."
  - "My roommate heard a rumor that John hates cheese."
  - "It disturbed Mary when I told her that my roommate heard a rumor that John hates cheese."
  - …and so on. "You could keep producing a sentence deeper and deeper embedded until you die."

#### Ambiguity

- Different rules can produce the same surface sentence.
- Groucho Marx: "I once shot an elephant in my pajamas. How it got into my pajamas I'll never know."
- Newspaper headline ambiguities:
  - "Complaints about NBA referees growing ugly."
  - "Kids make nutritious snacks."
  - "No one was injured in a blast which was attributed to the buildup of gas by one town official."
  - "General arrested for fondling privates." (Seoul/Korea University)
- There is a legal sub-field using linguistic theory to disambiguate sentences in the Constitution, legislation, and criminal cases.
- **Criminal case:** Two brothers (one with cognitive disability) committed a robbery. A police officer pointed his gun and said, "Give me the gun." The brother holding the gun was told by the other: **"Let him have it."** Ambiguity:
  - "Shoot him, let him have it" (= fire), or
  - "Give him the gun, let him have it."
  - The brother fired and killed the officer. Bloom's understanding: the speaker was found guilty.

### Chapter 5. Question and Answer on Language Structure

- **Q: How does syntax differ from grammar?** A: They are the same; "syntax" is the more technical term.
- **Q: Neurologically normal humans without language?** A: Bloom corrects: not every neurologically normal person ends up with language. Cases of children raised by wolves/dogs (likely apocryphal); 20th-century children locked away by insane/evil parents; deaf "linguistic isolates" in societies where no one signs to them. These show: a brain alone is not enough — someone must use language with you.
  - **Susan Goldin-Meadow:** studied deaf children with no signing input but who had deaf siblings. They create their own language — not a full system like ASL or *langue des signes québécoise*, but a language with words, syntax, phonology.
- **Q: Are there inherent limits on grammars?** A: Yes. Most linguists agree languages are highly constrained.
  - No language constructs a question by reversing word order.
  - No language has a rule "the fifth word must be a verb."
  - Constraints reveal what counts as a humanly natural language; but a constrained grammar still yields infinite sentences (analogy: restricting to odd numbers still gives infinitely many).

### Chapter 6. Noam Chomsky and Language Acquisition

- Chomsky (author of *A Review of Verbal Behavior*) makes a radical claim: language is not learned but **grown**.

> "No one would take seriously the proposal that a human organism learns through experience to have arms rather than wings, or that the basic structure of particular organs results from accidental experience. [Language] proves to be no less marvelous and intricate than these physical structures. Why, then, should we not study the acquisition of a cognitive structure like language more or less as we study some complex bodily organ?"

- So you didn't "learn" English the way you learned baseball or the American Civil War — exposure triggered growth.
- Environment does shape language: English requires English input; specific contrasts (l/r) and words ("dog" vs. *chien* vs. Greek term) are language-specific historical accidents across ~6,000 languages (Vietnamese, Urdu, Czech, etc.).
- Word order varies: English is SVO ("Bill hit John"); the majority of languages are SOV ("Bill John hit").
- Evidence that development is like growth:
  - All normal children acquire language.
  - Specific impairments exist: aphasias from trauma/stroke; genetic disorders (**specific language impairment**) in which smart, social, motivated children cannot learn language. Suggests language ability is separable from general intelligence, social motivation, and communicative drive.
- Language is learned without explicit feedback/training.
  - American "teach your baby language" industry (DVDs, flash cards) — not necessary.
  - In some communities adults don't speak to babies (saying "It'd be ridiculous to speak to a baby. The baby has nothing to say. You might as well just speak to your dog"). When American linguists answered "Yeah. We speak to our dogs," they revealed a cultural difference. Lack of baby-directed speech does not prevent language learning.
  - **Brown and Hanlon (1970s):** parents respond to affect/cuteness/sociability, not to grammaticality. A parent will not correct "I loves you, Mommy" for verb agreement; will not praise "I hate your guts, Mother" for clean subject-verb-object structure. Grammatical errors disappear without correction.

### Chapter 7. The Time Course of Language Acquisition

- **Prenatal/newborn melody preference:**
  - Studies in France with four-day-old babies using a sucking method on a pacifier — babies sucked harder to hear French than Russian.
  - Reviewers (mostly French) said maybe French just sounds better. Replicated in Russia: Russian babies sucked harder for Russian than French.
  - Babies are responding to the **rhythm** of the language, not words or syntax.
- **Early phonemic sensitivity:**
  - English-speaking babies distinguish English phonemes (e.g., "lip" vs. "rip") AND non-English contrasts (e.g., Czech, Hindi).
  - You as a baby were a "multilingual fool"; this sensitivity **narrows down to the language you hear by about twelve months of age**.
- **Q&A about baby talk:** Speaking in perfect adult English to babies is unusual; most people use "Oh. You're such a cute little baby."-style talk. Evolutionary psychologists debate function — aiding language learning vs. calming. Whether you do it or not doesn't make a big difference. It is very difficult to find effects of how parents talk to babies on language outcomes.
- **~7 months: babbling.**
- **Sign language acquisition:** A surprising finding of the last 10–20 years — acquisition of sign languages is, as far as known, **exactly the same** as spoken languages. Same developmental milestones: babbling, first words, first sentences, first complicated constructions, at the same points. No advantage for speech over sign.
- **~12 months:** First words — objects and actions ("dog," "up," "milk"). Sensitivity to word order ("dog bites cat" ≠ "cat bites dog").
- **~18 months:** Faster word-learning. Mini-sentences ("Want cookie," "Milk spill"). Function morphemes ("in," "of," "a," "the") gradually appear.
- **~7 years through puberty:** Language-learning ability declines.
  - **Elissa Newport and Sam Supalla:** studied immigrants in the US for 30–40 years. Best predictor of English proficiency is **not** intelligence, family size, or motivation — it is **age at first exposure**.
  - Learning a second language in the first few years: speak like a native.
  - Past puberty: huge variation; rare to lose an accent; phonology, syntax, morphology all affected.
  - "It's like the part of the brain that's responsible for language learning is only around early in development and if you don't get your language by then it'll just run out."
