# PSYC 110 - Lecture 10 - Evolution, Emotion, and Reason: Evolution and Rationality

Source: https://oyc.yale.edu/psychology/psyc-110/lecture-10

## Overview

This lecture introduces a second "astonishing hypothesis" of modern psychology — what Daniel Dennett called Darwin's dangerous idea — the modern biological account of the origin of biological (and psychological) phenomena. Professor Bloom contrasts William Paley's creationist argument from design with Darwin's account of natural selection, showing how variation, differential survival and reproduction, and adaptation can produce complex structures like the eye without a designer. He then explores how evolutionary theory does and doesn't apply to psychology, warning against confusing ultimate and proximate causation and against assuming everything is adaptive. The lecture closes with an introduction to human rationality through the heuristics-and-biases tradition of Kahneman and Tversky, including framing effects, base rates, availability bias, and confirmation bias, ending with the Wason selection task and Leda Cosmides's ecological reformulation.

## Key Concepts & Definitions

- **The Astonishing Hypothesis (Crick)**: The idea that our mental life, consciousness, morality, and capacity to make decisions and judgments is the product of a material physical brain.
- **Darwin's dangerous idea (Dennett)**: The modern biological account of the origin of biological phenomena, including psychological phenomena.
- **Creationism**: The view that biological structures were created by an intelligent being.
- **Argument from design (Paley)**: A complicated artifact like a watch (or an eye) implies an intelligent designer; the eye's complexity, like a camera's, suggests a designer "far smarter than any human engineer."
- **Vestigial characteristics**: Features of the human body (e.g., tailbone, goose-bumps) that are inexplicable unless the body is viewed as modifications from a previous form.
- **Natural selection**: Has three components — variation, differential survival and reproduction, and inheritance — giving rise to adaptations ("that perfection of structure that justly excites our imagination").
- **Adaptation**: A trait shaped by natural selection to solve a problem of survival and reproduction.
- **Byproduct / accident**: Traits that are not adaptations but accidental consequences of how the body/mind is built (e.g., back pain, hiccups, self-pity, bloating).
- **Ultimate causation**: Why something is there in the first place, over millions of years of history.
- **Proximate causation**: Why you are doing something now (the day-to-day psychological reason).
- **Auditory cheesecake (Pinker)**: Music as an evolutionary accident with no adaptive advantage — something we like to gorge ourselves on.
- **Heuristics (Kahneman & Tversky)**: Rough and ready rules of thumb that served us during evolutionary history but can lead us astray.
- **Framing effect**: Responding differently to a situation depending on how options are framed.
- **Loss aversion**: People hate a certain loss; framing in terms of losses changes decisions.
- **Endowment effect**: Once you own something, its value shoots up.
- **Base rates**: Background statistical frequencies, which people tend to ignore in favor of individuating information.
- **Availability bias**: Judging frequency by how easily examples come to mind.
- **Confirmation bias**: When holding a hypothesis, we look for confirmations.
- **Wason selection task**: A logical card task that is hard in abstract form but easier when framed as an ecologically valid social rule (Leda Cosmides).

## Detailed Notes

### Chapter 1. The Modern Biological Account of the Origin of Psychological Phenomena

- Course opened with Francis Crick's "Astonishing Hypothesis": mental life, consciousness, morality, and decision-making are products of a material physical brain.
- A second, equally or more shocking idea concerns the **origin** of mental life — Daniel Dennett's "Darwin's dangerous idea."
- **Paley's argument from design**:
  - Walking on a beach: a rock needs no special explanation, but a watch does.
  - Paley (a medical doctor) compared the watch to the eye: both have many parts interacting in complex ways.
  - Updated analogy: the eye is like a camera.
    - Both have lenses that bend light and project an image onto a light-sensitive surface (retina vs. film).
    - Both have a focusing mechanism (muscles changing the lens vs. a diaphragm).
    - Both are encased in black.
  - The eye is far more complicated than a camera. Bloom's "Six Million Dollar Man" (test pilot Steve Austin, 1974) anecdote: even 30+ years later, building an artificial eye that matches the human eye is fantasy, not even science fiction.
  - Paley's conclusion: complicated biological things must be created by an intelligent designer — God.
  - Bloom showed a Google Images search for "God," yielding "an old-bearded man wearing a crown."
- **Problems with the creationist view**:
  - It pushes back the question: where did the intelligent being come from? Particularly serious for psychological structures.
  - Evidence for evolution (broadly, not a specific mechanism):
    - **Fossil evidence** of rudimentary forms of body parts.
    - **Vestigial characteristics** like the human tailbone and goose-bumps.
    - **Parallels with other animals**: human, rat, cat, monkey brains follow a common plan — suggesting evolutionary descent.
    - **Occasional poor design**: the eye's blind spot; the male urethra running through the prostate gland — God is either malicious or incompetent.
- **Richard Dawkins** (*The Blind Watchmaker*): 100–150 years ago, anyone who didn't believe God created humans was a "moron" because the argument from design is "a damn good argument" in the absence of an alternative.
- **Darwin's accomplishment**: showed how complex biological structures like the eye emerge through a purely non-intentional, purely physical process — possibly the most important idea in the sciences.
- **Three components of natural selection**: variation → differential survival/reproduction → inheritance → adaptations ("that perfection of structure that justly excites our imagination").
- Examples:
  - **Camouflage**: animals better hidden survive and reproduce more.
  - **The eye**: parallels in other animals show rudimentary forms; computer simulations have generated eyes from plausible starting points under selective pressure.
- Why evolution matters for psychology: combine Darwin's idea with the rejection of Descartes (minds are physical). Then mental life is no less the product of natural selection than the eye or camouflage. Cognitive mechanisms evolved for survival and reproduction, shaped to solve specific problems (perception, communication, nutrition, rest, etc.).

### Chapter 2. Avoiding Misconceptions When Applying Evolutionary Theory to Psychology

- **Misconception 1**: Natural selection means animals consciously want to spread their genes.
  - Confuses **ultimate** and **proximate causation**.
  - A cockroach knows nothing about genes; mechanisms differ from mental states.
  - **William James** on why we eat:
    > Not one man in a billion when taking his dinner ever thinks of utility. He eats because the food tastes good and makes him want more. If you asked him why you should want to eat more of what tastes like that, instead of revering you as a philosopher, he will probably laugh at you for a fool.
  - "You eat because you're hungry" (proximate) and "you eat to spread your genes" (ultimate) are different levels of explanation, not competing alternatives.
  - Parents protect children because offspring carry 50% of their genes (ultimate); psychologically, no one but "a deranged psychologist" answers this way.
- **Misconception 2**: Everything is adaptive.
  - Distinguish **adaptations** vs. **byproducts/accidents**.
  - Back pain is not an adaptation — it's an accidental byproduct of back shape. Same for hiccups, self-pity, bloating.
  - Three big modern preoccupations — **pornography, television, and chocolate** — are largely evolutionary accidents:
    - Heterosexual male attraction to women is plausibly adaptive; porn substitutes are "evolutionary adaptive dead ends."
    - Liking chocolate stems from an evolved taste for sweet things (e.g., fruit in our natural environment); chocolate bars are recent and not good for us.
- **Clear cases**:
  - **Color vision** — adaptation (visual distinctions).
  - **Fear of snakes** — adaptation (ancestors not afraid didn't reproduce as much).
  - **Liking chocolate bars and NASCAR** — cannot be adaptations; too recent.
- **Hard cases (debated)**:
  - **Music**: Steven Pinker (*The Language Instinct*) called music "auditory cheesecake" — an evolutionary accident. Others argue music has an adaptive advantage.
  - **Male sexual violence**: adaptation or accident?
  - **Multiple languages**: accidental byproduct or group-selective advantage?
  - **Visual art, fiction, love of stories**: all subject to heated debate.

### Chapter 3. Claims against the Evolutionary Psychology

- **Objection 1 (metaphysical / dualist)**: If mental life is not the product of the brain, evolution is irrelevant.
  - **Lisa Simpson** said the Pope favored evolution — half right.
  - **John Paul II** said Darwinian theory is not incompatible with truth about man, but drew a line:
    > If the human body takes its origin from preexisting living matter, the spiritual soul is immediately created by God. Consequently, theories of evolution which consider the mind as emerging from the forces of living matter or as a mere epiphenomenon of this matter are incompatible to the truth about man.
- **Objection 2**: Even if the mind is physical, humans have no instincts.
  - **Ashley Montagu (1973)**:
    > With the exception of the reactions of infants to sudden withdrawals of support and to sudden loud noises, the human being is entirely instinctless. Man is man because he has no instincts, because everything he is and has become he has learned from his culture...
  - **Louis Menand** (New Yorker): "Every aspect of life has a biological foundation in exactly the same sense, which is that unless it was biologically possible it wouldn't exist. After that it's up for grabs." Despite work by **Spelke** and **Baillargeon** on infant hard-wiring, Menand thinks the interesting aspects of human nature are cultural.
- **Objection 3**: Even if there's a human nature, you can study it directly without evolution — what could evolution add?

### Chapter 4. Ways in Which Evolution Helps Describe the Mind

- Evolution of consciousness, morality, etc., is intuitively interesting in its own right.
- **What can be innate and what cannot**:
  - Plausibly innate adaptations: talking, mate selection, childcare.
  - Cannot be innate (too recent): written communication, interacting with strangers, driving a car, playing chess.
- **Group differences**:
  - **Children vs. adults**: face different evolutionary problems — predictable differences in brains.
  - **Racial / ethnic differences**: evolutionary theory makes no predictions about profound mental differences across humans evolved in different parts of the world.
  - **Sex differences**: evolutionary biology gives tools to discuss what to expect; Bloom shows a clip from the movie **"Roger Dodger"** as an example of "barroom evolutionary psychology" — a mix of reasonable claims and "total bull crap."
    - Map-reading sex difference claims: implausible biological root.
    - Trait developing to attract females: less crazy.
    - Telepathic claims: crazy.
- **Design perspective gives insights into current nature**. Two examples:
  - **Margie Profet on pregnancy sickness** (won the MacArthur Genius Award):
    - Traditional view: hormonal breakdown.
    - Profet: morning sickness is designed to protect the developing fetus from **teratogens** (poisons).
    - Predictions:
      - Onset/offset matches the period of maximal embryonic/fetal vulnerability.
      - Foods avoided correspond to those most deadly for the fetus **during the period when humans evolved**.
      - No aversion to **alcohol** — wasn't around in evolutionary history.
      - Relationship between **miscarriage and birth defects**: more morning sickness → more protection. Evidence supports this.
  - **Peter Salovey's "big three"** of attraction: **proximity, similarity, familiarity**.
    - Evolutionary problem: siblings are extremely close, similar, familiar — yet not "hot."
    - Reason: **incest avoidance**. Inter-mating with kin produces bad offspring.
    - No parenting guides address keeping siblings from having sex — they don't want to.
    - Illustrates proximate vs. ultimate causation: gut-level "eew," not reasoning about non-viable offspring.
  - **How do you tell who your kin are?**
    - Earlier evidence — **kibbutz studies**: people raised communally know they're unrelated but show diminished sexual desire toward each other.
    - **New paper in *Nature* (five days ago)**: an even bigger cue is whether you observed your **mother taking care of that person** — this diminishes desire to the level of disgust.

### Chapter 5. Heuristics: Framing Effects, Base Rates, Availability Bias and Confirmation Bias

- Psychology has produced Nobel Prize winners — most recently **Danny Kahneman**, in collaboration with **Amos Tversky**.
- Kahneman & Tversky shifted views away from purely logical decision-making toward **heuristics** — useful during evolutionary history, but error-prone.

#### Framing Effects

- Classic disease problem: 600 people will die.
  - **Program A**: 200 saved.
  - **Program B**: 1/3 chance all saved, 2/3 chance nobody saved.
  - Most pick A.
- Reframe in terms of deaths and chance of dying: preferences flip — a **framing effect**, combined with **loss aversion**.
- Practical example: hamburger advertised as "80% fat free" vs. "20% fat."
- **Laurie Santos** capuchin monkey studies:
  - Monkeys taught to use discs as money to buy banana or apple pieces.
  - Framing test: two experimenters both average 1.5 pieces.
    - One shows 1 piece, sometimes gives 2 (gain frame).
    - The other (pink experimenter) shows 2 pieces, sometimes gives 1 (loss frame).
  - Capuchins prefer the experimenter who shows 1 and sometimes gives 2 — framing/reference-point effects in nonhuman primates.
- **Endowment effect**:
  - "How much will you give me for this chocolate bar?" → ~$2.
  - "How much will you sell me this chocolate bar for?" → ~$2.50.
  - Owning something raises its value; selling is treated as a loss.

#### Base Rates

- 70 engineers, 30 lawyers; John is "40, married, three children, conservative, cautious, no interest in politics, awkward around people. His hobbies include carpentry, sailing, and solving mathematical puzzles, like online dating."
- Most say engineer; switching the base rates (30 engineers, 70 lawyers) doesn't change responses — people **ignore base rates**.
- **Disease test problem**:
  - Disease hits 1 in 1,000.
  - Test never misses (100% sensitivity), 5% false positive rate.
  - Given a positive test, probability of disease?
  - Medical students typically guess 50–95%.
  - Correct answer ≈ **2%**:
    - Of 1,000 people: 1 has disease, tests positive.
    - 999 don't have disease; ~5% (≈50) false positives.
    - So ~1 in 51 positive tests is a true case → ~2%.
- People panic because minds aren't evolved for base-rate computation.

#### Availability Bias

- Frequency judged by ease of mental retrieval.
- Classic Kahneman & Tversky: more "ing" words estimated than "ng" words, though every "ing" is "ng."
- **Shark attacks**:
  - Injury risk: 1 in 6 million.
  - Death: 1 in 500 million.
  - In Florida ("Shark Central"): injury risk ~1 in half a million.
  - Overestimated because shark attacks are salient/newsworthy.
- **Potato salad / food poisoning**:
  - Injury ~1 in 800; death ~1 in 55,000.
  - Potato salad is ~1,000 times more dangerous than shark attacks.
- **Estimating proportion of Jews in the US**:
  - Actual: between **1.9% and 2.1%**.
  - Average American thinks **20%**.
  - New York City: ~9% (most dense); New Haven: ~3.5%.
  - Cause: Jews prominent in highly visible positions (entertainment, academia) → easily come to mind.

#### Confirmation Bias

- **Child custody scenario** (jury must give sole custody):
  - **Parent A**: average income, average health, average work hours, reasonable rapport, relatively stable social life.
  - **Parent B**: above-average income, minor health problems, lots of work travel, very close relationship with child, extremely active social life.
  - When asked **who to award custody to** → slight preference for B.
  - When asked **who to deny custody to** → slight preference for B.
  - When asked to award, salient good-parent traits of B jump out; when asked to deny, salient bad-parent traits of B jump out.
- General principle: when we have a hypothesis, we look for confirmations.

#### Wason Selection Task

- Four cards: D, G, 3, 8 (letter on one side, number on the other).
- Rule: "If a card has a 'D' on one side, it has a '3' on the other side."
- Most people answer "D" or "D and 3" — but the correct answer is **"D" and "8"** (since you must check for a D on the back of 8 to falsify the rule).
- People find the abstract version very hard.
- **Leda Cosmides** (evolutionary psychologist, Santa Barbara): when reframed as an ecologically valid **social rule** (e.g., bartender checking that no one under 21 drinks beer — flip cards showing "beer" and "under 21"), the same logical problem becomes easy.
- Implies our minds are tuned to solve logically equivalent problems differently depending on **ecological/social content**.
