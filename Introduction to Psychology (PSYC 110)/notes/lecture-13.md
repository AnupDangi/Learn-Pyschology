# PSYC 110 - Lecture 13 - Why Are People Different?: Differences

Source: https://oyc.yale.edu/psychology/psyc-110/lecture-13

## Overview

After many lectures on human universals (language, rationality, perception, emotions, development), Professor Paul Bloom turns to the question of why people differ from one another psychologically. He frames the lecture around two main interesting factors at the root of all human differences: personality and intelligence — first asking how to characterize them and then asking why these differences exist. He warns that this is the class likely to bother students the most because the scientific findings on psychological differences are, to many, "shocking and unbelievable." After introducing measurement concepts (reliability and validity), personality frameworks (Eysenck, Cattell, the Big Five), and the structure of IQ ("G" and "S"), he turns to behavioral genetics — concluding with two surprising findings: (1) high heritability for almost everything, and (2) almost everything that's not genetic is due to non-shared environment, meaning shared environment (i.e., parents) counts for little or nothing once genes are controlled for.

## Key Concepts & Definitions

- **Personality**: A stable trait across situations and time — how someone deals with the world and with other people. Not a momentary reaction to a situation, but something carried around.
- **Reliability**: A test has no measurement error; testing the same person at different times yields the same result.
- **Validity**: The test measures what it's supposed to measure.
- **Rorschach inkblot test**: Personality test invented by Herman Rorschach using inkblots; widely used but, per Bloom, "transcendently useless."
- **The Big Five (OCEAN)**: Openness, Conscientiousness, Extroversion, Agreeableness, Neuroticism — five fundamental dimensions of personality.
- **Intelligence**: Hard to define, but core components named by experts include abstract reasoning, problem solving, and the capacity to acquire knowledge.
- **"G" (general intelligence)**: A general intellectual capacity people bring to all tasks in common.
- **"S" (specific intelligence)**: Ability on specific tests/subtests within an IQ battery.
- **Wechsler test**: Modern IQ test (for adults and children) where 100 is set as the average score.
- **Heredity / heritability**: The proportion of variation in a trait (across people) due to genetic differences — a claim about differences, not about the origin of any specific trait in one individual.
- **Shared environment**: Environmental influences shared by people raised in the same household (e.g., the same parents).
- **Non-shared environment**: Everything else not heredity and not shared environment — a "garbage can" category.
- **Monozygotic (MZ) twins**: Genetic clones; share 100% of their genes.
- **Dizygotic (DZ) twins**: Share 50% of genes; like regular siblings.
- **Adopted siblings**: Zero percent genetic overlap beyond randomness.
- **Flynn effect**: People have been getting smarter over generations; IQ tests hide this because 100 is always reset as the average.

## Detailed Notes

### Chapter 1. Personality, Intelligence and Determining Difference [00:00:00]

- Prior lectures focused on universals; this lecture shifts to differences.
- Plan: (1) how people are different (theories of difference) and (2) why people are different.
- Bloom warns: "the scientific findings on human psychological differences are, to many of us, shocking and unbelievable."
- Examples of ways people differ:
  - **Sexual identity** — male/female; central to most people's being; indexed in pronouns.
  - **Sexual orientation** — who you're attracted to (deferred to post-spring-break lecture).
  - **Happiness** — rank 1–10 differs across the room; deferred to last class of semester.
  - **Success and failure in life** — relationships, job satisfaction, criminal records.
- Two main factors at the root of human differences: **personality** and **intelligence**.

**Personality characterized**:
- A person's style in dealing with the world and with other people.
- Example: Homer (impulsive, irresponsible, sometimes lazy, good-hearted) vs. Bloom's colleague (responsible, reliable, very kind).
- Must be a **stable trait across situations and time** — not a one-off reaction to being smacked in the head, but how you walk around all the time.

**Reliability vs. validity**:
- **Reliability**: no measurement error; same result across testings. Example: a bathroom scale that gives the same number is reliable.
- **Validity**: the test measures what it claims. Example: predicting intelligence from birth date (Bloom was born Christmas Eve) — perfectly reliable but absolutely not valid.
- Something can be reliable but not valid, and vice versa.

**Personality tests in the wild**:
- Web-based tests are common. Bloom took "which super hero are you?" and got Batman ("you are dark, love gadgets, and have vowed to help the innocent not suffer the pain you have endured"). Earlier attempts gave him The Incredible Hulk, then Wonder Woman; eventually he tailored answers to get Batman — undermining both reliability and validity.
- The same site also offers a "hot or not" test.

**The Rorschach inkblot test**:
- Invented by **Herman Rorschach**, whose teenage nickname was "Inkblot."
- Originally for psychiatric cases, now extremely common.
- ~80% of clinical psychologists claim to use it; most APA-accredited graduate programs teach it; Catholic seminaries use it for seminary applicants.
- Used in child custody cases.
- Right/wrong answers exist: "it is important to see the blot as two human figures, usually females or clowns." Failing to do so is "a sign you have problems relating to people." Acceptable alternatives: "a cave entrance," "butterfly," or "vagina."
- Bloom's verdict: "transcendently useless... as useless as throwing dice... as useless as tea leaves."
- Inkblots and answers are available on the web.

**Reducing personality dimensions**:
- **Gordon Allport** went through the dictionary, found ~18,000 personality-related traits, but many overlapped (friendly/sociable/welcoming/warm-hearted tap the same thing).
- **Cattell** and others tried to narrow it down — how many parameters of personality difference are needed?
- **Eysenck**: initially two dimensions — introverted–extroverted, neurotic–stable → four types of people. Later added **psychoticism vs. non-psychoticism** (aggressive vs. empathetic) → eight types.
- **Cattell**: 16 personality factors.

### Chapter 2. Measuring Personality with "The Big Five" [00:13:53]

- Modern consensus: 2–3 too few, 16 too many. The **Big Five** dimensions:
  - **Neurotic vs. stable** (nutty/worrying vs. calm)
  - **Extrovert vs. introvert**
  - **Open to experience vs. closed to experience**
  - **Agreeable** (courteous, friendly) **vs. non-agreeable** (rude, selfish)
  - **Conscientious vs. not conscientious** (careful/reliable vs. careless/undependable)
- Mnemonic: **OCEAN** — Openness, Conscientiousness, Extroversion, Agreeableness, Neuroticism.

**Why take the Big Five seriously**:
- **Reliability over time**: Stable across years; once you pass age 30, very stable indeed. Test parents now; in 10 years they'll still be there.
- **Agreement across observers**: Roommate, professor, and mom's ratings of you tend to overlap a lot. "Your personality leaves a trail in the minds of people around you."
- **Predicts real-world behavior**:
  - **Conscientiousness** → faithfulness to spouse.
  - **Openness** → likelihood of changing jobs.
  - **Extroverts** look people in the eye more and have more sexual partners.

**Simpsons study**:
- 13 subjects rated Simpsons characters on the five dimensions; considerable agreement.
- Most **agreeable**: Ned Flanders (6.27) and Marge Simpson (5.46).
- Least agreeable: Mr. Burns and Nelson. (Nelson is "the little kid that when trouble happens he goes, 'Ha ha.'")

### Chapter 3. Defining and Measuring Intelligence [00:19:47]

- No easy definition. A survey of 1,000 experts:
  - Core consensus: **abstract reasoning, problem solving, capacity to acquire knowledge**.
  - Other commonly mentioned: memory, mental speed, language, math, knowledge, creativity.
- Gut intuition: Homer (limited intelligence), Bloom's colleague (very smart), Einstein (really, really smart), Ralph Wiggum (particularly stupid).

**Spearman's G and S**:
- **S**: ability on specific subtests (math, reading, spatial — different scores on each).
- **G**: general intelligence brought to all tasks in common.
- Why G is needed: performance on multiple intellectual tasks is correlated — there's a sort of general correlation people bring to tasks.
- Casual usage: "I think he is high 'G'" means he's a smart guy.

**Athletic analogy**: A gym with running, basketball shooting, swimming, fencing, etc. Scores aren't independent — someone good at running and swimming is probably good at climbing. Likewise IQ has a general factor.

**Modern IQ tests (Wechsler, adult and child)**:
- **100 is average** by definition (like grading the Midterm and giving the average 100).
- Works on the **normal curve**:
  - 68% score between 85 and 115.
  - 95% score between 70 and 130.
  - Above 145 IQ = 0.13% of the population.

**The Bell Curve and the controversy**:
- **Herrnstein and Murray, *The Bell Curve***: argued IQ matters immensely for everyday life; status, wealth, success follow from IQ.
- Sparked huge controversy.
- **American Psychological Association** convened 50 leading researchers to write a report; another group of IQ researchers wrote a separate report. Both reports' links were on the slide.

**Broad consensus on IQ's importance**:
- IQ is "strongly related — probably more so than any other single measurable human trait — to many important educational, occupational, economic, and social outcomes."
- Strong correlation: success in school, success in military training.
- Moderate but robust: social competence.
- Smaller but consistent: law-abidingness.
- Conclusion: "whatever IQ tests measure it is of great practical and social importance."
- IQ matters for **social achievement, prestigious positions, on-the-job performance**.

**Caveats**:
- Some role of IQ may be a **self-fulfilling prophecy**: SAT to get into Yale, GRE for grad school, etc. Society could make height matter the same way (no one under six feet gets into Yale → height "predicts" educational success).
- But IQ is not entirely a social construction: IQ scores relate to **mental speed and memory span** (how fast you think, your memory abilities).

### Chapter 4. Question and Answer on Personality and Intelligence [00:30:29]

- **Q (gaming the test)**: A student took a personality test and was "accepted into Slytherin" (Hogwarts reference). How do you avoid gaming?
  - **A**: Test constructors use **catch questions** (e.g., "I have never done anything I am ashamed of" — endorsement suggests a liar). Same question is asked in different ways across the 100 items; correlations expose liars. Proof is in the pudding: a test easily fooled wouldn't survive.
- **Q (Emotional IQ)**: Is emotional/social intelligence a better predictor?
  - **A**: Evidence for predictive power not as strong as standard IQ; **Peter Salovey** has done interesting research on it. Emotional intelligence is also related to "good, old-fashioned intelligence" — they pull together.
- **Q (what makes a good test)**: Reliability + validity. Replicability over time plus relationship to real-world phenomena (grades, number of girlfriends, being liked). SAT is useful because it corresponds with other things like grades.
- **Q (stability of personality)**: Personality does change somewhat — a ten-year-old's test only weakly relates to the same person at 50. But "if you're an extrovert now you'll likely be an extrovert twenty years from now." Same for IQ — might go up or down but not much, justifying it as a "more or less stable trait."

### Chapter 5. The Roles of Genes and Environment in Explaining Human Differences [00:36:33]

- Two factors: **genes/heredity** and **environment/experience**.
- Crucial clarification: heritability is about the role of genes in explaining **differences between people**, not the proportion of an individual's trait due to genes.

**Height example**:
- Height is reasonably **heritable** — population differences are largely (not entirely) due to genes.

**Number of legs example**:
- Whether you have legs is highly genetic, but **variation in leg count (0, 1, 2) is not very heritable** — people with fewer legs typically lost them in accidents.
- Heredity is a claim about differences, not about the origin of any specific trait.

**Two kinds of environment**:
- **Shared environment**: things siblings in the same household share (e.g., having the same lousy parents).
- **Non-shared environment**: everything else — a snowball to the head at age five, winning the lottery at 21, even aliens from Pluto zapping your brain.
- Heredity + shared environment + non-shared environment = 1.

**Behavioral genetics tools** (you can't easily separate genes from environment in everyday families):
- **Monozygotic twins**: clones, 100% shared genes.
- **Dizygotic twins**: 50% shared genes; like regular siblings.
- **Adopted siblings**: 0% genetic overlap beyond randomness.
- Two people raised in the same house = 100% shared environment by definition.

**Comparisons and inferences**:
- MZ much more similar than DZ → large role of genes (caveat: MZ twins look more alike and may be treated more similarly).
- MZ no more similar than DZ → low role of heredity.
- Adopted children highly similar to their non-biological siblings → high role of shared environment.
  - Hypothetical: if the 7 Bloom kids all have IQ 104 and adopted kids also end up at 104, there's a household effect.
- Adopted kids' IQ unrelated to biological Bloom children's → being raised by Bloom has no effect on IQ.

**Identical twins reared apart — the gold standard**:
- Cartoon: "Separated at Birth, the Mallifert Twins Meet Accidentally" (twins end up at a patent office with the same device).
- Astonishing similarities in: attitudes to death penalty, religion, modern music; rates of crime, gambling, divorce.
- Bizarre similarities: a pair who both pretended to sneeze in elevators; the **"Giggle Twins"** who both burst into giggles constantly.
- Brain scans of MZ twins reared apart often indistinguishable.

**First surprising finding**:
- **High heritability for almost everything**: intelligence, personality, happiness, religiosity, political orientation, sexual orientation.

**Within-group vs. between-group differences**:
- Clear, reliable IQ score differences exist between American racial groups: whites, Asians, African Americans, Ashkenazi Jews.
- Groups are partially socially constructed (e.g., whether a child of a Jewish man and a non-Jewish woman counts as Jewish depends on reform vs. orthodox).
- But genuine genetic differences exist across groups too (e.g., Ashkenazi Jews and **Tay-Sachs** vulnerability).
- **High heritability within a group does NOT entail genetic differences between groups.**

**Richard Lewontin's seed analogy**:
- Two plots of land planted with the same seeds; one fertilized a lot, the other a little.
- Within each plot, growth differences are largely genetic (high heritability).
- The difference **between** plots has no genetic cause — it's the fertilizer.

**Bloom's two-Midterms thought experiment**:
- One side of the class gets a "savagely hard" Midterm; the other gets "Which is bigger, a dog or an elephant?"
- Within-group variation could be genes or study habits; between-group difference is entirely due to the test, not genes.

### Chapter 6. Genes, Environment and Intelligence [00:51:16]

**Two textbook reasons group differences are largely environmental, not genetic**:
1. IQ differences correspond better to **socially defined groups** (how people are treated/thought about) than to genetically defined groups.
2. **IQ can differ radically without genetic differences** — the **Flynn effect**.

**The Flynn effect**:
- People have been getting smarter; IQ tests hide this because 100 is always set as the current average.
- A 1980 average person taking the 1950 test would score about **120**.
- If you push today's average person back 20–30 years, they would do much better than average.
- No one knows why people are getting smarter; multiple theories. (Reading response: "Explain the Flynn effect.")
- Plotted with two lines (American and Dutch; Bloom isn't sure which is which).
- Implication: dramatic IQ change over decades without genetic change → likely environmental causes for group differences too.

**Second surprising finding**:
- **Almost everything that's not genetic is due to non-shared environment.**
- **Shared environment counts for little or nothing** for personality or intelligence.
- An adopted child is no more similar to his siblings than to a stranger.
- IQ correlation in genetically unrelated adults raised in the same family ≈ **zero**.
- "Suppose the Bloom family all has an IQ of 104 and we adopt a kid... What's his IQ? Answer? We have no idea because the IQ of the Bloom family who are unrelated to him has no effect at all."

**Do parents matter?**
- *Newsweek* cover captured the issue.
- Parents are shared environment. If shared environment doesn't affect intelligence/personality, then **parenting does not affect intelligence/personality.**
- Parents do have a huge effect — around **0.5** — but this is at the **moment of conception**. After that, very little role in shaping you.

**Judith Harris, *The Nurture Assumption***:
- Subtitle: "Why Children Turn Out the Way They Do, Parents Matter Less than You Think and Peers Matter More."
- Harris was kicked out of Harvard graduate school and told she wouldn't amount to much. The chair who wrote the dismissal letter was **George Miller**.
- In **1997**, Harris won the **George Miller award**.

**Philip Larkin's poem (her starting point of disagreement)**:
> They fuck you up, your mum and dad.
> They may not mean to but they do.
> They fill you with the faults they had
> And add some extra just for you.

Ending: "Man hands on misery to man. It deepens like a coastal shelf. Get out as early as you can and don't have any kids yourself."

**Harris's rebuttal**:
> How sharper than a serpent's tooth to hear your child make such a fuss. It isn't fair. It's not the truth. He's fucked up, yes, but not by us.

- British psychoanalyst **Oliver James**, outraged, wrote a rebuttal book called ***They Fuck You Up***.

**Why parent–child correlations don't prove parenting effects**:
- High correlation between parent and child for everything (bookish parents → bookish kids, religious parents → religious kids, "raised by Bonnie and Clyde, you will be a young thug," poor parents → poor children, brilliant parents → brilliant children).
- Three alternative explanations:
  1. Parents and children share genes.
  2. Children affect parents, not just vice versa.
  3. Some confound makes parents and kids both alike.

**The "worst study ever done" — family meals and teens**:
- Headline: family meals help teens avoid smoking, alcohol, drugs.
- Phone questionnaire of teens and parents.
- Bloom: "the worst study ever done in the history of science." Possible alternative explanations:
  - **Reverse causation**: a good kid sticks around for the family meal; a kid "smoking pot and cavorting with prostitutes" doesn't come home for dinner.
  - **Confounding family type**: good families have drug-free kids and family meals; bad families have stoned kids and no family meals.
  - **Age confound**: sample was ages 12–17. **Twelve-year-olds don't use a lot of drugs and eat with family; seventeen-year-olds "are stoned all the time and they don't eat with their family."**
- Same criticisms apply to "parents read to kids" studies — bookish kids may get parents to read to them; good parents do many good things.

**The cycle of violence**:
- Parents who smack kids tend to have more violent kids.
- Alternative explanations:
  - Reverse causation: troublemakers get smacked more.
  - Heritability of violence: violent kids inherit propensity to violence from violent parents, regardless of upbringing.

**Why this is hard to argue against personally**:
- Without being adopted, you can't disentangle "I'm like my parents because of upbringing" from "I'm like my parents because we share genes."
- "Mom smacked me a lot and that's why I turned out to be such a rotten person" — "Well, maybe she smacked you because you were rotten."

**Response to "this is a dangerous message"**:
- Concern: if parenting has no effect, why be nice to kids?
- Answers: because you love them; because you want them happy; because you want good relationships with them.
