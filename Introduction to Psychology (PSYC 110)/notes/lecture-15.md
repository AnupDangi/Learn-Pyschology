# PSYC 110 - Lecture 15 - A Person in the World of People: Morality

Source: https://oyc.yale.edu/psychology/psyc-110/lecture-15

## Overview

Professor Paul Bloom organizes the lecture around three facets of morality: moral feelings, moral judgments, and moral action (with particular focus on why good people do bad things, leading into a review of the Milgram study). He argues that moral feelings such as empathy and in-group caring can be grounded in evolution through kin selection and cooperation, but that empathy is unevenly distributed (as illustrated by psychopaths) and group membership powerfully shapes moral concern. Moral judgments are not just feelings; they carry obligation and sanctions, and although Western culture frames morality in terms of an ethics of autonomy, Jonathan Haidt's research shows people exhibit "moral dumbfounding," suggesting moral intuitions can run ahead of conscious reasoning. The lecture closes by laying out two forces for evil (deindividuation of self and denigration of others) and two forces for good (contact/interdependence and perspective taking).

## Key Concepts & Definitions

- **Moral feelings**: feelings of condemnation, shame, pride, righteous anger, affection, caring for others, and being upset at injustice.
- **Kin selection**: evolutionary explanation in which altruism is preserved because animals act to preserve others sharing their genes; caring is proportional to shared genes.
- **Cooperation**: a second evolutionary force for kindness; even unrelated animals are nice (warning cries, grooming, food exchange) because minds evolved to surmount prisoner's dilemmas, deception, and cheating, giving rise to moral emotions like guilt and anger.
- **Empathy**: "the feeling that your pain matters to me"; being hard-wired and attuned to another's pain.
- **Psychopaths**: a population in which instinctive empathy is broken and the pain of others doesn't bother them.
- **In-group / out-group**: differential moral caring based on group membership; we favor our group over others.
- **Robber's Cave study (Sherif)**: study with 11–12-year-old boys that demonstrated how arbitrary group divisions produce within-group solidarity, negative stereotypes, and intergroup hostility.
- **Superordinate goal**: a shared goal (and ideally a common enemy) that brings warring groups together.
- **Minimal groups (Tajfel)**: groups created with trivial criteria (e.g., Klee vs. Kandinsky lovers, coin flips) still elicit in-group favoritism.
- **Moral judgment**: an evaluation carrying a sense of obligation and sanctions (punishment for violation).
- **Ethics of autonomy**: morality framed in terms of rights, equality, and freedom.
- **Ethics of community**: morality framed in terms of duty, status, hierarchy, and interdependence.
- **Ethics of divinity**: morality framed in terms of purity, sanctity, pollution, and sin.
- **Moral dumbfounding (Haidt)**: people struggle to find rational explanations for moral judgments they hold strongly, suggesting intuitions precede reasoning.
- **Deindividuation of self**: diffusion of personal responsibility in groups, through obedience, or via anonymity.
- **Diffusion of responsibility**: in groups, individuals assume others will act, decreasing willingness to help.
- **Denigration of others**: reducing the moral status of others via distance, dehumanizing language, humor, removing names, or seeing them as disgusting.
- **Disgust**: a "body and soul emotion" (Paul Rozin); a basic human universal with a characteristic facial expression, elicited universally by feces, urine, blood, vomit, rotten flesh, and most meat.
- **Contact Hypothesis**: simple contact with others (especially under equal status, common goal, and social support) increases liking.
- **Perspective taking**: imagining oneself in another's place; expands moral concern.

### Chapter 2. Kin Selection, Cooperation and Moral Feelings [00:02:43]

Three facets of morality to be covered: moral feelings, moral judgments, moral action (Milgram).

**Evolution of moral feelings**: two answers.
- **Kin selection**: evolution at the level of genes; altruism preserved because it protects shared genes. Even dying to save another can be explained in cold-blooded evolutionary terms.
- **Cooperation**: animals (warning cries, grooming, food exchange) evolved to cope with prisoner's dilemmas, deception, cheating; produces emotions like guilt and anger.

**Empathy**
- Definition: "the feeling that your pain matters to me."
- Adam Smith (often falsely seen as a proponent of selfishness) noted empathic flinching:
  > When we see a stroke aimed and just ready to fall upon the leg or arm of another person, we naturally shrink and draw back our own leg or arm and when it does fall we feel it in some measure and are hurt by it as well as the sufferer.
- Examples: cringing at someone kicked in the groin in a movie, or banging their thumb with a hammer.
- **Babies**: hearing other babies cry is aversive to them. Cynical alternative: babies are too stupid to distinguish; clever experiment played tapes of other babies vs. self crying — babies cry more to others' crying than their own, indicating sensitivity to "otherness."
- **Chimpanzees**: studies (now considered unethical) — a chimp in one room presses a lever for food; pressing shocks a non-relative chimp in adjacent room. Chimps refrain (don't starve themselves but go long periods without food). Species-specific: with a rabbit in the other room, the chimp slaps the lever repeatedly to make the rabbit scream and jump.
- **Aristotle**: empathy not logically tied to morality — seeing pain might cause you to run away or blame the sufferer. But empathy induction increases pro-social action; high-empathy individuals tend to be nicer.
- **Psychopathy**: instinctive empathy is broken. Quotes from Damon's book and others:
  - Thirteen-year-old mugger of blind people: "What do I care? I'm not her."
  - Gary Gilmore: "I was always capable of murder. I can become totally devoid of feelings of others, unemotional. I know I'm doing something grossly… wrong. I can still go ahead and do it."
  - Ted Bundy: astonished at the fuss — "I mean, there are so many people."

**In-group / out-group**
- We care more about kin > friends > strangers; favor our groups in every way.
- Group memberships: men/women, Yale students, young, racial groups, etc.
- Even on-the-fly groups exert powerful moral control.

**Robber's Cave study (Sherif)**
- Subjects: well-adjusted, racially homogeneous, fairly rich 11–12-year-old boys at a camping program.
- Put in separate cabins, leaders assigned; boys named themselves "The Eagles" and "The Rattlers."
- Separation produced distinct cultures; competition increased within-group solidarity.
- Stereotypes: Eagles saw themselves as clean-living, no cuss words, respectful; viewed Rattlers as dirty, tough, slovenly. Rattlers viewed Eagles as goody-goody.
- Escalated to raids and violence (Eagles burnt Rattlers' banner; cuss words used). Sherif: "Excellent."
- Failed reconciliation attempts:
  - Peace talks: representatives ostracized as treasonists.
  - Individual Olympic-style competition: degenerated into Eagles vs. Rattlers.
  - Shared meals → food fights; shared movies → fights; shared firecracker fun nearly ended the experiment.
  - Religious sermons on brotherly love: kids respected them but interpreted "love thy neighbor" as loving fellow group members and not "those scummy Eagles."
- What worked: telling all kids the water line to the camp was cut and they had to defend it — a **superordinate goal** plus a common enemy.
- Bloom's aside: solution to bringing together warring countries/religions = an alien attack.

**Tajfel and minimal groups**
- Question: how little is needed to create a group?
- Klee vs. Kandinsky experiment: based on modern art preferences (assignments made up), people viewed in-group as more similar, smarter, and gave them more resources.
- Coin flip experiments produce the same effect (subtler): heads vs. tails groups favor own group.
- Implication: moral feelings are exquisitely attuned to group psychology, not just individuals.

**Student question** addressed: group experiments bear on morality because moral feelings (empathy, caring) are partially determined by group membership; obligations to in-group members are felt more strongly.

### Chapter 3. Moral Reasoning and Moral Judgments [00:23:07]

- **Moral judgment ≠ mere evaluation.** "I don't like strawberry ice cream" is a judgment but not moral — no obligation, no sanctions. "I don't like baby killers" carries:
  1. Obligation (we are obliged not to kill babies)
  2. Disagreement = wrong
  3. Sanctions (should be punished).
- Moral judgments happen constantly and often unconsciously: what to eat (moral vegetarians), charity, who to socialize with, how to treat homeless people, life goals, sex partners and conditions.
- Bloom's favorite dilemma: whether to make eye contact and greet, nod, or avoid someone you sort-of know on the street.

**Universals**
- Some moral intuitions appear everywhere on Earth, with weaker evidence of similar intuitions in young children and nonhuman primates (chimpanzees, capuchins, macaques): anger at cheaters, gratitude toward sharers, sense of right/wrong (consistent with prisoner's dilemma).

**Cross-cultural variation (Richard Shweder)**
> People have found it quite natural to be spontaneously appalled, outraged, indignant, proud, disgusted, guilty and ashamed by all sorts of things.

- Long list of variable triggers: masturbation, homosexuality, sexual abstinence, polygamy, abortion, circumcision, corporal punishment, capital punishment, Islam, Christianity, Judaism, capitalism, democracy, flag burning, miniskirts, long hair, no hair, parents/children sleeping in same bed (or not), women working (or not).
- Bloom notes baby killing and reciprocity (prisoner's dilemma) appear hard-wired and not culturally variable.

**Shweder's three ethics**

| Ethics | Core concepts |
|---|---|
| Autonomy | Rights, equality, freedom (Western moral philosophy default) |
| Community | Duty, status, hierarchy, interdependence |
| Divinity | Purity, sanctity, pollution, sin |

- Example: rights of men and women.
  - Autonomy: equal rights in all domains; restrictions need compelling argument involving others' freedom.
  - Community: different rights and duties; born to certain roles.
  - Divinity: religious injunctions (e.g., women not preparing food while menstruating; restrictions on who can have sex with whom rooted in pollution and sin).
- Western debates (abortion, hate speech) framed in autonomy terms.

**Jonathan Haidt's work (University of Virginia)**
- Even highly educated Westerners committed to autonomy show moral judgments not reducible to harm.
- The Julie and Mark scenario:
  > Julie and Mark are brother and sister. They are traveling together in France on summer vacation from college. One night they are staying alone in a cabin near the beach. They decide it would be interesting and fun if they tried making love. At the very least, it would be a new experience for each of them. Julie was already taking birth control pills but Mark uses a condom too just to be safe. They both enjoy making love but they decide not to do it again. They keep that night a special secret which makes them feel even closer to each other.
- Most people say it's wrong; when asked why, they exhibit **moral dumbfounding** — they cite a birth defect risk (refuted by birth control), underage status (refuted), and ultimately "it's just wrong."
- Other Haidt-style scenarios:
  - **Dead dog**: family dog hit by car; family cooks and eats it.
  - **Flag/toilet**: using the American flag to clean a dirty toilet.
  - **Frozen chicken**: a man buys a frozen chicken, has relations with it, then cooks and eats it.
- Among non-elite-college populations, the majority judge these as wrong; reasons offered (e.g., "disease") are post-hoc, since removing the consideration leaves the reaction.
- Conclusion: moral intuitions are governed by factors not consciously accessible.

### Chapter 4. Milgram's Work in the Context of Morality [00:38:48]

- Motivation: the Holocaust — why such a thing could happen.
- Milgram biography: Yale professor, denied tenure; moved to Harvard, denied tenure; reputation as "mad doctor"; ended at CUNY as full professor at age 33; died in his early 50s. Also originated "six degrees of separation."
- Setup recap (from "Obedience" movie): subject thinks they are teaching another in a memory task; the "learner" is a confederate; subject delivers escalating shocks from "slight shock" to "XXX"; learner protests; experimenter requests obedience.
- Majority deliver fatal shocks based on instructions.

**Bad explanations rejected**
- Subjects were not abnormal psychopaths (replicated broadly).
- Not sadism — subjects were acutely uncomfortable, embarrassed, conflicted, stressed; nobody enjoyed it.

**Follow-up findings — variables that reduce fatal shocks:**
- Moving experiment off-site reduces authority of Yale → fewer fatal shocks.
- Teacher in same room as learner → fewer fatal shocks.
- Teacher must place learner's hand on shock plate → fewer fatal shocks.
- Experimenter instructs by phone → fewer.
- Instructions from an ordinary man (not white-lab-coat) → fewer.
- Witnessing rebellion by another participant → much less likely to continue.
- If participants choose their own shock level → very few go all the way.

**Ethical critique**
- Milgram's study is why human-subjects committees exist; the study would not be approved today.
- 2% reported regret; most reported being happy to participate, but serious damage could have been done. Participants learned about themselves that they could kill on command — Bloom argues psychologists have no right to do that.

**Historical/sociological critique**
- Goldhagen argued that Nazi participants were not merely obedient but enthusiastic volunteers; obedience may not be the right model for genocide (controversial).

**Ingredients of the "perfect situation" to get someone to do a terrible thing**
- Authority (Yale + science: "the experiment must go on").
- A self-assured experimenter who accepts responsibility (would change if nervous/unsure).
- Distance between learner and experimenter (less effect when you must touch the victim).
- A novel situation with no behavioral model.
- Knowing about Milgram's setup arms you against it in real life.

### Chapter 5. Two Forces for Evil, Two Forces for Good [00:46:20]

**Two forces for evil**

1. **Deindividuation of self** — diffuse responsibility:
   - In groups (smashing windows alone vs. with 20 others).
   - Accepting orders ("I'm just an instrument").
   - Anonymity (face paint, masks; psychologically liberating).
   - Explains failure to help others. Diffusion of responsibility (Kitty Genovese case — murder in a courtyard surrounded by apartment buildings while dozens watched and did nothing).
   - Practical advice: don't yell "Help" in a crowd; point to a specific person — "You in the green sweater, call the police" — and they will.

2. **Denigration of others** — make the victim matter less:
   - Psychological or physical distance.
   - Dehumanizing language: "cargo" instead of people; "extermination" instead of murder.
   - Humor (makes target less of a person; ties in to later lecture on laughter).
   - Removing names. UN Declaration of Human Rights: "Every person has a right to a name." Names mark people as persons; once you know someone's name, you're less likely to hurt them.
   - Seeing them as disgusting.
- **Disgust (Paul Rozin)**: "the body and soul emotion"; human universal; basic emotion with a characteristic facial expression (cf. Paul Ekman's basic emotions); universally elicited by feces, urine, blood, vomit, rotten flesh, most meat.
- **Charles Darwin** anecdote on disgust extending to people: "a native touched with his finger some cold preserved meat and plainly showed disgust at its softness whilst I felt utter disgust at my food being touched by a naked savage though his hands did not appear dirty."
- **Martha Nussbaum**: "Thus, throughout history certain disgust properties have repeatedly and monotonously been associated with Jews, women, homosexuals, untouchables, lower class people. All of those are imagined as tainted by the dirt of the body."
- Genocidal movements with written records use disgust to dehumanize.

**Two forces for good**

1. **Contact and interdependence**
   - Extended selfish-gene logic — interconnection breeds care.
   - **Robert Wright**: "One of the many reasons I don't want to bomb the Japanese is that they built my minivan."
   - **Thomas Friedman's Golden Arches Theory of Human Conflict**: no two countries with McDonald's would go to war. Falsified by the NATO bombing of (Bloom recalls) Sarajevo, but the idea — interdependence promotes peace — is the spirit.
   - **Contact Hypothesis**: simple contact (especially equal status, common goal, social support) increases liking. Many studies on US blacks and whites show reduced animosity when brought together properly.
   - **Robber's Cave** — interconnection via shared goal then contact produced moral feeling.
   - **US military** as a successful case: integrated work toward a shared goal under institutional support, on equal footing — reduced racism.
   - Application to universities like Yale: debate over whether the conditions for positive contact are met (e.g., between blacks and whites, US South vs. North, US vs. international students); campus segregation debate.

2. **Perspective taking**
   - JFK on equal rights — invited white listeners to imagine themselves in the place of those facing discrimination:
     > If an American, because his skin is dark, cannot eat lunch in a restaurant open to the public — [and so on and so on and so on], then who among us would be content to have the color of his skin changed and stand in his place? Who among us would be content with the counsels of patience and delay?
   - Nussbaum on Greek dramas: invited audiences to take perspectives of those they would never meet — "empathetic expansion."
   - Bloom: "one of the greatest circles for moral good is storytelling."
   - **Direct ways**: ask "How would you feel if…" (used with children).
   - **Indirect ways (metaphor)**:
     - Fetus described as a "pre-born child" to recruit moral concern.
     - Animal described as human-like to elicit caring.
     - Calling unrelated strangers "brothers and sisters" — family metaphor used by political movements.
   - **Steven Spielberg** chose a cute child actor (not a faceless mechanical robot) to portray a sentient AI/robot to recruit moral concern.
