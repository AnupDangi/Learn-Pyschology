# PSYC 110 - Lecture 9 - Evolution, Emotion, and Reason: Love (Guest Lecture by Professor Peter Salovey)

Source: https://oyc.yale.edu/psychology/psyc-110/lecture-9

## Overview

This guest lecture, delivered on Valentine's Day by Dean Peter Salovey, examines love and, more centrally, the social psychology of attraction. Salovey opens by defining love through Robert Sternberg's three-component theory (intimacy, passion, commitment) and walks through the permutations that arise when only some components are present. He then surveys the social psychology of attraction, organized into the "big three" (proximity, similarity, familiarity) and the "more interesting four" (competence/Pratfall Effect, physical attractiveness, gain-loss effects, and misattribution of arousal). Salovey acknowledges that much of the research is older, sometimes ethically impossible to replicate today, and skewed toward androcentric, heterosexual dyads. He closes with the serious implications of misattributed arousal, including its possible role in why people remain in abusive relationships.

## Key Concepts & Definitions

- **Love (Sternberg's theory)**: Made up of three components — intimacy, passion, and commitment (decision/commitment). All three must be present for love.
- **Intimacy**: "The feeling of closeness, of connectedness with someone, of bonding." Operationally, sharing secrets/information not shared with others.
- **Passion**: "The drive that leads to romance"; physical attraction or sex.
- **Decision/Commitment**: "The decision that one is in a love relationship, the willingness to label it as such, and a commitment to maintain that relationship at least for some period of time."
- **Non-love**: No intimacy, no passion, no commitment.
- **Liking**: Intimacy only — typical casual friendships.
- **Infatuation / Infatuated love**: Passion only — "love at first sight."
- **Empty love**: Commitment only; often the final stage of long-term relationships gone bad, or the first stage in arranged marriages.
- **Romantic love**: Intimacy + passion, no commitment ("Romeo and Juliet when they first met").
- **Companionate love**: Intimacy + commitment, no passion — "your best friend."
- **Fatuous love**: Passion + commitment, no intimacy — "whirlwind courtship," Hollywood romance, shotgun wedding.
- **Consummate love**: All three components — Sternberg's complete love.
- **Pratfall Effect**: Our liking for a competent person grows when they make a mistake/blunder. Conversely, blunders make mediocre people seem even more mediocre.
- **Confederate ("stooge")**: A person in the employ of the experimenter who plays a role in the experiment.
- **Gain Effect (Aronson)**: We are attracted to people whose regard for us is gaining momentum over time — "the first derivative of their regard for us is positive."
- **Loss Effect**: People who really hurt us are those who were once positive and become negative — "you can only hurt the one you love."
- **Misattribution of arousal**: Feeling physiologically aroused without being sure why, and inventing an explanation — sometimes mistakenly labeling the arousal as love/attraction.

## Detailed Notes

### Chapter 1. Introduction to Dr. Peter Salovey

- Professor Paul Bloom introduces Dean Peter Salovey as the first guest lecturer.
- Salovey is described as Dean of Yale College, an active social psychologist studying health psychology, framing of health messages, and the founder/developer of the idea of emotional intelligence.
- Bloom notes Salovey once taught the largest course ever in Yale College — a course on Psychology in Law — and was a legendary Introduction to Psychology teacher.

### Chapter 2. Defining Love and Its Permutations

- Lecture is given on Valentine's Day; Salovey's main research area is human emotion (he doesn't personally study love in the lab).
- Caveats up front:
  - Some experiments described could not be done today (1950s–early 1970s ethics were different).
  - Much of the social psychological research on romantic love is androcentric and heterosexual — usually male participants, female targets.
  - Question to keep in mind: do these results generalize to other kinds of dyadic relationships?
- Most of the experiments are about **attraction**, not love.
- **Sternberg's theory of love** (Robert Sternberg, formerly Yale faculty ~30 years, then dean at Tufts): love = intimacy + passion + commitment.
  - Sternberg: not love if you don't call it love and don't desire to maintain the relationship.
- **Permutations** (Sternberg's terminology):

| Intimacy | Passion | Commitment | Type |
|---|---|---|---|
| – | – | – | Non-love |
| ✓ | – | – | Liking |
| – | ✓ | – | Infatuation / Infatuated love |
| – | – | ✓ | Empty love |
| ✓ | ✓ | – | Romantic love |
| ✓ | – | ✓ | Companionate love |
| – | ✓ | ✓ | Fatuous love |
| ✓ | ✓ | ✓ | Consummate love |

- **Empty love** is often the final stage of long-term relationships gone bad ("we'd better stay together for the kids…appearance's sake…financially"). It's also the first stage in arranged marriages.
- Arranged marriages do not seem to have a greater chance of ending in divorce than love marriages, but there is a major **confound**: arranged marriages tend to occur in societies that frown on divorce, making divorce socially costly.
- Reference: Meat Loaf's song "Two Out of Three Ain't Bad" used to introduce the two-element permutations.
- **Fatuous love** illustrated with: "Maybe you find yourself in Las Vegas and you get married for a day and a half and then realize that this wasn't such a good idea. And maybe your name is Britney and you're a singer."
- Suggested (humorous) homework: tally everyone you know on the three elements.

### Chapter 3. The Social Psychology of Love and Attraction

- The social psychology of love has essentially been a social psychology of attraction.
- **Seven variables**, grouped into the **"big three"** and the **"more interesting four"**.

#### The Big Three (all other things being equal)

1. **Proximity (close spatial proximity)**
   - Tested in New York City using city-block metrics: people who live more closely together are more likely to end up in romantic relationships.
   - On college campuses: negative correlation between feet from one's door to another's door and likelihood of a romantic relationship.
   - "It isn't the stranger you fall in love with."
2. **Similarity** — "Birds of a feather flock together."
   - On any measured dimension (height, age, attitudes toward capital punishment, Red Sox vs. Yankees preference), more similar = more attracted.
   - "Opposites attract" — not much evidence; "similarity breeds contempt" — not much evidence.
   - When students claim their partner is their opposite, Salovey responds: "Good luck."
3. **Familiarity**
   - We tend to fall in love with people in our environment with whom we're already familiar.
   - The cultural myth of "some enchanted evening you will see a stranger across a crowded room" (from "South Pacific") is just that — a myth.
   - Familiarity effect even works with **words in unknown languages**: briefly flashed unfamiliar characters are later rated as more likable than new ones, even when participants don't remember seeing them.

#### The More Interesting Four

##### 1. Competence and the Pratfall Effect

- We prefer competent people over incompetent ones, but **super-competent people are threatening** and make us feel diminished.
- We are most attracted to the **competent individual who occasionally blunders** — the **Pratfall Effect**.
- Example: **Bill Clinton's** popularity at the end of his term did not suffer much despite the Monica Lewinsky mistake — described in the media as "just shows he's human."
- **Classic Pratfall Experiment** (Elliot Aronson, UC Santa Cruz):
  - Subjects listen to audio interviews with possible "College Bowl" quiz-show representatives.
  - **Nearly perfect person**: answers 92% of questions correctly, modestly admits being in campus honor society, was editor of the yearbook, ran varsity track.
  - **Mediocre person**: answers 30% correctly, average grades, yearbook proofreader, tried out for track but didn't make it.
  - On attractiveness, the competent person is rated much more attractive.
  - **Blunder condition**: tape continues with clattering dishes and "Oh, my goodness. I've spilled coffee all over my new suit."
  - Result: the **competent person who blunders** is rated as *even more* attractive; the **mediocre person who blunders** is rated as *even more* mediocre.
- **Salovey's personal anecdote** — arriving at Yale in 1981 as a grad student, trying to persuade **Judy Rodin** (later President of the University of Pennsylvania, now President of the Rockefeller Foundation) to be his adviser. He spilled coffee on her desk and tried to invoke the Aronson experiment. Rodin replied: "Peter, Peter, Peter. You know that effect only works if I think you're competent first."

##### 2. Physical Attractiveness

- College students surveyed say warmth, sensitivity, intelligence, compassion, sense of humor matter — and that looks don't.
- Empirically, **looks predict who gets a second date** after a blind date, not warmth, sensitivity, intelligence, compassion, or sense of humor.
- Long-term relationships likely depend on more than looks; looks specifically predict the second-date outcome.
- **University of Minnesota study**: a computer algorithm (essentially random) paired students for a dance; those who rated their randomly assigned partner as attractive were more likely to continue the relationship.
- **Aronson's "Frizzy Wig" experiment**:
  - A confederate (a naturally attractive woman) was made to look either attractive or unattractive (frumpy clothes, bad make-up, frizzy wig).
  - She posed as a clinical psychology grad student interviewing male participants and giving each a personal clinical evaluation.
  - Conditions: attractive vs. unattractive × favorable vs. unfavorable feedback.
  - Results:
    - Attractive + favorable feedback → delighted.
    - Attractive + unfavorable feedback → very upset; often sought future interaction to disprove her evaluation.
    - Unattractive (either feedback) → didn't really care.
  - Interpretation: feedback from attractive people has more impact on us.

##### 3. Gain and Loss

- General principle: we are wired to be more sensitive to **change** than to steady states (change signals danger or opportunity — survival/reproductive value).
- **Gain Effect (Aronson)**: more powerful than steady positive regard is regard that **starts lower and rises** over time. "The first derivative of their regard for us is positive."
  - Even if the average regard is lower than someone who was always positive, the rising trajectory captures attention.
- **Loss Effect**: people who have always been negative cannot hurt us much; the **person who was positive and becomes negative** is who hurts us most.
- Salovey references a Ricky Skaggs country song line: "Nothing can hurt you like the person you love."
- "You can only hurt the one you love because you are expecting positive feedback from the one you love."

### Chapter 4. Misattribution for the Causes of Arousal

- Connects to Schachter & Singer's emotion work (referenced in passing).
- **Misattribution of arousal**: feeling physiologically aroused, being unsure why, and constructing an explanation — sometimes wrongly labeling it love.
- **Thought experiment (Yale/Starbucks)**: A Pierson student takes a friend to a New Blue concert and then to Starbucks. The friend orders a "decaf double espresso mocha skinny frothed," but the barista mistakenly uses caffeinated espresso. Walking back, her heart pounds, palms sweat, breath shortens — she misattributes the caffeine arousal to love. Quote: "What a day this has been. What a rare mood I'm in. Why, it's almost like being in love."
- She turns to "the most salient object in her immediate social environment" — the narrator — and concludes she's in love.

#### The "Rickety Bridge" Field Experiment (University of British Columbia)

- Two bridges across a river on campus: a **rickety rope bridge** hundreds of feet above the water (sways in the breeze, ~3 feet wide) and a **low solid wood bridge** near the water.
- An attractive female confederate intercepted male students crossing each bridge, asked questions, requested a short story, and gave her phone number in case of questions.
- Results:
  - Men on the **rickety bridge** wrote sexier, more ribald stories and were more likely to call her later.
  - Men on the **low bridge** wrote boring stories and were less likely to call.
- Interpretation: misattributed arousal — fear/heart pounding from the rickety bridge gets attributed to attraction.
- **Methodological flaw**: no random assignment to bridges → not a true experiment. People who choose the rickety bridge may be more adventure-seeking by disposition.

#### Lab Experiments on Misattributed Arousal

- **Painful shock instruction**: Subjects told "we'll begin the painful shock experiment in a moment" rate the experimenter as **more attractive** than those given a neutral instruction — even though the source of the arousal (fear of shock) is obvious.
- **Treadmill experiment**: Subjects asked to run 10 minutes on a treadmill before rating the experimenter find the experimenter **more attractive** than the control group who just sat — even though the arousal source (exercise) is obvious. Salovey jokes the "fourth floor of Payne Whitney Gym" is dangerous because of "that combination of aerobic exercise and spandex."
- **Playboy centerfold / fake heartbeat experiment**:
  - Male subjects view ~10 Playboy centerfolds while wearing headphones that supposedly amplify their heartbeat.
  - In reality, they hear a **tape recording** of a heartbeat that the experimenter speeds up at random intervals.
  - Subjects rate the slide that coincided with the sped-up heartbeat as the most attractive.
  - Shows that even **phony arousal** can be misattributed.

#### Serious Implications: Domestic Violence

- **Number one reason** people stay in abusive relationships: economic dependence or perceived lack of alternatives (homelessness, starvation, danger to kids).
- Additional factor: sometimes people misattribute the arousal of fights, screaming, name-calling, or violence as love — "Well, he wouldn't be yelling and screaming at me if he didn't love me."
- Salovey relates a story from a social psychologist friend: 30 years ago, while dating her now-husband, during a parking-lot argument he punched out her car window. She told Salovey, "That's when I knew he really loved me." Salovey calls this misattributed arousal — misattributing his aggression as love and her own fear as mutual attraction.

### Chapter 5. Question and Answer

- **Q: If subjects are told in advance about painful shock (via consent form), does the misattribution effect still occur?**
  - A: The more salient the actual source of arousal, the less likely the misattribution. But the effect can still occur even when the cause is somewhat obvious.
- **Q: Do the big three (proximity, familiarity, similarity) affect maintenance of relationships or just initial attraction?**
  - A: Probably both, but the literature focuses on initial attraction; following couples over time creates "Heisenberg-esque" problems (observation interferes with the relationship). Most of these experiments are really about initial attraction.
- **Q: Can someone feel consummate love for more than one person?**
  - A: Debated in the literature. From an **evolutionary perspective**, feeling that kind of love for more than one person could facilitate passing genes to more of the next generation. From a **socially constructed perspective**, most societies (except very unusual polygamist societies) hold that you cannot love more than one. Salovey's guess: yes, you could, but you would be conflicted because the evolutionary impulse and social constraints clash.
- **Q: Wouldn't natural selection favor people who learn this material and apply it?**
  - A: This feels like passing on an acquired characteristic (counter to Darwinian theory), but if there were a heritable proclivity for learning such material, evolution might favor it. It would "make the several thousand social psychologists in this world very happy."
