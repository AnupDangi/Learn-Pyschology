# PSYC 110 - Lecture 20 - The Good Life: Happiness

Source: https://oyc.yale.edu/psychology/psyc-110/lecture-20

## Overview

This final lecture wraps up the prior discussion of clinical psychology by examining whether therapy works, then turns to the emerging field of positive psychology and the science of happiness. Professor Bloom argues that therapy in general does work, partly because of "nonspecific factors" like support and hope, and that different disorders respond best to different therapies. He then surveys happiness research, noting that most people rate themselves as happy, but that simple self-report measures can be skewed by trivial circumstances. Three surprising findings frame the happiness discussion: happiness has a strong heritable set point and people adapt to most events ("hedonic treadmill"); happiness is relative to others' status and salary; and memories of past pleasure or pain are skewed toward peaks and endings (Kahneman). Bloom closes with humility about open questions in psychology and optimism about its scientific methods.

## Key Concepts & Definitions

- **Regression to the mean**: A statistical phenomenon where extreme states tend to trend back toward an average over time; cited as a reason people may falsely attribute improvement to therapy.
- **Control group**: A comparison group (e.g., waiting-list patients) used to test whether treatment has a real effect.
- **Nonspecific factors**: Properties shared by virtually all therapies — e.g., support and hope — that contribute to therapeutic benefit independent of the specific method.
- **Placebo effect**: Benefit that comes from belief that something is being done to help, rather than from a specific therapeutic mechanism; can be a self-fulfilling prophecy.
- **Positive psychology**: A movement focused on human flourishing, psychological strengths, and virtues — taking people who are okay and making them better — rather than only treating the broken.
- **DSM-IV (The Diagnostic and Statistical Manual of Mental Disorders)**: Reference catalog of mental disorders.
- **The Handbook of Positive Psychology**: Reference work listing psychological strengths and virtues.
- **Lake Wobegon effect (with happiness)**: Most people rate themselves as happier than average — statistically improbable.
- **Affective forecasting**: Predicting how happy or sad we will be in the future based on what's happening to us; people are bad at it (Dan Gilbert's work).
- **Set point (happiness)**: A genetically influenced natural happiness level or range to which people return.
- **Hedonic treadmill**: Adaptation to good and bad events such that they lose their effect on happiness — "you keep on running but no matter how fast you run you stay where you are."
- **Peak-end rule (Kahneman)**: Memory of pleasure/pain is highly skewed toward peaks and endings rather than total amount.

### Chapter 1. How and Why Therapy Works

- The lecture finishes the clinical psychology discussion begun previously (which had ended with the gruesome, unsuccessful, arbitrary history of treating mental illness). Dr. Nolen-Hoeksema had reviewed therapies for depression; the textbook covers schizophrenia, anxiety disorders, etc.
- **Does therapy work?** Hard to tell because self-report of improvement may be due to **regression to the mean**.
  - Mood plots up and down around an average; extremes trend back to average regardless of intervention.
  - People go to therapy when feeling unusually bad, so subsequent improvement is statistically likely even without effect.
  - Bloom's illustration: "On the worst day of your life you could do naked jumping jacks on the roof of your college for ten minutes. I guarantee you your next day would probably be better."
- **Proper test**: compare to a control group of comparable people who don't receive treatment.
  - Example study: depressed patients statistically equal at pre-therapy; some randomly assigned to cognitive training, others to waiting list. Cognitive training group had lower depression scores.
- **General conclusions:**
  - Therapy by and large works; treated people do better than untreated, not merely because they sought help.
  - Different therapies suit different problems:
    - **Everyday unipolar depression** → cognitive behavioral therapy + possibly SSRIs.
    - **Bipolar depression** → CBT useless; medication is best bet.
    - **Anxiety disorder** → CBT can help.
    - **Schizophrenia** → CBT not of much help.
  - **Therapist quality varies** — large individual differences in therapist efficacy for reasons not fully understood.
- **Nonspecific factors** (why therapy generally works):
  - **Support**: Acceptance, empathy, encouragement, guidance, human touch — someone who really cares about you.
  - **Hope**: Enthusiasm/belief that treatment may work; can become a self-fulfilling prophecy.
  - **Placebo effect** is often dismissed but powerful — even if the therapist "runs around and dances," belief alone can produce benefit.

### Chapter 2. Question and Answer on Therapy

- **Q: Isn't regression to the mean arbitrary?**
  - A: It assumes most people have a stable average and fluctuate around it; this holds for things like mood. Statistically, after a bad day you trend up; after the happiest day, you trend down. Human behavior isn't a random walk — we seem to have set points.
- **Q: Could the control group (waiting list) experience anxiety from being told they're not chosen?**
  - A: Yes, a plausible confound. Other studies use designs where the control group doesn't know they are controls (e.g., low-scoring depression-inventory students intervened upon while others are unaware). Multiple methods are needed.

### Chapter 3. Happiness and Positive Psychology

- Most psychology focuses on human misery — clinical interventions aimed at bringing broken/sad people up to normal.
- **DSM-IV** catalogs everything that can go wrong, "from Aspergers syndrome to fetishes to paranoid schizophrenia." Browsing it can make one feel mentally unstable.
- **Positive psychology**: Movement asking how psychology can give insight into flourishing, strengths, and virtues. Has its own **Handbook of Positive Psychology**.
  - Some of the work is "real crap" — new age banalities tied to grant funds and Time magazine; attracts self-help hucksters.
  - Other work is "quite neat, quite interesting and quite promising."
- **Recommended books on happiness:**
  - Marty Seligman, *Authentic Happiness* — Seligman is the pioneer of positive psychology.
  - Jonathan Haidt (brilliant young scholar; also disgust/morality, the "sex with dead chicken study") — happiness work.
  - Nettle, *Happiness* — smart, beautifully written, extremely short.
  - Dan Gilbert, *Stumbling on Happiness* — funny, smart, on NYT bestseller list.
- **How happy are you (1 to 10, 5 = average)?**
  - Most common answers are high — 7 or 8.
  - **Lake Wobegon effect**: most people think they're happier than most people.
- **Cross-national data** (study of 42 countries):
  - No country reported being unhappy on average.
  - **Happiest**: the Swiss. (One suggestion offered: chocolate.)
  - **Saddest**: the Bulgarians.
  - **Americans**: 7.71 — pretty happy.
- **Reasons for caution about these numbers:**
  - **Dime-on-photocopier study**: People asked "How happy are you with your entire life?" right after using a copy machine reported greater life satisfaction when a dime had been planted on top.
  - **Sunny day study**: Phone interviews found people reported greater life happiness on sunny days — but the effect disappeared if "How's the weather?" was asked first, prompting them to discount weather as the source.

### Chapter 4. Getting Used to Happiness

- **What is happiness for?** An evolutionary answer: happiness is a goal state we evolved to pursue, a signal that needs have been satisfied. Like food/satiation.
- **Steven Pinker's evocative summary**: "We are happier when we are healthy, well-fed, comfortable, safe, prosperous, knowledgeable, respected, non-celibate, in love."
- **The puzzle**: Modern Americans are healthier and richer than any prior generation but no happier.
  - People in the 1950s — poorer, sicker, shorter-lived — are/were as happy as people today.
  - Parents and grandparents were as happy as current students.
  - Poor countries: little effect of national wealth on happiness.
  - Large individual differences in happiness even among those whose basic needs are met.

**Three surprising findings about happiness:**

#### 1. Happiness doesn't change as much as you think.

- Strong **heritable basis** — a genetically determined set point or range.
- Identical twins are very similar in happiness, but not identical.
- **Life events have surprisingly small lasting effects:**
  - **Paralysis from the neck down**: Initially devastating but happiness returns roughly to baseline after about a year.
  - **Lottery winners (many millions of dollars)**: Initially elated but not as happy a year later. Can even backfire — wrenches winners from family, work, friends, leading to depression.
  - **Tenure (Dan Gilbert's research)**: Assistant professors overestimate the happiness boost from getting it and the misery from being denied.
  - **Pre-2004 election (Bush vs. Kerry)**: People radically overestimated how much the outcome would affect their happiness.
  - **Consumer goods (Xbox 360, flat screen TV)**: Joy fades almost immediately.
- **Affective forecasting** (Dan Gilbert): we are bad at predicting future feelings.
  - **Day-to-day irrelevance**: Most of daily life isn't spent thinking about who's president, prizes won, etc. Bloom: even after winning Nobel + Guggenheim + MacArthur in one day, a month later "the plumber's not coming and my kids don't respect me."
  - **Adaptation / set point logic**: We get used to good and bad things alike.
- **Exceptions to adaptation:**
  - **Noise**: We don't habituate to ongoing noise (e.g., construction); happiness drops and stays down.
  - **Cosmetic surgery (breast enhancement/reduction)**: Makes people happier and they stay happier — explanation: appearance matters constantly and you don't get used to looking different.
- **Hedonic treadmill**: keep running, stay in place; habituate like to a hot or cold bath.
- **Biblical illustration (Ecclesiastes)**: A king with gardens, parks, vineyards, castles, slaves, and male and female concubines yet says: "I hated life. All this vanity and a chasing after wind and there is nothing to be gained under the sun."
- **Advice from happiness books on dealing with the treadmill:**
  - Possessions are not the key — we adapt quickly.
  - **Endless novelty** strategy: keep doing new things (sex with someone new, climb Everest, become accountant, scuba dive...).
  - **"Old guy" alternative**: step off the treadmill — seek substantial goods like friends, family, long-term projects, rather than quick fixes of delight.

#### 2. Happiness is relative.

- National wealth matters little once above starvation, but **personal relative salary** matters.
- **H.L. Mencken**: "A wealthy man is one who earns a hundred dollars more than his wife's sister's husband."
- Experimental preference: People prefer **$70,000 when others make $65,000** over **$75,000 when others make $80,000** — relative position wins.
- **British civil servants research**: happiness, health, relationship quality depend on relative position, not absolute salary.
- Humans are status-conscious primates; hierarchy level affects happiness.
- **Maria Callas and Stanley Fish anecdote**: Fish, on being hired, reportedly said he didn't want to talk salary — he just wanted $100 more than the top person in the department.
- **Q on solitary confinement/prison and habituation to social losses**:
  - Hard initially due to shifts in status and social relationships.
  - But if you become popular within the new setting (e.g., head of the prison book club), a cheerful person tends to remain cheerful.

**Advice to the king (Ecclesiastes):**
- Mild unipolar depression → SSRIs + CBT.
- Move castle to a quiet part of the kingdom (noise stress).
- Give up concubines; find a queen.
- Develop social relationships, join a club, get involved in charity, take up a hobby.

#### 3. Memory of pleasure and pain is skewed (Daniel Kahneman).

- Kahneman: one of the few living psychologists with a Nobel prize; previously discussed under rationality research.
- **Dental torture thought experiment** (after the movie *Marathon Man*):
  - **A**: an hour of severe pain.
  - **B**: an hour of severe pain + a few additional minutes of mild pain.
  - Logically A is less painful in total, but **B leaves a less bad memory**.
- **Peak-end principle**: Remembered experience is skewed to peaks and endings, not total amount.
- **Empirical work**: Kahneman tested this in the lab (cold-water immersion) and clinically with patients undergoing extremely painful colonoscopy procedures.
- **Implication**: Topping a painful event off with mild pain gives a less bad memory.
- **Party example**:
  - **A**: 90% great, last 10% someone slaps you and pours dip on you.
  - **B**: 90% slapping/dip, last 10% great.
  - Simple summing favors A; memory favors B because endings matter.

**Two closing themes:**

- **Humility**: Many basic questions about the mind remain unanswered.
  - We know the brain produces mental life but not how a "lump of meat" produces conscious experience.
  - About half of personality variance is genetic; the other half is experiential — but we lack good theories of which experiences make one adventurous, timid, bitter, or satisfied.
  - We understand social influences toward evil but not why some are immune and act heroically.
- **Optimism**: Scientific methods — evolutionary, neurological, developmental, computational; experimental and observational testing — will continue to make progress on questions about beliefs, emotions, decisions, even moral sense.
- Pushback on the worry that scientific study diminishes humanity's specialness: serious scientific study deepens appreciation of the mind's complexity, uniqueness, and beauty.
