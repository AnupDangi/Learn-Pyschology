# PSYC 110 - Lecture 17 - A Person in the World of People: Self and Other, Part II; Some Mysteries: Sleep, Dreams, and Laughter

Source: https://oyc.yale.edu/psychology/psyc-110/lecture-17

## Overview

This lecture by Professor Paul Bloom continues the discussion of social psychology and then turns to two "mysteries": sleep/dreams and laughter. In the social psychology portion, Bloom examines how we form impressions of others — focusing on the power of first impressions, "thin slices," self-fulfilling prophecies (the Pygmalion effect), and the operation of stereotypes at public, private, and unconscious levels. He argues stereotypes are a normal cognitive tool for generalizing about categories but become problematic through confirmation bias, biased media data, moral concerns about profiling, and effects like stereotype threat. In the second half, Bloom treats sleep as a "minor mystery" with reasonable explanations (restoration, energy conservation), dreams as a "greater mystery" partly explained by memory consolidation, and laughter as a "true mystery" rooted in mock aggression, group cohesion, and surprise.

## Key Concepts & Definitions

- **Confirmation bias**: Once you believe something, other information is encoded along lines that support what you believe.
- **Thin slices**: Brief exposures (as little as five seconds, or about one second for sexual orientation) are sufficient to form fairly accurate impressions of others.
- **Self-fulfilling prophecy / Pygmalion effect**: If I believe you have a certain characteristic, this might cause you to behave as if you have that characteristic.
- **Stereotypes**: Information we have about categories and intuitions about the typicality/frequency of certain features of categories (psychologists' broader usage).
- **Profiling**: Applying stereotypes to people in day-to-day life — viewed as morally problematic even when the stereotype is accurate.
- **Stereotype threat (Claude Steele)**: If your group has a negative stereotype in a domain, being reminded of your group membership immediately before performing damages performance in that domain.
- **Public / Private / Unconscious stereotypes**: Three levels — what you publicly say, what you privately believe, and unconscious associations you may not know about.
- **Implicit Attitudes Test (IAT)**: Developed by Mahzarin Banaji; measures speed differences (congruent vs. incongruent pairings) to reveal implicit attitudes.
- **Automaticity theory (Trish Devine)**: Everyone holds stereotypes; these are automatically activated on contact; non-stereotyped action requires conscious effort to override.
- **EEG**: Method for studying sleep by placing electrodes on the scalp.
- **Sleep stages**: Stage I (transition), II–IV (successively deeper, with slow, irregular, high-amplitude delta waves), then back up through III and II, then REM.
- **Slow-wave (quiet) sleep**: Eyes drift slowly/separately; hard to wake.
- **REM sleep**: Rapid eye movement; brain looks awake; body paralyzed except eyes; men get erections; dreams occur.
- **Real dreams vs. sleep thought**: Real dreams are bizarre/narrative (REM); sleep thought is rumination during slow-wave sleep.
- **Lucid dreams**: Dreams in which you choose what to dream about.
- **Tickling**: Touching parts of the body in a mock attack.

### First and Fast: How We Form Impressions of Others

**First impressions matter a lot:**
- Driven in part by **confirmation bias**: once you believe something, new information gets encoded to support it.
- **Kelley's classic study**: a guest speaker was introduced to students with bios describing him as either "very warm" or "rather cold." Later impressions were heavily biased by the initial description.
- Examples of framing effects on interpretation:
  - Described as "vivacious and creative" → bouncing around confirms it.
  - Described as "drinks too much" → same behavior reads as alcoholism.
  - Described as "insecure and nervous" → activity reads as nervous twitches.
- Film example: **Being There** (Peter Sellers) — character **Chauncey Gardner** is mildly retarded but reputed a genius; people interpret simple statements ("Well, I like being in the garden") as profound.
- **Washington snipers** example: initial (false) reports of a white van caused everyone to keep seeing white vans in subsequent incidents.

**Thin slices:**
- Literature showing impressions form very quickly.
- **Rosenthal and Ambady**: showed clips of university professors; after just **five seconds**, observers could accurately predict teaching evaluations.
- Brief exposure also lets people accurately rate others on **"The Big Five"** dimensions of personality.
- **"Gaydar" study** (same psychologists): people did not know sexual orientation was at issue; orientation was one item on a long form; interviews were filmed; viewers could detect sexual orientation in about **one second**. Everybody is better than chance; gay people are better than straight people; accuracy is only a bit better than chance.
- Combined implication: brief exposures shape much of how we think about people going forward.

**Self-fulfilling prophecies / Pygmalion effect:**
- Term from George Bernard Shaw's **Pygmalion**: "The difference between a lady and a flower girl is not how she behaves but how she's treated. I shall always be a flower girl to Professor Higgins because he always treats me as a flower girl and always will."
- Made into **My Fair Lady**; Bloom prefers **La Femme Nikita** as illustration.
- **Rosenthal and Jackson** classic experiment: teachers were told (randomly) which students would show an IQ "spurt." Those children did, in fact, show IQ jumps — because teachers gave them more focus and attention.

### Positive Uses and Negative Effects of Stereotypes

**Stereotypes are necessary cognition:**
- Used in a broader sense by psychologists: information about categories and typicality.
- Essential for survival: we must make generalizations about novel things (chair → can sit on; apple → edible; dog → barks, has tail, doesn't speak English).
- Generalizations are typically (not logically) true: a chair could be explosive, an apple poisoned, a dog vegetarian — but generally these stereotypes work.

**Stereotypes about people:**
- We form stereotypes about men/women, children/adolescents/adults, whites/blacks/Asians, etc.
- Not inherently bad:
  - Some are positive (about creativity, intelligence, your own group).
  - Often accurate — e.g., people correctly answer who is more likely to be a lawyer (Jewish vs. Hispanic) or taller (Sweden vs. Japan).

**Problems with stereotypes:**

1. **Confirmation bias inaccuracy:**
   - If you believe gay men are effeminate, effeminate gay men confirm it; non-effeminate ones get dismissed.
   - If you believe black men are criminals, you weight supporting cases more heavily.
2. **Unreliable data:**
   - Classical music: women stereotyped as less proficient. **Blind auditions** (behind a screen) led to far more women being hired — stereotype was incorrect and had real negative effects.
3. **Misleading media data:**
   - Media (TV, movies, plays, books) skews perceptions.
   - Italian Americans portrayed as mobsters (e.g., **The Sopranos**).
   - Jewish portrayals — e.g., **Shylock in Merchant of Venice**.
   - Counter-portrayals: **Battlestar Galactica** — character "Starbuck" (male in the original) was transformed into a woman in the remake.
4. **Moral problems / profiling:**
   - It's fine to stereotype chairs, apples, dogs, even dog breeds (e.g., greyhound vs. pit bull). No one calls you a "dog racist."
   - Applying stereotypes to people = profiling, considered immoral even when stereotypes are accurate.
   - But some stereotypes are accepted (e.g., higher auto insurance for young drivers due to accident rates with "reefer and alcohol"); charging Asians more than Europeans would be considered unacceptable.
5. **Stereotype threat (Claude Steele, Stanford):**
   - Reminding members of a group of a negative stereotype before a task (e.g., math test) drops their performance.
   - Works for black students on math; works for women on math too.

**Three levels of stereotypes:**

| Level | Description |
|---|---|
| **Public** | What you say publicly; few would say a black or female candidate should be disqualified for president. |
| **Private** | What you really think but don't tell people. |
| **Unconscious** | Implicit associations the person isn't aware of but which affect thoughts about race, gender, etc. |

**Data on public stereotypes:**
- Proportion willing to vote for an African American president was about **half** around the time Bloom was born; now near 100%.
- White respondents endorsing negative traits of blacks is now infinitesimal.

**Subliminal priming study:**
- Subjects complete word fragments (e.g., "hos-") while subliminal black or white faces flash.
- Black face primes → more likely to complete as "hostile."
- White face primes → more likely to complete as "hospital."

### Implicit Attitudes

- **Implicit Attitudes Test (IAT)** developed by **Mahzarin Banaji** (formerly Yale, now Harvard).
- Largest psychology experiment ever — about a million participants; available at **implicit.harvard.edu**.
- Class demonstration:
  - **Congruent** trial: African American or bad word → "right"; white American or good word → "left."
  - **Incongruent** trial: white American or bad word → "right"; African American or good word → "left."
  - Across many trials, people are slower on the incongruent version, suggesting biased associations.
- Extended to: gender (women–English, men–math), age, obesity, sexual orientation.

**Why care about a half-second difference?**
1. **Police shooting studies**: split-second decisions about whether to shoot someone holding an unclear object are heavily affected by stereotypical attitudes.
2. **Judgment calls**: subtle effects on real decisions.

**Bystander + race study:**
- A scream is heard from a black or white person; subject is either alone or with others.
- Alone: nearly everyone helps regardless of race.
- With others: big difference by race of victim.
- Subjects don't say "I didn't help because the person is black"; they say "someone else would help."

**Jack Dovidio hiring study (Yale colleague):**
- Subjects judge hiring based on recommendations.
- Strong recommendations (1989): both African Americans and white Americans get hired at similar rates.
- So-so recommendations: white Americans hired significantly more often than African Americans.
- Same pattern in **1989, 1999, and about a year and a half ago**.

**Trish Devine's automaticity theory:**
- Everyone holds stereotypes.
- They are automatically activated on contact with individuals.
- Overriding requires conscious effort, at both individual and group level.

### Question and Answer on Stereotypes

- **Q (paraphrased): What can be done?**
  - **Group-level interventions:**
    - In psychology department job searches, deliberately surface names from disadvantaged groups (not affirmative action, but counteracting the bias by which qualified people don't come to mind).
    - **Blind reviewing** in journals (because of evidence that male vs. female names affect evaluations).
    - **Blind auditions** as a classic example.
  - **Individual level:** harder — but be conscious of biases, recognize "I'm explicitly not racist" isn't enough, and try to set up systems where qualifications are observed absent race/sex info.
- **Q (paraphrased): Stereotypes vs. own-group bias?**
  - Both play a role.
  - Stereotypes operate above and beyond in-group favoritism — evidence: African Americans also show the same IAT bias against African Americans and in favor of white Americans.

### The Minor Mystery of Sleep

- Sleep is a **motivation** (like food or drink); depriving someone of sleep is a form of torture.
- Big individual differences in how much sleep people need.
- "Sleep macho" used to mean very little sleep; now means a lot.

**Studying sleep — EEG:**
- Electrodes on scalp record brain electrical activity.
- Wakeful attention: irregular **beta waves**.
- Awake but non-attentive: large, regular **alpha waves**.
- Sound asleep: deep **delta**.

**Sleep stages:**
- **Stage I**: transition while falling asleep.
- **Stages II–IV**: successively deeper; slow, irregular, high-amplitude **delta waves**.
- After Stage IV, you ascend back through III, II.
- **REM sleep** emerges: brain looks wide awake, body relaxed, rapid eye movements, dreams occur.
- A good night = **four to five sleep cycles** alternating non-REM and REM.

**Two types of sleep:**

| Type | Features |
|---|---|
| **Slow-wave / quiet sleep** | Eyes drift separately and slowly; hard to wake up. |
| **REM sleep** | EEG similar to waking; body paralyzed except eyes; men get erections; dreams. |

- Sleep-researcher joke (which Bloom told the class not to write down): dreams fly through the ether and erections serve as antennas.

**Why do we sleep?**
- Body is worn out; sleep restores it.
- Growth-producing hormone is released; brain and organs get restored; less food needed; immune system active.
- If you stop sleeping, you don't die dramatically — you get sick and then die.
- Analogy: like turning off a car's engine to repair it.
- Related view: sleep evolved to preserve energy and keep us out of trouble at night. The two views are not incompatible.

**Sleep disorders:**
- Bloom briefly lists them.
- **Ambien side effects**: some users go downstairs, open the refrigerator, and eat huge amounts of food while asleep; a more recent and serious side effect is sleep-driving.

### The Greater Mystery of Dreams

- Hamlet on dreams: deciding whether to kill himself; reasons for: "the slings and arrows of outrageous fortune," "a sea of troubles," "the heartache in the thousand natural shocks that flesh is heir to." Only reason against: **nightmares** — "To sleep, perchance to dream..."
- Philosophical questions: Are you dreaming right now? (Descartes; see also **The Matrix**.) Can you be immoral in a dream? (Augustine.)
- Bloom's answer: you are not dreaming now; you usually can't be immoral in a dream. Exception: **lucid dreams**, where you choose what to dream — possibly immoral if it encourages bad habits of thought.

**Real dreams vs. sleep thought:**
- **Real dreams** (REM): e.g., "in a submarine wrestling a chicken while your grandmother looks disapprovingly on."
- **Sleep thought** (slow-wave): rumination, e.g., "did I take the garbage out?"

**Facts about dreams:**
- Everyone dreams (3–4 times a night, depending on sleep duration).
- Not everyone remembers them; dreams leave fragile memories that fade quickly.
- **Dream diary** recommended — write dreams down upon waking.

**Dream content (from dream reports, e.g., Hill's 50,000-report collection at dreambank.net):**
- Most dreams are mildly negative (on 1–10 scale, below 5.5).
- Report misfortunes.
- **Tribal societies** > industrialized societies in physical aggression.
- **Men** > women in aggressive dreams.
- **Americans** > Europeans in aggressive dreams.

**What people want to dream about:**
- Women: romance and adventure.
- Men: sex with strangers.
- After adolescence, about **10% of dreams have explicit sexual content**.

**Most common dream:**
- Not falling, not flying, not public speaking, not naked in public.
- **Being chased** — a "primal" puzzle for evolutionary psychologists and cognitive neuroscientists.

**Theories of dreams:**
- Freudian (disguised wish-fulfillment): not much support.
- Popular theory: dreams are a **side effect of memory consolidation**. Memories get played over to consolidate them across brain regions — like backing up a computer. Random events flash to consciousness and get woven into a story.
- On this view, dreams serve **no adaptive function**; they are **epiphenomena** — byproducts of another system.

### The True Mystery of Laughter

- Bloom got interested through watching his own son **Zachary** laugh.
- A baby-laughing clip from the web shown as another example.
- From an evolutionary standpoint, the puzzle: why make this weird noise in bizarre circumstances?

**A bad neuroscience-textbook explanation:**
> "We laugh when there is incongruity between what we expect and what actually happens unless the outcome is frightening."
- Bloom: about as wrong as anything can be.
  - Doesn't explain why incongruity should cause a loud, distinctive noise of staccato segments (each ~1/15 sec separated by ~1/5 sec).
  - A bowl of fruit appearing would be incongruous; nobody shrieks with laughter at it.
  - Lots of laughter isn't caused by incongruity.

**Robert Provine's descriptive work (book: Laughter):**
- Sent himself and students to places like shopping malls to record what made people laugh.
- Recorded over **1,000 laughter-initiated situations**.
- Most things that triggered laughter were not jokes or humor.
- Sample laughter-initiating lines:
  - "I'll see you guys later."
  - "Look. It's Andre."
  - "Are you sure?"
  - "I know."
  - "How are you?"
  - "I try to lead a normal life."
  - "It wasn't you."
  - "We can handle this."
- Only ~10% of comments could later be re-coded as actually humorous, e.g.:
  - "Poor boy looks just like his father."
  - "You smell like you've had a good workout."
  - "Did you find that in your nose?"
  - Dorm food references.
  - "He has a job pulling back skin in the operating room."

**Properties of laughter:**
- **Universal**: every society has it.
- **Early-emerging**: infants do it.
- **Social/communicative**: it's loud — loud noises evolve to communicate.
- **Involuntary noisemaking** and **contagious**:
  - **Laugh track** invention made TV/movies funnier.
  - **Rim shot** is a pre-technological attempt to simulate laughter.
  - Children especially affected by contagious laughter.
- **Other primates**: monkeys laugh when they attack (gathering to kill/eat); chimpanzees laugh when they tickle each other.

**Tickling:**
- Touching parts of the body in a **mock attack**.
- Has to be recognized as mock; being mugged is not funny.

**Theories — laughter as mock/collective aggression:**
- Laughter often signals teasing, kidding, insults, mock attack — aggression that is attenuated and not real. Laughter signals it isn't real.
- Collective aggression: mob assaults, executions, lynchings, rapes are accompanied by laughter — signaling group solidarity.
- **Plato**: laughter as bonding against a common enemy; sound of group cohesion against a common enemy.

**Comedians on aggression in laughter:**
- **Dave Barry**: "The most important humor truth of all is that to really see the humor in a situation you have to have perspective. Perspective is derived from two ancient Greek words, 'pers' meaning something bad that happens to someone else and 'ective' meaning ideally somebody like Donald Trump."
- **Mel Brooks**: "Tragedy is when I cut my finger. Comedy is when you fall in an open sewer and die."

**Ingredients of humor:**
- **A target who experiences some harm** — enemy, friend, or self.
- **Harm not so severe** that it elicits fear, grief, or pity. A stranger slipping on a banana peel is funny; splitting his head open with blood is not (unless you dislike him).
- Damage often involves **embarrassment, sex, scatology, banana peel, pie in the face, pants falling** — where empathy doesn't kick in.
- Aggression is unleashed at someone.
- **Surprise** must be present — this is what made the baby laugh (unpredictable sound), and what made Zachary laugh at **Winnie the Pooh and the Blustery Day** (Rabbit climbs a shelf, it crashes down, he's stunned but not dead).

**Why can't we tickle ourselves?**
- No surprise, no mock aggression, and likely a general deadening of self-stimulation.
- Some people claim they can — Bloom has heard this multiple times.
