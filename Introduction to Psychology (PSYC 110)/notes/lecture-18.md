# PSYC 110 - Lecture 18 - What Happens When Things Go Wrong: Mental Illness, Part I (Guest Lecture by Professor Susan Nolen-Hoeksema)

Source: https://oyc.yale.edu/psychology/psyc-110/lecture-18

## Overview

This guest lecture by Professor Susan Nolen-Hoeksema provides an overview of how modern clinical psychology conceptualizes, diagnoses, and treats mental disorders, using mood disorders (depression and bipolar disorder) as a case example. She emphasizes that there is no biological test for any known mental disorder; diagnosis relies on behavioral criteria matched against the DSM, with judgments influenced by social norms, characteristics of the target person (e.g., gender), and context. The lecture covers the "three Ds" heuristic — distress, dysfunction, and deviance — and walks through DSM criteria for major depression and manic episodes, illustrated by video clips of patients. Statistics on age and gender differences in depression are reviewed, along with biological, cognitive, and interpersonal theories and treatments. The lecture closes with comparative effectiveness data for SSRIs and cognitive behavioral therapy (CBT), noting that CBT substantially reduces relapse rates.

## Key Concepts & Definitions

- **Behavioral criteria**: A set of symptoms a person reports about how they feel and think, plus observations about behavior and how typical/atypical it is; matched against DSM criteria via subjective judgment.
- **Three Ds**: Distress, dysfunction, deviance — heuristics for labeling behavior as abnormal.
- **Distress**: Behaviors that cause the individual or others significant distress.
- **Dysfunction**: Behaviors that prevent the person from functioning in daily life.
- **Deviance**: Behaviors or feelings that are highly unusual (most controversial; heavily influenced by social norms).
- **Antisocial personality disorder**: Individual has no regard for the rights of other people, no hesitation to steal or hurt others, no empathy/sympathy, and no distress over this; causes other people distress.
- **DSM (Diagnostic and Statistical Manual)**: Manual for diagnoses in U.S. clinical psychology/psychiatry, fourth revision; since 1980 emphasized objective, observable criteria.
- **Unipolar depression**: Depression only.
- **Bipolar disorder**: Cycling between depression and mania (formerly called manic-depression).
- **Anhedonia**: Diminished interest or pleasure in usual activities.
- **Insomnia / Hypersomnia**: Trouble sleeping / sleeping all the time.
- **Psychomotor retardation/agitation**: Slowed movement (retardation; more common) or hyperactivity (agitation; rare).
- **Mania**: Abnormally and persistently elevated, expansive, or irritable mood, lasting at least one week, with associated symptoms.
- **Flight of ideas**: Racing thoughts that the manic person cannot speak fast enough to express.
- **Hypomania**: A more mild version of mania with the same symptoms.
- **Monoamines**: Class of neurotransmitters implicated in mood disorders — serotonin, norepinephrine, dopamine.
- **Short allele on the serotonin transporter gene**: A polymorphism that, combined with stress (e.g., maltreatment), predicts depression.
- **Negative cognitive triad** (Aaron Beck): A negative view of the self, the future, and the world.
- **All-or-nothing thinking**: Thinking things are either all good or all bad.
- **Emotional reasoning**: e.g., "If I feel like a loser I must be a loser."
- **Personalization**: Self-blame seen in depressed people.
- **Attributional style in depression**: Internal (self-blame), stable (lasting forever), global (affecting many areas).
- **Anticipatory coping**: Anticipating situations that trigger negative feelings and preparing coping responses.
- **Cognitive Behavioral Therapy (CBT)**: Structured, focused, relatively short-term therapy based on Beck's theory.
- **Interpersonal Therapy (IPT)**: Less structured, more focused on past relationships and how upbringing fostered negative self-views.
- **Walking wounded**: Depressed people who haul themselves through the day acting normal while suffering.

### Behavioral Criteria for Assessing Mental Disorders (Chapter 2)

- Goal: brief overview of how modern clinical psychology views mental disorders, characteristics that cut across them, then mood disorders (depression and bipolar disorder) as examples.
- The fundamental question: "What is abnormality?" — where to draw the line between normal/healthy/typical and abnormal/atypical/deviant/unhealthy/maladaptive.
- **There is no biological test for any of the known mental disorders right now.**
- Instead: behavioral criteria — symptoms reported by the person plus observations of behavior, matched against existing criteria; comes down to a subjective judgment call.

**Factors influencing diagnostic judgments:**

1. **Social norms** — e.g., a woman wearing a veil is typical/prescribed in a Muslim community but until recently was often considered atypical in non-Muslim cultures.
2. **Characteristics of the target person (e.g., gender)** — a man crying is seen as unusual in our culture; a woman crying less so. Conversely, a woman beating up someone is unusual; less so for a man. Gender stereotypes/roles influence judgments.
3. **Context** — "paranoia" and hyper-vigilance for threat is adaptive in downtown Baghdad but abnormal in a quiet farm in Central Connecticut.

**The Three Ds:**

- **Distress** — e.g., depression is a miserable state; antisocial personality disorder causes others distress.
- **Dysfunction** — depression causes complete decline in functioning (can't go to class/work, withdraw socially, lose job, flunk out).
- **Deviance** — highly unusual behaviors/feelings; most controversial because heavily influenced by social norms.

**The DSM:**

- Diagnostic and Statistical Manual; fourth revision.
- Around since the '50s; early editions (1950s–60s) were highly subjective and based on Freudian theory.
- Since 1980, real effort to make criteria objective, observable, and reliable across clinicians.
- Provides lists of symptoms, required symptoms, and number of symptoms; deviation, dysfunction, and distress are built into the criteria.

**Prevalence:**

- As many as **1 in 4 women** will have an episode of serious depression in her life.
- **About 13% of men** will have an episode of serious depression.
- College years are one of the peak times of **first onset** of depression.
- **Late adolescence / early 20s** is the peak onset time for bipolar disorder.

### Unipolar Disorders (Chapter 3)

Mood disorders divide into unipolar (depression only) and bipolar (cycles between depression and mania).

**DSM Criteria for Major Depression:**

- **Criterion 1 (required):** Sadness OR diminished interest/pleasure in usual activities (**anhedonia**).
- Plus at least **four of the following** symptoms:

| # | Symptom | Notes |
|---|---------|-------|
| 1 | Significant weight or appetite change | Lose interest in eating; or binge eating (her friend gained 50 lbs in a year, binge eating especially at night) |
| 2 | Sleep disturbances | Insomnia (esp. waking at 3–4 a.m. unable to return to sleep) or hypersomnia (e.g., sleeping 20–22 hours a day) |
| 3 | Psychomotor retardation or agitation | Retardation more common: slowed movement, reaction times, speech; prone to accidents. Agitation much rarer. |
| 4 | Fatigue / no energy | Can't get up or get moving |
| 5 | Feelings of worthlessness or excessive guilt | Can become psychotic (e.g., believing they are Satan; that random events like a flood are their fault). More commonly unrealistic negative self-esteem. |
| 6 | Diminished ability to concentrate or indecisiveness | Reading passages repeatedly without processing; can't decide on a paper topic |
| 7 | Suicidal ideation or behavior | Occurs in all psychopathology but especially common in depression |

- Symptoms must persist for **at least two weeks** to receive the diagnosis.
- Average length of an untreated episode is **at least six months**.

**Video clip of "Tara":**

- Differentiates everyday sad moods from her debilitating, overwhelming, vegetative depression.
- There is a continuum from normal bad moods to disorder, with no clear demarcation line.
- More moderate forms still warrant a diagnosis and can morph into more serious forms if untreated.
- **"Walking wounded"** — depressed people who haul themselves through the day pretending to be normal, often out of shame or belief they shouldn't need treatment; can persist for years before falling apart.

### Bipolar Disorders (Chapter 4)

Bipolar disorder involves periods of depression and distinct periods of the opposite — **mania**.

**Criteria for a Manic Episode:**

- **Criterion 1:** Abnormally and persistently elevated, expansive, or irritable mood for **at least one week** — not just a good day.
- Plus **three or more** of the following:

| # | Symptom | Notes |
|---|---------|-------|
| 1 | Inflated self-esteem / grandiosity | "If you can't keep up with me it's your fault." |
| 2 | Decreased need for sleep | Only a couple of hours, up "raring to go" |
| 3 | More talkative; pressured speech | Talk rapidly, hard to interrupt |
| 4 | Flight of ideas | Racing thoughts; can't talk fast enough to express them |
| 5 | Highly distractible | |
| 6 | Increase in goal-directed activity | Grand schemes — cash out family bank accounts, sell house/car, finance irrational ventures |
| 7 | Pleasurable but dangerous activities | Sexual promiscuity, drug abuse, gambling, feeling on a "hot streak" |

- Mood often irritable and trigger-fire; can become violent due to agitation/irritability.

**Video clips:**

- An older woman in a manic episode at the hairdresser — pressured speech, increasing agitation, then flipping into a depressed state. **Lithium** mentioned but not stabilizing her.
- "Bernie" — a man with bipolar disorder, not currently in episode. **Hypomania** introduced as the more mild version with the same symptoms.

**Continuum and famous cases:**

- Mania runs along a continuum from mild to severe/psychotic.
- Psychotic mania: grandiose delusions — believing they are the Messiah, Albert Einstein reincarnated, or have supernatural powers; grandiose hallucinations.
- Some chronically mildly manic people, especially with talent/intelligence, "make it work for them."
- **Kay Jamison** (Johns Hopkins) wrote a book chronicling historical figures (Robert Schumann, Winston Churchill, well-known authors/poets/musicians/politicians) who likely had mild bipolar disorder and channeled mania through extraordinary talent.
- Some very successful CEOs may be chronically slightly manic — function on a few hours of sleep, grandiose, self-confident, channeled productively.

**Consequences and motivations for treatment:**

- Mania can lead to STDs, drug activity, arrest, bankruptcy.
- Negative consequences and the eventual plunge into depression motivate help-seeking (since mania itself can feel pleasurable).

**Prevalence comparison:**

- Depression: ~22% of women, ~13% of men lifetime (note: earlier in lecture she said "1 in 4 women"; in Chapter 4 stated as "22% of women").
- **Bipolar disorder: ~1% of population; equally prevalent in women and men.**

### Statistics for Depression (Chapter 5)

**Age differences (nationwide study, ages 15–55, several thousand people, community sample; percentage with major depression episode in the past month):**

- Highest in **15–24** age range.
- Decline somewhat thereafter, although 35–44 remains fairly high.
- Rates of major depression in the **elderly** are quite low by most national statistics, true up to about age 80 or 85.

**Three explanations for low elderly rates:**

1. As you get older you get wiser → lower depression.
2. Cohort/historical effect — current younger generations are more prone to depression for life compared to grandparents, due to changes in social support, family networks, etc.
3. Depression negatively impacts physical health (higher rates of cardiovascular disease, stroke, immune system diseases); people with lifelong depression die earlier, leaving a healthier elderly sample.
- All three may be partly true.

**Gender differences:**

- Data: compilation of hundreds of studies of children and adolescents, using self-report measures (e.g., **Beck Depression Inventory** and its kiddy version) on several thousand children.
- **Prior to age ~13:** boys and girls have similar levels of depression.
- **Around puberty:** girls' rates rise dramatically; boys' rates stay the same or decline.
- **By 18–20:** nearly a **2:1 ratio** of depressed girls to depressed boys, persisting through the adult lifespan.

**Hypotheses for gender difference:**

- Biological — hormones.
- Sociological — stress, particularly abuse, in girls' lives.
- Likely a combination.

### Biological, Cognitive, and Interpersonal Theories and Treatments (Chapter 6)

**Genetics:**

- Strong evidence for bipolar disorder.
- **Monozygotic (identical) twin concordance: >60%** if co-twin has bipolar disorder.
- **Dizygotic (fraternal) twin concordance: ~12%**.
- **Second-degree relatives: ~2%** (barely above general population ~1%).
- For major depression: stronger genetic component in **early onset depression** (first episodes in childhood/very early adolescence); weaker for depressions clearly triggered by major life events (trauma, loss).

**Neurotransmitters — the monoamines:**

- Serotonin, norepinephrine, dopamine.
- Both bipolar disorder and depression linked.
- Old view: too little neurotransmitter (especially serotonin).
- Current view: **receptor functioning** — receptors don't function efficiently, so neurons can't make use of available neurotransmitter. Antidepressants improve neurotransmitter functioning.

**Gene × Stress interaction (serotonin transporter gene):**

- Researchers at Yale: **Julia Kim-Cohen** (recently joined); **Joan Kaufman** in psychiatry.
- **Classic study by Avshalom Caspi and colleagues**: people with one or two **short alleles** on the serotonin transporter gene who were confronted with stress (e.g., maltreatment) were more likely to develop depression.
- Genotype alone did not predict depression in the absence of stress.
- Replicated in at least **four different studies** with other samples and other forms of major trauma.
- **Gene × environment intersection** is a major risk factor.

**Brain areas implicated:**

| Area | Function | Finding in mood disorders |
|------|----------|---------------------------|
| Prefrontal cortex | Higher-order thinking, problem solving, goal-directed behavior | Lowered activity in depression; may explain concentration, planning, emotion regulation difficulties |
| Amygdala | Processing of emotional information | Overactive responses to emotional information (depression and bipolar) |
| Hippocampus | Memory and concentration | Shrinkage in chronic depression; may relate to concentration/attention problems |
| Anterior cingulate | Responses to stress and choice of behaviors | Dysregulation may underlie poor coping and difficulty adjusting behaviors |

**Drug treatments:**

- **Monoamine oxidase inhibitors (MAOIs)** — older class.
- **Tricyclic antidepressants** — still used; ~60% respond; many side effects; fatal in overdose.
- **SSRIs (selective serotonin re-uptake inhibitors)** — Paxil, Prozac, etc.; **Prozac introduced 1987** in U.S.; took over the market; not much more effective than older antidepressants, but fewer side effects.
- **SNRIs (selective serotonin/norepinephrine re-uptake inhibitors)** — prevent re-uptake of serotonin (and norepinephrine) back into the sending neuron, increasing synaptic neurotransmitter; reasonably effective, but a substantial number do not respond; may have to cycle through drugs.
- **Lithium** — drug of choice for bipolar disorder; stabilizes mood swings by stabilizing multiple neurotransmitter systems; major side effects (gastrointestinal), dangerous during pregnancy for fetal development; often combined with antidepressants because lithium primarily affects manic episodes.
- **Antipsychotic medications** — used in mood disorders when person has lost touch with reality.

**Cognitive Behavioral Therapy (CBT) — Aaron Beck's Negative Cognitive Theory:**

- Aaron Beck — psychiatrist at the University of Pennsylvania; founded CBT.
- Applies to depression alone (not bipolar disorder).
- **Negative cognitive triad:** negative view of self, future, and world.
- Fed by cognitive distortions:
  - **All-or-nothing thinking**.
  - **Emotional reasoning** ("if I feel like a loser I must be a loser").
  - **Personalization** (self-blame).
- Depressed people make attributions for negative events that are **internal, stable, and global**.

**Prospective evidence (Temple University + University of Wisconsin study):**

- Identified first-year college students with a negative cognitive/attributional style who had never been depressed.
- Tracked over two and a half years.
- Substantially higher rate of major depression in negative-cognitive-style group vs. comparison group.

**CBT therapy components:**

1. Identify themes in negative thoughts and triggers.
2. Help the person challenge those thoughts — what's the evidence, are there other interpretations, how could they cope if a bad thing did happen.
3. Recognize negative beliefs/assumptions and challenge their truth value.
4. Change aspects of the environment related to depressive symptoms via active problem-solving.
5. Teach mood-management skills to prevent decline.

**CBT vs. Paxil study (240 patients with major depressive disorder):**

- 4 months of acute treatment: CBT or Paxil (SSRI); placebo (sugar pill) control.
- **8 weeks:** Paxil had a slight edge over CBT.
- **16 weeks:** Paxil and CBT essentially even — about **60%** of people no longer depressed in each.

**Relapse over 12 additional months:**

| Group | Relapse rate |
|-------|--------------|
| Placebo (off Paxil; no CBT) | **~80%** in first year |
| Stayed on Paxil | **~50%** |
| Received CBT | **~35%** |

- CBT teaches coping skills for new stressors; reduces relapse dramatically. Finding replicated repeatedly.

**CBT video clip:**

- Therapist demonstrated: **Aaron Beck's daughter**, his heir in CBT practice and development.
- Role play: depressed man who recently lost his job.
- Therapist has him generate his own challenges to negative thoughts and write them down.
- Major premise: most of the work happens **between** sessions, not in session.
- Helps him develop phrases to use when discouraged.
- Teaches **anticipatory coping** — anticipating triggering situations and pre-planning responses.

**Interpersonal Therapy (IPT):**

- Other major psychotherapy for depression.
- Theory: negative self-views and relationship expectations are rooted in upbringings that fostered them.
- Helps clients understand negative self-views and how they're rooted in past relationships.
- Less structured than CBT; more focused on the past.
- CBT is focused on the present.
- Less research on IPT than on CBT; positive alternative especially for those whose depression is tied up in recurrent themes in relationships.

**Closing note:**

- The good news: multiple drug therapies and at least two psychotherapies (CBT and IPT) shown by controlled research to be helpful; people have choices and there is no need to stay in depression.
