# PSYC 110 - Lecture 12 - Evolution, Emotion, and Reason: Emotions, Part II

Source: https://oyc.yale.edu/psychology/psyc-110/lecture-12

## Overview

This lecture continues the two-part treatment of emotions, moving from emotions toward kin to social emotions toward non-kin. Professor Bloom uses the exotic (Emperor penguins, vampire bats) to motivate scientific study of things that feel "natural" (like parental love), echoing William James. He explains how reciprocal altruism evolves only when animals can detect and punish cheaters, and uses The Prisoner's Dilemma and The Ultimatum Game to demonstrate how human emotions correspond to permutations of cooperation, defection, and fairness. The lecture closes with a discussion of "cultures of honor" — arguing that mild irrationality (like a hair-trigger temper) can be adaptive, and that this plays out in cultural differences such as those between the American South and North. The overarching claim is that emotions are not noise but exquisitely complicated motivational systems shaped by evolution and modified by culture.

## Key Concepts & Definitions

- **Appeasement smile** — In nonhuman primates, smiles correspond almost entirely to "don't hurt me" signals, equivalent to the human "coy smile."
- **Imprinting (debunked version)** — The discredited idea that a baby must see its mother at a "magical moment" right after birth or attachment will fail.
- **Cupboard Theory** — Skinnerian/operant conditioning account: babies love mothers because mothers provide food (law of effect).
- **Bowlby's theory** — A nativist, hard-wired account positing two things: a draw to mom for comfort and social interaction, plus fear of strangers.
- **Reciprocal altruism** — Good behavior toward another animal predicated on the expectation of reciprocation; benefits outweigh costs when animals cooperate.
- **Cheaters / Free-riders** — Animals (or agents) who take the benefits of cooperation without paying the costs.
- **Cheater detection** — The mental apparatus (recognize, remember, motivation to punish) required for reciprocal altruism to be an evolutionarily stable strategy.
- **The Prisoner's Dilemma** — A game in which the dominant strategy is to defect regardless of the other player's choice, but mutual defection leaves both worse off than mutual cooperation.
- **Tit-for-Tat** — Anatol Rappaport's winning 4-line program: cooperate on first encounter, then mirror the other player's previous move. Features: starts friendly, not a sucker, forgiving, transparent.
- **The Ultimatum Game** — Player A offers a split of money to Player B; B can accept (both keep proposed amounts) or reject (neither gets anything).
- **Adaptive irrationality** — Having a reputation for irrational responses (e.g., a temper, head-over-heels love) can give an evolutionary edge by deterring exploitation.
- **Culture of honor** — A culture where law is unreliable and resources are easily taken, making a reputation for violent retaliation important.

## Detailed Notes

### Chapter 1. Emotional Responses to Caregivers and Kin [00:00:00]

- **Follow-up on nonhuman primate smiles**: Bloom contacted experts. Answer: nonhuman primates (chimps, gorillas, gibbons) have smiles that correspond almost entirely to appeasement smiles ("don't hurt me"), equivalent to the human coy smile.
  - No equivalent to the **greeting smile** or **Pan Am smile**.
  - No equivalent to the **Duchenne smile** (genuine expression of happiness).
- **Review of course arc**: functions of emotions → smiling/facial expressions → nonsocial emotion (fear) → social emotions toward kin → evolutionary basis.
- **Quality vs. quantity in offspring**: Humans/birds/mammals produce few children and invest heavily; long period of dependence creates deep parent–child bonds.

#### Using the exotic to study the familiar
- Analogy from a friend who studies psychology of religion: when speaking to non-specialists, start with exotic examples (e.g., a culture where men put butter on their heads in summer — Dan Sperber's example, or cultures believing trees can talk) to motivate study of practices in our own culture.
- This connects to a William James point: things natural to us feel obvious; equally odd things are natural to other species.
- Applied to **parental love**: don't look head-on; look at other species.

#### "March of the Penguins" Example
- Emperor penguins; the documentary was the second most popular documentary of all time, beaten only by "Fahrenheit 9/11."
- Elaborate, precarious system: long trek from water to breeding grounds (protected from wind, firm ice).
- Responses to the film:
  - Conservatives: celebration of family values (love, trust, monogamy).
  - Liberals: "monogamous for one breeding season... if you add it up, it's pretty slutty."
  - Intelligent design / creationist take: example of God's intricate design.
  - Darwinian take: agrees it couldn't be accidental — it's an exquisite biological adaptation for parental care, shaped because children share parents' genes.

#### How children attach to caregivers
- Babies develop attachment to whoever is closest — typically the mother (prefer her voice, face, smell).
- **Imprinting myth**: The idea of a magical post-birth moment is silly; attachment forms "in the fullness of time."
- **Two competing theories**:
  - **Cupboard Theory (Skinnerian)**: attachment via operant conditioning — mother provides food.
  - **Bowlby's nativist theory**: hard-wired draw to mom for comfort/social interaction + fear of strangers.
- **Harlow's experiments** (reviewed from prior lecture):
  - Two surrogate mothers: wire mother (food/Skinnerian) vs. cloth mother (warmth, cuddling).
  - Babies went to wire mother to eat ("You've got to eat to live") but loved and attached to the cloth mother — used her as a base when threatened and to explore from.

#### Effects of no contact
- Real-world relevance: debates about daycare.
- Short answer on daycare: nobody really knows; differences (if any) are subtle and not big/dramatic.
- **Harlow's solitary confinement monkeys**: raised in steel cages with only wire mother — got nutrition but no mothering.
  - Outcomes: withdrawn, don't play, bite themselves, incompetent sexually, socially, and maternally.
  - One artificially inseminated solitary-raised monkey banged her infant's head on the floor and bit it to death.
- **Human natural experiments**: children in harsh orphanages with little social contact develop severe social/emotional problems — either insatiable need for cuddling or apathetic.
- **Reversibility**:
  - "Monkey therapists": a younger goofing-around monkey clings to and follows the solitary one, producing gradual improvement.
  - Human anecdote: children at age 1.5 taken from a harsh orphanage to a home for mentally retarded women who cuddled them — apparently brought them back to normal.

### Chapter 2. Question and Answer on Emotions towards Kin [00:15:44]

- **Q: Do children in orphanages comfort each other?**
  - Bloom: probably not in practice because they tend to be very young babies not thrown together that way. TA confirms there is evidence that among the young, peer support can help both monkeys and children.
- **Q: What about the middle ground (parent comforts only a little)?**
  - No good evidence that mild under-affection has dramatic effects.
  - Parents who aren't affectionate tend to have kids who aren't affectionate, but it's unclear if this is genetic or environmental.
  - **Take-away**: outside extreme conditions, effects are not big and dramatic.

### Chapter 3. Evolutionary Explanations for Emotional Responses [00:18:16]

- Kin attraction is not a big evolutionary puzzle (gene reproduction logic).
- The puzzle: animals (including humans) have complicated, nice relationships with **non-kin**.
- **Examples of niceness to non-kin**:
  - **Grooming** — picking lice off each other.
  - **Warning cries** — risky to the caller, often protecting non-relatives.
  - **Shared childcare**.
  - **Food sharing** — vampire bats: a successful hunter returns to the cave and vomits blood into other bats' mouths.
- **Reciprocal altruism**: behavior is predicated on reciprocation; the group does better than each working alone.

#### The cheater problem
- Two hypothetical vampire-bat genes:
  - Gene A: accepts blood and shares blood.
  - Gene B: accepts blood but doesn't share.
  - Gene B outproduces A in the long run (healthier, more offspring) — cooperation collapses.
- **Warning-cry version**: adaptive to **respond** to warning cries; not adaptive to **give** them.
- **Bar analogy**: it's good to accept drinks but not to buy rounds — if everyone defects, "buying a round" fades.

#### Cheater detection as solution
- Reciprocal altruism evolves only if animals are wired to **recognize, remember, and punish cheaters**.
- Vampire bat experiment: scientists prevent a successful bat from sharing. Result: when other bats strike it big, they **starve the selfish bat**. Confirmed cheater detection in bats.
- Animals (and humans) are sensitive to cheating; this plays a powerful role in evolution of social behavior and social emotions.

### Chapter 4. Cooperative Behavior and the Prisoner's Dilemma [00:25:20]

- A core construct across cognitive science, psychology, economics.
- **Setup**: You and a friend rob a bank; you're caught; police interrogate separately. Each can cooperate (stay silent) or defect (squeal).
- **Payoff logic**:
  - Best individual outcome: defect while other cooperates (walk free).
  - Worst: cooperate while other defects (life in prison).
  - Best joint outcome: both cooperate (minor sentence).
  - Worst joint outcome: both defect.
- **Key structural feature**: regardless of opponent's move, defecting is individually best — but mutual defection makes both worse off.

#### Other examples
- **Divorce lawyers**: both better off with mediator, but each tempted to hire a lawyer; both end up with lawyers and worse off.
- **Nuclear disarmament**: country A vs. B; mutual disarm is best, but each tempted to arm.
- **Drug deal ("reefer")**: $1,000 for "a ton" of reefer behind the gym at 2 a.m.; each tempted to bring a gun; both show up armed.

#### In-class one-shot game
- Students played with cards reading "cooperate"/"defect."
- Payoff: cooperate/cooperate = $3 each; cooperate vs. defect = defector gets $5, cooperator gets nothing.
- Then five repeated games. Bloom joked the high scorer (~$20–25) won by exploiting cooperator partners — "really a measure of honesty."

#### Robert Axelrod's tournament
- About 20 years ago; **63 competitors** submitted programs.
- Programs ranged from "always cooperate"/"always defect" to elegant genetic algorithms.
- **Winner: "Tit-for-Tat" by Anatol Rappaport** (who died about a month before this lecture).
- **4 lines of basic code**:
  - First encounter: cooperate.
  - Afterward: do what the other program did on the previous trial.
- **Why it won — four features**:
  - Starts friendly.
  - Not a sucker (defects back when crossed).
  - Forgiving (returns to cooperation if other does).
  - Transparent (other programs can tell it's not a sucker and is forgiving).

#### Emotions mapped onto Prisoner's Dilemma cells
- We **like** people who cooperate with us → motivates future niceness.
- We feel **anger and distrust** toward those who betray us → motivates betrayal/avoidance.
- We feel **bad/guilt** when we betray cooperators → motivates better future behavior.

#### Bloom's home experiment
- With his 7- and 10-year-old sons, chocolate chips as stakes.
- Older boy tricked younger: "Okay, let's both cooperate" then defected.
- Younger boy reacted with **rage** (not guilt on the older boy's side).

### Chapter 5. The Ultimatum Game, Rationality and Irrationality [00:40:44]

#### The game
- A has $10. A offers B any integer amount $1–$10. B accepts (deal goes through) or rejects (nobody gets anything).
- Class played one round.

#### Rationality analysis
- **Rational play**: $1 > $0, so B should always accept any positive offer; A should offer $1.
- **Actual play**: few offered $1 because **people are not purely rational**; even in one-shot games people **reject unfair distributions out of spite**.
- Neuro-economic evidence: people offered $1 get really angry.

#### Moral: rationality is exploitable
- A rational person is easily exploited; their responses to provocation are measured and predictable.
- A person with a temper / reputation for irrationality is **treated better** — others avoid messing with them.
- Mild irrationality can be **adaptively beneficial**.

#### Application to love
- Rational mate ("I'll stay as long as you're the most attractive available") is less trustworthy than a partner who is **head over heels in love** — the very irrationality signals reliability.

#### Application to violence (Daly and Wilson)
- Most murder is **not** caused by reasonable provocation — it's caused by insult, curse, petty infraction.
- This is **adaptive irrationality**, not crazy irrationality.
- Daly and Wilson: "in chronically feuding and warring societies an essential manly virtue is the capacity for violence. To turn the other cheek is not saintly, but stupid or contemptibly weak."
- Modern New York Times example: violence often due to disrespect / dirty looks — rational in environments with repeated interaction and weak police presence.

### Chapter 6. Cultures of Honor [00:49:21]

#### Defining properties of a culture of honor
- Cannot rely on the law.
- Resources are easily taken.
- → Reputation for **violent retaliation** becomes important.

#### Examples
- Scottish highlanders
- Masai warriors
- Bedouin tradesmen
- Western cowboys
- **American South** — most studied by modern psychologists; settled by herdsmen, traditionally less centralized legal control.

#### Documented differences (South vs. North, US)
- More permissive gun laws in the South.
- More approval of corporal and capital punishment.
- More positive attitudes toward the military.
- In questionnaires, more forgiving of honor-based violence ("Somebody insults my woman and I punch him in the face").
- Streets not generally more dangerous overall, but **higher rate of honor-related crimes** (e.g., shooting an intruder, killing someone for insults).

#### Nisbett and Cohen's experiment
- Conducted at University of Michigan.
- Sample: white males, **not Hispanic and not Jewish** (homogeneous sample; culture of honor seen as male phenomenon).
- **Provocation**: subject walks down a hallway; a male grad student holding files bumps him, calls him "**Asshole**," and keeps walking.
  - The grad student survived hundreds of these encounters — no fights or shootings.
- **Findings**:
  - Southern males showed **higher hormone response** — increases in **testosterone and cortisol**.
  - Differences in later behavior and fill-in-the-blank responses: northerners completed "John went to the store and bought a __" with neutral words (e.g., "an apple"); southerners more aggressive (Bloom's joke: "an AK-47 to kill that freaking graduate student").
- Southerners were not more violent overall — they were **more sensitive to honor provocations**.

#### Caveats Bloom offers
- These are **average differences**; not every northerner or southerner fits.
- A southern student previously felt the discussion was unfair to southerners.
- Nisbett (himself a southerner) found northerners "astonishingly rude" — possibly because no culture of honor restrains them.
- Culture-of-honor virtues — **honor, loyalty, courage, self-reliance** — are not necessarily bad.

#### Closing synthesis
- Emotions (fear, love for children, anger, gratitude) are not noise — they're complex motivational systems crafted by evolution to handle natural and social environments.
- An evolutionary approach is required to see this clearly.
- Bloom closes with D'Arcy Thompson: "everything is the way it is because it got that way."
