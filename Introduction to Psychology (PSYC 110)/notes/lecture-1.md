# PSYC 110 — Lecture 1 — Introduction

Source: https://oyc.yale.edu/psychology/psyc-110/lecture-1

## Overview

In this opening lecture, Professor Paul Bloom welcomes students to Introduction to Psychology and lays out the course as "a comprehensive introduction to the study of the human mind." He previews a wide range of topics — brains, children, language, sex, memory, madness, disgust, racism, love, and more — and stresses that scientific psychology has real-world relevance to everyday problems such as studying, persuading others, and choosing therapies. Bloom outlines course logistics (lectures, readings, exams, weekly questions, a book review, and an experimental participation requirement) and describes the five traditional sub-areas of psychology the course will cover: neuroscience, developmental, cognitive, social, and clinical. He argues that the mind cannot be studied through psychology alone and that the course will draw on evolution, economics, game theory, philosophy, computer science, anthropology, literature, and theology. He closes by previewing five example topics — the brain, children, attractiveness, morality (good and evil), and mental illness — and announces four guest lecturers.

## Key Concepts & Definitions

- **Psychology (as framed in this course):** "A comprehensive introduction to the study of the human mind" — how the human mind works, how we think, and what makes us what we are.
- **Neuroscience:** "The study of the mind by looking at the brain."
- **Developmental psychology:** The area "trying to learn about how people develop and grow and learn." (Bloom notes this is the area he focuses on.)
- **Cognitive psychology:** "A sort of computational approach to studying the mind, often viewing the mind on analogy with a computer and looking at how people do things like understand language, recognize objects, play games, and so on."
- **Social psychology:** "The study of how people act in groups, how people act with other people."
- **Clinical psychology:** "The study of mental health and mental illness" — the aspect many people think of immediately when they hear "psychology."
- **Capgras syndrome:** Typically the result of some sort of stroke; you "develop a particular delusion" that "the people you love the most have been replaced" — by aliens, robots, Martians, CIA agents, or trained actors/actresses. Associated with a very high level of violence. Hundreds of cases exist.
- **Cotard's syndrome:** A related disorder involving the very same parts of the brain as Capgras. "You believe that you're dead; you are persuaded that you're dead." You're walking around, you know you're walking around, you know there are people around, but you think that you're dead.
- **Psychopathy:** "People who, either due to brain damage or because they are born that way, have no moral understanding."
- **Multiple personality disorder:** Cases "where people have more than one personality" — with debate over whether such cases are a real phenomenon or made up.

## Detailed Notes

### Chapter 1. Introduction to and Requirements for the Course [00:03:40]

- Professor: **Dr. Paul Bloom**, professor of the course.
- The course is "a comprehensive introduction to the study of the human mind."
- Topics will include: brains, children, language, sex, memory, madness, disgust, racism, love, and many others.
- Sample questions the course will address:
  - The proper explanation for differences between men and women.
  - Whether animals can learn language.
  - The puzzle of what grosses us out.
  - Why some of us eat too much and what we could do to stop.
  - Why people go crazy in groups.
  - Whether you can trust your childhood memories.
  - Why some of us get depressed and others don't.

**Format & expectations:**
- Two lectures a week plus course readings.
- "To do well in the course, you have to attend both the lectures and do the readings."
- Some overlap between lectures and readings; some readings will not appear in lectures, and some entire lectures will not connect to the readings.
- If you miss class, get notes from a friend or the person next to you.
- Slides will be posted online in a black-and-white, easy-to-print format. Slides are **not** a substitute for attending class.

**Texts (both required):**
- **Peter Gray, *Psychology*, 5th edition** (textbook).

### Chapter 2. General Goals for the Course [00:10:03]

- Common (sometimes joking) motivations students bring:
  - "Maybe you're crazy and hope to become less crazy."
  - "Maybe you want to learn how to study better, improve your sex life, interpret your dreams, and win friends and influence people."
- Bloom: with the exception of the sex part, the course might actually help with some of these. Scientific psychology has real-world relevance — for studying, interacting with people, persuading others, choosing therapy.
- Larger goal: "a state of the art introduction to the most important topic that there is: us."

**Five traditional sub-areas of psychology (all to be covered):**

| Sub-area | Definition (as given) |
|---|---|
| Neuroscience | Study of the mind by looking at the brain |
| Developmental | How people develop, grow, and learn (Bloom's focus) |
| Cognitive | Computational approach to the mind; mind on analogy with a computer; language, object recognition, games |
| Social | How people act in groups and with other people |
| Clinical | Study of mental health and mental illness |

**Related disciplines the course will draw on:**
- "You cannot study the mind solely by looking at the discipline of psychology."
- Evolution of the mind.
- Economics and game theory ("now essential tools for understanding human thought and human behavior").
- Philosophy, computer science, anthropology, literature, theology, "and many, many other domains."

### Chapter 3. Examples of Materials Covered in the Course [00:13:08]

Bloom previews **five example topics**.

#### Example 1: The Brain (covered next week, Monday)
- Shows an image of a specific brain — **Terri Schiavo's brain**, with a "little white mark."
- Schiavo was in a coma without consciousness as a result of brain damage.
- The case is "a stark illustration of the physical nature of mental life" — the physical basis for "everything that we normally hold dear, like free will, consciousness, morality and emotions."
- The course will begin by asking how a physical thing can give rise to mental life.

#### Example 2: Children
- Shows a photo of his younger son **Zachary**, dressed as Spider-Man.
- Why study child development:
  - **The question of development:** Everyone in the room can speak English, has some understanding of how the physical world works, and how other people behave. How do we come to have this knowledge? How much is "hard-wired, built-in, innate," and how much is "the product of culture, of language, of schooling"?
  - Developmental psychologists use ingenious methods to figure out "the basic components of human nature."
- **The question of continuity:** To what extent is your fate sealed? If we met you at age five, could we describe the way you are now?
  - Quotes poet **William Wordsworth**: "The child is father to the man" — meaning you can see in every child the adult he or she will become.
  - Is this true for personality? Interests? Intelligence?
- **The question of individual differences:** People differ in taste in food, IQ, aggressiveness/shyness, sexual attraction (males, females, both, or neither), musical ability, political liberal/conservative orientation.
  - Translated as a **genes vs. environment** question, including their interaction.
  - One common theory: we are shaped by our parents.
  - Quotes British poet **Philip Larkin**:
    > They mess you up, your mum and dad.
    > They may not mean to but they do.
    > They fill you with the faults they had
    > And add some extra just for you.
  - Whether parents matter has been a huge controversy in popular culture; it will occupy much of the course.

#### Example 3: Attractiveness
- Question: "What makes for a pretty face?"
- Shows highly-rated attractive **computer-generated** Caucasian male and female faces — not real people.
- By generating faces and asking people to rate them, scientists have learned what makes a face attractive, both **within cultures and across cultures**.
- To be discussed when covering **social behavior** and **sex**.
- Not all attractiveness is linked to sex — example: **pandas** are "notoriously cute."

#### Example 4: Morality — Good and Evil
- "Morality is extremely central to our lives."
- The deep question: good and evil, evil and good.
- Three pictures exemplify different sorts of evil:
  1. **Institutional evil** — somebody behaving cruelly toward another, "perhaps not due to malice but because of the situation that she's in."
  2. **Osama bin Laden** — "a mass murderer or driven by political cause?"
  3. **Ted Bundy** — an example before getting into "technical stuff like crazy-evil."
- Same question: is being good or bad part of your nature, or largely due to the situation you fall into?
- "There's a lot of some quite spectacular experiments that try to tease that apart."
- **Good** — two notoriously good men:
  - **Oskar Schindler** (Holocaust).
  - **Paul Rusesabagina** (Rwanda).
  - Both saved many lives at great risk; both had good movies made about them.
  - "You couldn't have predicted ahead of time that they would be heroes."
  - Personal question: what would we do in such situations?

#### Example 5: Mental Illness
- A full week near the end of the course will be devoted to **major disorders** like **depression** and **anxiety**, because of their profound social importance.
- "Such disorders are reasonably common in college students."
- Many people in the room are currently suffering from a mood disorder, an anxiety disorder, or both; many are on medication.
- The course will discuss current research on causes and the best ways to treat them.

**Less common disorders (also covered):**
- **Memory disorders** — including disorders that prevent forming new memories, and **amnesia** (forgetting the past).
- **Phineas Gage**:
  - A construction worker about 100 years ago.
  - An explosion drove a metal pipe through his head; pipe ended up "100 feet away, covered with brains and blood."
  - He was not killed. He sat down and said "uh, oh," then stopped by a pub for cider on the way to the hospital.
  - He was not blind, not deaf, not retarded.
  - But: "He lost his sense of right and wrong. He lost his control."
  - Was previously a hard-working family man; afterward he could not hold a job, could not stay faithful to his wife, could not speak for five minutes without cursing, got into fights and brawls, got drunk.
  - Ended up on a circus sideshow traveling the country with the steel pipe that went through his head.
  - "An extraordinary example of how the brain can give rise to the mind."
- **Multiple personality disorder** — people with more than one personality; debated whether real or made up.
- **Capgras syndrome**:
  - Typically results from a stroke; hundreds of cases.
  - Delusion: "the people you love the most have been replaced" — by aliens, robots, Martians, CIA agents, trained actors and actresses.
  - Associated with very high levels of violence.
  - Example: a man in Australia, under the delusion that his father had been replaced with a robot, cut off his head.
- **Cotard's syndrome**:
  - Involves the same parts of the brain as Capgras.
  - You believe you are dead, even while walking around aware of others.
- These syndromes are "located … at a pinpoint level to certain parts of your brain." The course will discuss "the best modern theories as to why these syndromes occur."

**Why study extreme/abnormal cases:**
- Not because they are frequent, and not from "gruesome, morbid curiosity."
- "By looking at extreme cases, they can help us best understand normal life."
- Extremes throw into sharp contrast things we take for granted.
- **Psychopathy** helps with questions of free will and responsibility, and the relationship/difference between **mental illness and evil**.
- **Multiple personality** cases force the question: **What is a self?** Are we composed of multiple people, or unified across time?
- **Capgras** tells us how we see the world — that "there is a difference between recognizing something in the sense that you could name it, and knowing what it is."
