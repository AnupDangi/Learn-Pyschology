## Overview

This lecture, delivered by Professor Paul Bloom, completes loose ends from the Freud lecture and then introduces B. F. Skinner and behaviorism. Bloom first revisits the question of why an unconscious would evolve, offering a deception-based hypothesis: the best lies are ones we tell ourselves. He then turns to behaviorism, presenting it as a movement championed by figures like John Watson and later packaged and publicized by Skinner, organized around three radical claims — strong emphasis on learning, anti-mentalism, and no interesting differences across species. The bulk of the lecture walks through the three learning principles behaviorists used to explain all of mental life: habituation, classical conditioning, and operant (instrumental) conditioning. Bloom closes with the criticisms — innate knowledge, the legitimacy of mental notions, animal-specific constraints (e.g., the Garcia effect), and Chomsky's argument that Skinner's terms are too vague to be scientific — concluding that while behaviorism faded, its mechanisms and training techniques remain an important legacy.

## Key Concepts & Definitions

- **Deception (broad sense):** "to act or be in some way that fools others into believing or thinking or responding to something that's false."
- **Behaviorism:** A school of thought, predating Skinner, championed by psychologists like John Watson, built on three radical views (strong emphasis on learning, anti-mentalism, no interesting cross-species differences).
- **Habituation:** "a decline in the tendency to respond to stimuli that are familiar due to repeated exposure." The very simplest form of learning.
- **Classical conditioning:** "the learning of an association between one stimulus and another stimulus," where stimulus is "a technical term meaning events in the environment like a certain smell or sound or sight." Thought up by Pavlov.
- **Unconditioned stimulus (UCS):** Stimulus that gives rise to an unconditioned response without learning (e.g., poking with a stick; food powder in the dog's mouth).
- **Unconditioned response:** The natural, unlearned response to the unconditioned stimulus (e.g., saying "ouch"; salivation).
- **Conditioned stimulus (CS):** A previously neutral stimulus that, through pairing with the UCS, comes to elicit a response (e.g., the bell).
- **Conditioned response:** The learned response to the conditioned stimulus.
- **Reinforced trial:** When the CS and UCS go together — "you're teaching the dog that the bell goes with the food."
- **Unreinforced trial:** When the CS occurs without the UCS — "you get the food without the bell."
- **Extinction:** When teaching stops, the response goes away.
- **Spontaneous recovery:** After extinction, if you wait a while and try the CS again after a couple of hours, the response (e.g., saliva) comes back.
- **Stimulus generalization:** A conditioned response extends to similar stimuli (e.g., Little Albert's fear extending from a rat to a white rabbit).
- **Systematic desensitization:** A cure for phobias that exposes you to what causes fear while relaxing you at the same time, so you replace the aversive classically conditioned fear with something positive.
- **Operant (instrumental) conditioning:** "learning the relationships between what you do and how successful or unsuccessful they are, learning what works and what doesn't." Voluntary; unlike classical conditioning, the organism chooses to act.
- **Law of effect:** "a tendency to perform – an action's increased if rewarded, weakened if it's not."
- **Positive reinforcement:** You do something (give a reward) to increase a behavior.
- **Negative reinforcement:** A type of reward in which you "take away something aversive" (e.g., remove a heavy collar). Different from punishment.
- **Shaping:** Working an animal or child gradually toward a complex behavior by rewarding successive approximations.
- **Primary reinforcers:** Things organisms naturally like (food) or naturally don't like (being hit, shocked).
- **Token economies:** Trained systems where "seemingly arbitrary rewards like poker chips" function as reinforcers via classical conditioning.
- **Schedules of reinforcement:** Fixed vs. variable, ratio vs. interval. Ratio = reward every certain number of times; interval = over a period of time.
- **Partial reinforcement effect:** "Behaviors last longer if they're reinforced intermittently."
- **Garcia effect:** Conditioned taste aversion — a connection between a new food's taste and getting sick is "natural" and "hard wired," but is special to taste and nausea; the same association does not form with painful shock.

## Detailed Notes

### Chapter 1. A Brief Review on the Unconscious

- Bloom returns to Freud to address material skipped on Wednesday — specifically why an unconscious would exist.
- He notes that the right question may not be "Why are some things unconscious?" but rather "why is this tiny subset of mental life conscious?" because "by far the vast majority of what our brains do, the vast majority of what our minds do, is unconscious and we're unaware of it."
- **Evolutionary hypothesis: deception.** Most animals deceive.
  - Physical examples:
    - Chimpanzees: when threatened, "their hair stands up on end and that makes them look bigger to fool others to thinking they're more dangerous than they are."
    - Angler fish at the bottom of the ocean has "a rod sticking up from the top of its head with a lure to capture other fish."
  - Humans, especially, use minds and behavior continually to trick others into thinking we're "tougher, smarter, sexier, more reliable, more trustworthy."
  - Social psychology in part concerns self-presentation to make "the maximally positive impression even when that impression isn't true."
- We've also evolved good lie-detection mechanisms — so there's evolutionary pressure both to lie and to detect lies.
- **Key hypothesis:** "the best lies are lies we tell ourselves. You're a better liar, more generally, if you believe the lie that you're telling."
- **Hitchcock anecdote:** Hitchcock hated working with child actors. A child actor couldn't cry, so Hitchcock leaned over and whispered, "Your parents have left you and they're never coming back." The kid burst into tears, Hitchcock said "Roll 'em," and the child looked genuinely sad because he was.
- Other illustration: if there was a $100,000 prize for looking most in pain, jamming a pen into your groin works because you really will be in pain.
- Implication: "certain motivations and goals, particularly sinister ones, are better made to be unconscious because if a person doesn't know they have them they will not give them away."
- **Anecdote on falsification of Freud:** Bloom's young son was asked who he would marry — instead of naming his mother (Oedipal prediction), he answered, "I would marry a donkey and a big bag of peanuts." Both parents are psychologists, "he hates these questions and at times he just screws around with us."

### Chapter 2. B. F. Skinner and Behaviorism

- Skinner vs. Freud:
  - Freud's psychoanalysis is "as close as you could find in science to a solitary invention."
  - Behaviorism predates Skinner and was championed by figures like **John Watson**.
  - Skinner came later but packaged, expanded, publicized, and developed behaviorism — both scientifically and popularly — through the 1960s and 1970s. He appeared on talk shows; his books were bestsellers.
- **Three radical views at the core of behaviorism:**
  1. **Strong emphasis on learning:** "everything you know, everything you are, is the result of experience. There's no real human nature." People are infinitely malleable.
     - Watson's famous boast, paraphrasing the Jesuits ("Give me a child until the age of seven and I'll show you the man"):
       > Give me a dozen healthy infants, well-formed and my own specified world to bring them up and I'll guarantee to take any one at random and train them to become any type of specialist I might select — doctor, lawyer, artist, merchant, chief, and yes, even beggar-man and thief, regardless of his talents, penchants, tendencies, abilities, vocations and race of his ancestors.
     - This view has "egalitarian" appeal: if there's no human nature, no group can be superior by race or sex.
  2. **Anti-mentalism:** A reaction to Freud — behaviorists felt that "claims about internal mental states like desires, wishes, goals, emotions and so on, are unscientific." The manifesto: develop a science using only **stimulus, response, reinforcement, punishment, environment** — real-world tangible events.
  3. **No interesting differences across species:** A behaviorist might say differences are "just general associative powers that differ," or even that "humans and rats aren't different at all. It's just humans tend to live in a richer environment than rats." Methodologically: study human learning by studying nonhumans.

### Chapter 3. Habituation: The Very Simplest Form of Learning

- Definition: "a decline in the tendency to respond to stimuli that are familiar due to repeated exposure."
- Demonstrated by Bloom shouting "Hey!" — second time startles less.
- Common examples: getting used to a ticking clock, traffic noise.
- Imagine life without habituation: you'd be startled every time someone waved their hand.
- It's "critically important to get used to things because it's a useful adaptive mechanism to keep track on new events and objects." New things may harm you, so you must notice them; but once they show themselves to be part of the environment, you stop noticing.
- Counts as learning because it happens through experience and changes how you think.
- **Use in developmental psychology:** habituation has been used by clever developmental psychologists to study creatures who can't talk — nonhuman animals and young babies (to be discussed Wednesday).

### Chapter 4. Classical Conditioning: Associating Stimulus

- Definition: learning of an association between one stimulus and another. Thought up by **Pavlov**.
- **Origin (scientific serendipity):** Pavlov originally had no interest in learning — he was interested in saliva. He attached apparatus to dogs and put food powder in their mouths to make them salivate. He noticed that when a person who typically gave the food entered the room, salivation would start. Pairing a bell with food meant the bell came to cause salivation.
- **Distinction between two kinds of stimulus-response relationships:**
  - **Unconditioned stimulus → unconditioned response** — innate, unlearned. Examples: poke with a stick → "ouch"; food powder → saliva.
  - **Conditioned stimulus → conditioned response** — learned via repeated pairing. The bell starts as a neutral stimulus; paired with food repeatedly, it eventually generates saliva on its own.
- **Reinforced vs. unreinforced trials:**
  - Reinforced = CS + UCS together (teaching the dog).
  - Unreinforced = UCS without CS (not teaching).
- **Extinction:** stop the teaching → the response goes away. Pattern: "bell, food, bell, food, bell [pant]," then just "bell, bell, bell" → give up.
- **Spontaneous recovery:** After extinction, waiting a couple of hours and trying again — the saliva comes back.
- **Stimulus generalization — Little Albert (John Watson):**
  - In *The Norton Reader*, Watson reported a bizarre experiment with a baby named Little Albert.
  - Little Albert originally liked rats (shown via video).
  - Watson banged a metal bar behind the baby while Albert played with the rat. Albert screamed/sobbed.
  - **Unconditioned stimulus:** loud noise (bar bang). **Unconditioned response:** crying/sadness/misery.
  - **Conditioned stimulus:** the rat. **Conditioned response:** fear.
  - The fear generalized to other things, including a white rabbit.
  - Bloom: "This is one of a long list of experiments that we can't do anymore."
- **Generality:** Classical conditioning has been replicated in many species — crabs, fish, cockroaches — and applied to human responses.
- **Applications to human responses:**
  - **Fear / phobias:** Plausible story: a dog (neutral) bites you (UCS → UCR pain/fear) and becomes a CS → CR fear of dogs.
    - Treatment by extinction: present the CS without the UCS.
    - Cartoon joke: a man trapped in a small booth filled with snakes, dangling from a building — simultaneously confronting heights, snakes, and the dark. The logic isn't bad (nothing bad happens), but the panic attack often makes the fear worse.
    - **Systematic desensitization:** expose person to the feared stimulus while relaxed. Traditionally via relaxation exercises; "nowadays they just pump you full of some drug to get you really happy," replacing aversive conditioning with positive.
  - **Hunger:** Environmental cues associated with eating, smoking, or drinking can generate desire — "those of you who are trying to quit smoking... certain activities... really make you want to smoke."
  - **Sexual desire / fetishes:** Speculative behaviorist story — "your lover's caress brings you to orgasm, your eyes happen to fall upon a shoe," and the shoe becomes a CS → sexual pleasure. "This almost certainly is not the right story" but may play a role.
  - Some treatment for pedophiles and rapists has involved "controlled fantasies during masturbation to shift the association from domination and violence... to develop more positive associations with sexual pleasure."
- **What is classical conditioning for?**
  - Traditional view: "It's just association" — CS and UCS happen together, so the response transfers. Predicts that strongest learning is when CS and UCS are simultaneous, and CR = UCR.
  - **Current mainstream view: preparation.** "you become sensitive to a cue that an event is about to happen and that allows you to prepare for the event."
    - Predictions: best timing is CS *before* UCS. CR may differ from UCR.
  - Example: A child beaten by his father flinches when the father raises his hand. Flinching is *preparation* for being hit, not the same response as being hit.
- **Final exercise — *A Clockwork Orange*:**
  - A violent murderer/rapist is given a drug that makes him violently ill, eyes propped open, shown scenes of violence. Afterward, encountering real-world violence triggers nausea.
  - UCS = drug; UCR = nausea; CS = violence; CR = nausea.

### Chapter 5. Operant Conditioning: Operating on the Environment

- Also called **instrumental conditioning** — Skinner's signature theory.
- Definition: "learning the relationships between what you do and how successful or unsuccessful they are, learning what works and what doesn't."
- Key contrast with classical conditioning: classical can occur even if you're strapped down and immobile; operant requires voluntary choices.
- **Thorndike's puzzle box:**
  - A cat must pull a special lever to escape.
  - Cats "do not solve this problem through insight." They bounce around scratching bars, pushing the ceiling, digging the floor, howling, etc.
  - Pressing the lever happens to get them out. Over trials, they stop other behaviors and just press the lever. Gradual improvement.
- **Analogy with Darwin:** Behaviors are like random variation; reinforced ones survive, others go extinct. Skinner "explicitly made the analogy from the natural selection of species to the natural selection of behavior."
- **Law of effect:** "a tendency to perform – an action's increased if rewarded, weakened if it's not."
- **Example — training a pig:**
  - Reinforce walking forward; punish walking backward → over time, pig walks forward.
- **Positive vs. negative reinforcement:**
  - Both increase behavior (both are rewards).
  - **Positive:** you give something.
  - **Negative:** you take away something aversive (e.g., remove a heavy collar).
  - Negative reinforcement is **not** punishment.
- **Shaping** (for complex behaviors):
  - You can't just wait for the pig to dance. Reward closer and closer approximations: stumbling → more dance-like motion → dancing.
  - Child-as-beer-fetcher example: reward the child for walking to the kitchen, then for opening the fridge, then for retrieving and opening the beer.
- **Skinner's examples:**
  - Skinner developed a **pigeon-guided missile** in WWII (never used).
  - Military history includes attempts to train pigeons and dolphins for deadly tasks.
  - Skinner taught animals to play **Ping-Pong** by rewarding approximations.
- **Primary reinforcers vs. learned reinforcers:**
  - Primary: food (positive), being hit/shocked (negative — aversive).
  - Learned via classical conditioning: e.g., saying "Good dog" — not innately pleasurable, but pair it with a treat and the phrase acquires reward value.
  - **Token economies:** Use seemingly arbitrary rewards (poker chips) — increases utility and ease of training.
- **Schedules of reinforcement:** Fixed vs. variable, ratio vs. interval.
  - **Ratio:** reward after a certain number of behaviors (e.g., every tenth newspaper retrieval).
  - **Interval:** reward after a period of time (e.g., dog dances for an hour straight).
  - **Fixed:** rigid schedule (every 5th time).
  - **Variable:** changing (sometimes 3rd, sometimes 7th).
  - **Slot machine** = variable ratio (depends on number of pulls, but the number is unpredictable).
- **Partial reinforcement effect:**
  - "Behaviors last longer if they're reinforced intermittently."
  - Slot-machine analogy: continuous reward then sudden stop → you give up quickly. Intermittent reward → you persist much longer.
  - Parenting example: telling a kid "No, no, no, no... Okay" inadvertently exploits the partial reinforcement effect — "the evolution of nagging."
- **Extension to humans / society (Skinner):**
  - Skinner argued the prison system should be reformed — focus on reinforcing good and punishing bad behavior instead of justice/retribution.
  - Argued operant conditioning should be extended to everyday life; people's lives would be fuller "if they were controlled in a properly behaviorist way."

### Chapter 6. Question and Answer on Behaviorism

- **Q (student):** Wouldn't extinction occur for things like poker chips?
  - **Bloom:** Yes — the connection with poker chips is established via classical conditioning, so eventually they lose reinforcing power. You'd need to retrain. Similarly, "Good dog" without occasional treats eventually won't cut it.
- **Q (student):** [inaudible — about Skinnerian involvement in prisons]
  - **Bloom:** Skinner and Skinnerian psychologists were never directly involved in creating prisons. But behaviorism shaped criminal justice thinking. Some mental institutions and prisons have installed token economies (poker-chip style rewards for good behavior) influenced by behaviorist principles.

### Chapter 7. Controversies and Criticisms on Behaviorism

- **Three behaviorist claims, all now believed mistaken:**
  1. No innate knowledge; everything is learned.
  2. You can explain psychology without mental notions like desires/goals.
  3. Mechanisms apply across all domains and species.

- **(1) Innate knowledge:** Considerable evidence exists for innate knowledge and innate desires — discussed later via language learning, sexual preference development, understanding of material objects. "There's nobody who doubts nowadays that a considerable amount for humans and other animals is built-in."

- **(2) Mental states / unobservables:** Mature sciences like physics and chemistry are full of unobservables. "It makes sense to explain complex and intelligent behavior in terms of internal mechanisms and internal representations." The computer revolution illustrates this — you can't explain a chess-playing computer without talking about its programs and mechanisms.

- **(3) Cross-domain / cross-species generality fails in multiple ways:**
  - **Tolman's maze study:** Rats learned to run a maze faster whether rewarded or not. Reinforcement helps but is not necessary. Tolman's work suggests rats have **mental maps** — internal mechanisms — contrary to behaviorist doctrine. "A huge embarrassment for the Skinnerian theory."
  - **Species-specific constraints on responses:**
    - You can train a pigeon to peck for food (natural response) but it's "very difficult to train it to peck to escape a situation."
    - You can train it to flap its wings to escape (natural) but it's very difficult to train wing-flapping for food.
    - Animals have natural response repertoires that learning channels and exploits.
  - **The Garcia effect** (discussed in the Gray textbook):
    - Food aversions in humans and other animals can form through association — getting nauseous after eating a new food (even if the food wasn't the cause) generates strong aversion.
    - Bloom's anecdote: as a teenager, he drank the Greek liqueur **ouzo** with beer, became violently ill, and now cannot abide the smell of ouzo. (Thankfully no aversion to beer developed.)
    - **The aversion is special to taste-nausea pairings.** New food + nausea-inducing drug → aversion. New food + painful shock → no aversion. We're hard-wired to connect tastes with sickness, but the effect doesn't generalize.
  - **Phobias and evolution:** The claim that phobias form via classical conditioning "is almost always wrong." Some phobias are evolved — humans and chimpanzees are particularly prone to fear snakes. The phobias you're likely to have depend more on evolutionary than personal history.

- **Chomsky's *Review of Verbal Behavior*:**
  - Other assigned reading. Chomsky is "one of the most prominent intellectuals alive" — still a professor at MIT.
  - This excerpt is "one of the most influential intellectual documents ever written in psychology" — more than anything else, it ended behaviorism's dominance.
  - **Chomsky's core argument:** When applied to humans, Skinner's notions of reward and punishment are so vague they're no longer science. This raises the issue of **unfalsifiability** (paralleling Freud).
  - Example: Skinner's *Verbal Behavior* tries to explain why we talk to ourselves, imitate sounds, create art, give bad news to an enemy, fantasize — all as "reinforced behaviors." But Skinner doesn't mean food pellets or pats on the head; he means things like "self-reinforcing" or "reinforcing because it makes your enemy feel bad."
  - Chomsky: This is not wrong — it's "just so vague as to be useless." Saying "giving bad news to an enemy is reinforcing because it makes the enemy feel bad" says nothing different from "giving bad news to an enemy feels good because we like to give bad news to an enemy."
  - Chomsky's dilemma: The law of effect, applied to humans, is **either trivially true or scientifically robust and obviously false.**
  - Example: "Why did you come to Yale for a second semester?" "Because the first semester was rewarding." That's true, but you don't mean pellets — you just mean you chose to come back. "There's nothing wrong with saying that but we shouldn't confuse it with science."

- **Legacy of behaviorism:**
  - Behaviorism has faded as a dominant intellectual field but remains a major 20th-century contribution.
  - It produced a "richer understanding of certain learning mechanisms" — habituation, classical conditioning, operant conditioning — real, scientifically studiable, important in animal life and probably human life. "They just don't explain everything."
  - Behaviorists provided **powerful training techniques**, especially for nonverbal creatures — useful to animal trainers, teachers of young children/babies, and clinicians working with severely autistic or severely retarded populations.
