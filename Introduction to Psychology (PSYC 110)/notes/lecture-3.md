Source: https://oyc.yale.edu/psychology/psyc-110/lecture-3

## Overview

This lecture introduces psychoanalysis as one of two "grand theories" in psychology (the other, behaviorism, is covered in the next lecture). Professor Paul Bloom situates Sigmund Freud as the most famous psychologist ever and emphasizes that his theory is unusual in scope: rather than being narrow, it attempts to explain everyday life, child development, mental illness, religion, war, and love. The lecture walks through the core Freudian ideas — unconscious motivation, the conflict between id, ego, and superego, the stages of psychosexual development, defense mechanisms, dreams, and the aims of psychoanalysis. Bloom then turns to the scientific assessment of Freud, drawing on Karl Popper's criterion of falsifiability and Frederick Crews's critique to argue that much of Freudian theory is "not even wrong." The lecture closes by showing that despite the rejection of Freud's particulars, his core idea of the dynamic unconscious has been vindicated by modern empirical work in language comprehension, social psychology, and terror management.

## Key Concepts & Definitions

- **Psychoanalysis**: Freud's grand theory of mind, developed over many decades, encompassing everyday life, child development, mental illness, religion, war, and love.
- **Unconscious motivation**: The idea that desires and motivations governing behavior may not be available to the person's awareness; you may not know why you do what you do.
- **Id**: Present at birth; the "animal part of the self" that wants to eat, drink, pee, poop, get warm, and have sexual satisfaction. "Outrageously stupid." Works on the **Pleasure Principle** — it wants pleasure and it wants it now.
- **Polymorphous perversity**: Freud's phrase for the infant's pure desire for pleasure.
- **Ego (the self)**: Develops as a reaction to the fact that pleasure isn't always available; plans how to satisfy desires or how to suppress them. Works on the **Reality Principle**. Its emergence symbolizes the origin of consciousness.
- **Superego**: The internalized rules of parents and society — a conscience. Also "stupid" — it tells you not just to avoid bad actions but to avoid bad thoughts ("You should be ashamed of yourself.").
- **Pleasure Principle**: Governs the id.
- **Reality Principle**: Governs the ego.
- **Psychosexual development**: Five stages of personality development, each associated with a particular erogenous zone. Getting "stuck" at a stage produces personality consequences.
- **Oral stage**: First stage; mouth is associated with pleasure (sucking, chewing). Premature weaning can produce an "oral person" (eats too much, chews gum, smokes; or is dependent and needy).
- **Anal stage**: Follows the oral stage. Problems with toilet training produce an "anal personality" — literally, unwillingness to part with one's own feces; manifests as compulsive, clean, stingy.
- **Phallic stage**: Focus of pleasure shifts to the genitals. Fixation can lead to excessive masculinity (in females or males) or, if female, a need for attention or domination.
- **Oedipus Complex**: Based on the myth of a king who killed his father and married his mother. The boy desires his mother, sees his father as a rival, infers his father will castrate him, and gives up.
- **Electra complex**: Freud's add-on theory for women; tied to the penis envy story.
- **Penis envy**: Freud's claim that girls discover they lack a penis, infer they have been castrated, turn to the father as a "penis substitute," and reject the mother.
- **Latency stage**: After the Oedipal resolution; sex is repressed for several years.
- **Genital stage**: The healthy adult stage.
- **Defense mechanisms**: Mechanisms that keep "horrible stuff from the id" from reaching consciousness; part of normal life, not pathological.
  - **Sublimation**: Redirecting sexual or aggressive energy into another channel (e.g., Picasso channeling sexual energy into artwork). "Gives up the details and keeps the energy."
  - **Displacement**: Refocusing shameful desires from a forbidden target onto a more acceptable one (e.g., a bullied boy kicks the dog instead of his father).
  - **Projection**: Attributing one's own uncomfortable impulses to someone else (e.g., projecting homosexual desires onto others).
  - **Rationalization**: Giving a socially acceptable explanation for a bad action (e.g., a parent who enjoys smacking his child says "It's for the child's own good").
  - **Regression**: Returning to an earlier stage of development under stress (children cry, suck thumb, seek a blanket).
- **Hysteria**: A category popular in Freud's time including hysterical blindness, hysterical deafness, paralysis, trembling, panic attacks, gaps of memory including amnesia. Viewed as symptoms of mechanisms keeping things unconscious.
- **Free association**: Freud's later technique (after abandoning hypnosis) for accessing repressed material; patients offer **resistance**, and the analyst helps them gain **insight**.
- **Manifest content**: What you experience in a dream.
- **Latent content**: The hidden implication of a dream.
- **Wish fulfillment**: Freud's view that every dream is a wish (sometimes forbidden) the dreamer has.
- **Falsifiability**: Karl Popper's criterion: what distinguishes science from non-science is that scientific predictions make strong claims that could be proven wrong.
- **Subliminal**: "A fancy term meaning below the level of consciousness, you don't know you're seeing them."
- **Terror management / subliminal death primes**: Studies in social psychology in which words like "corpse," "dead," "dying" are flashed too fast to be consciously seen, affecting attitudes.

## Detailed Notes

### Chapter 1. Sigmund Freud in a Historical Context

- The course will cover two grand theories — psychoanalysis (Freud) and behaviorism (Skinner) — across this lecture and the next.
- Unlike most psychological theories, which are narrow (e.g., a theory of racial prejudice is not a theory of language acquisition; a theory of schizophrenia is not an explanation of sexual attractiveness), these two are "theories of everything," encompassing day-to-day life, child development, mental illness, religion, war, and love.
- Bloom states this is "not a history course"; the point is that these ideas have critical influence on how we think about the present.
- Biographical facts:
  - Born in the 1850s.
  - Spent most of his life in Vienna, Austria.
  - Died in London after escaping there at the beginning of World War II as the Nazis began to occupy his city.
  - Not known for any single discovery, but for an encompassing theory of mind developed over many decades.
  - A celebrity in his time, recognized on the street.
  - Extraordinarily productive, in part because he was a serious cocaine addict.
  - Nominated for Nobel Prizes in medicine and in literature; got neither. Albert Einstein wrote a letter opposing the medicine prize, saying, "Don't give the prize to Freud. He doesn't deserve a Nobel Prize. He's just a psychologist."
- Universally acclaimed as profoundly important, but also disliked:
  - Personally: deeply ambitious about promoting psychoanalysis, often dishonest, extremely brutal to friends, terrible to enemies.
  - Anecdote: leaving Europe during the rise of the Nazis, the Gestapo demanded he sign a letter saying he had not been harassed; he signed and added, "The Gestapo has not harmed me in any way. In fact, I highly recommend the Gestapo to everybody."
  - As a Jewish "sexual renegade" he was identified by the Nazis as out to destroy sacred Christian notions.
- Some of Freud's stupider claims are what critics latch onto:
  - **Phallic symbols**: certain architectural monuments as subconscious penile representations.
  - **Penis envy**: the developmental claim that every female discovers she lacks a penis, infers she has been castrated, turns to the father as a penis substitute, and rejects the mother.

### Chapter 2. Unconscious Motivation: The Id, Ego and Superego

- At the core of Freud's importance are two big ideas:
  1. The existence of an **unconscious / unconscious motivation**.
  2. The notion of **unconscious dynamics / unconscious conflict** that lead to mental illnesses, dreams, slips of the tongue, etc.
- Unconscious motivation rejects the claim that you know what you're doing. Example: You may give honest reasons for marrying someone ("I love them, they're smart, I want kids"), but a Freudian might say you want to marry John because he reminds you of your father or because you want to get back at someone.
- If you protest, the Freudian replies these processes are unconscious, so of course you don't know what's happening.
- We already accept this unconscious operation in visual perception (we don't know how we see cars, trees, people) — Freud applies it to relationships and major life decisions.
- Everyday examples of unconscious motivation:
  - Liking or disliking someone without knowing why.
  - Arguing for something without being able to articulate the reasons.
  - Forgetting someone's name at exactly the wrong time.
  - Calling out the wrong name "in the throes of passion."
- The unconscious is not a rational computer looking out for your interests. Three distinct processes — id, ego, superego — are in violent internal conflict.
- **Id**: present at birth; animal part; wants pleasure (food, drink, elimination, warmth, sex); outrageously stupid; Pleasure Principle; "polymorphous perversity."
- **Ego** (self): emerges because pleasure isn't always available; copes by planning how to satisfy desires or to suppress them; Reality Principle; origin of consciousness.
- **Superego**: internalized rules of parents and society; conscience; "like a little angel above your head." Also stupid — it doesn't reason; it shames ("You should be ashamed of yourself. That's disgusting. Stop doing that.").
- The ego sits between two "screaming creatures" — id pushing for desire, superego shaming.
- Most of this is unconscious — like the workings of kidneys or stomachs; you cannot introspect and find them.

### Chapter 3. Personality Development and Psychosexual Development

- Five stages of personality development, each tied to an erogenous zone. Getting stuck at a stage produces lasting personality effects.

| Stage | Focus / Issue | Fixation outcome |
|-------|---------------|------------------|
| Oral | Mouth (sucking, chewing); premature weaning is the danger | "Oral person" — overeats, chews gum, smokes; or dependent and needy |
| Anal | Toilet training | "Anal personality" — literally unwilling to part with feces; compulsive, clean, stingy |
| Phallic | Genitals; site of Oedipus Complex | Excessive masculinity (in either sex); in females, need for attention or domination |
| Latency | Post-Oedipal; sex is repressed | — |
| Genital | Healthy adult stage | — |

- **Oedipus Complex** narrative (for boys, per Freud):
  1. Around age 3–4, in the phallic stage, the boy seeks an external object of pleasure.
  2. He turns to his mother ("Mom is nice, I love Mom").
  3. Father is in the way; the boy plans to kill the father. ("Every three- and four-year-old boy thinks this.")
  4. Because children don't have a good sense of the boundary between mind and world, the boy assumes the father can tell, and that the father is angry.
  5. The worst thing the father could do is castration; the boy fears this.
  6. The boy concedes — "Dad wins" — and gives up sexual interest, entering **latency**.
- Bloom's aside: as the father of two sons, both went through a phase where they explicitly said they wanted to marry Mommy.
- **Genital stage**: healthy adult stage. But you are "not out of the woods" — the id/ego/superego dynamic continues. The superego represses not only bad actions but bad thoughts; the id sends up "weird, sick stuff" (sexual and violent desires), and the superego pushes back. Material is repressed and does not even reach consciousness.

### Chapter 4. Defense Mechanisms, the Aims of Psychoanalysis, Dreams

- Freud held a **"hydraulic" theory**: repressed material slips out in dreams, in slips of the tongue, and in clinical symptoms.
- **Defense mechanisms** (normal, non-pathological):
  - **Sublimation**: redirecting sexual or aggressive energy — e.g., Picasso channeling sexual energy into artwork.
  - **Displacement**: refocusing shameful desires onto a more acceptable target — a boy bullied by his father may kick the dog and think he hates the dog.
  - **Projection**: attributing one's own impulses to others — Freud's classic example is a man who, ashamed of his homosexual desires, accuses other men of lusting after him. Freud suggested "perhaps not implausibly" that men obsessed with the sexuality of other men are projecting their own desires.
  - **Rationalization**: a parent who enjoys smacking his child says, "It's for the child's own good. I'm being a good parent."
  - **Regression**: returning to an earlier stage of development under stress — children cry, suck their thumb, seek a blanket.
- When these mechanisms fail, symptoms arise. Freud's era called this **hysteria**: hysterical blindness/deafness, paralysis, trembling, panic attacks, gaps of memory including amnesia.
- The therapeutic story (often dramatized in movies): a patient with blackouts or amnesia goes to an analyst, who provides an interpretation; the patient achieves insight and the symptom remits.
- Freud first used **hypnosis** to access repressed memories, then moved to **free association**. Patients **resist**; the analyst's job is to overcome resistance and help the patient achieve **insight**.
- Core claim of psychoanalysis: your problems reflect deeper hidden phenomena; once you see them, the problems will go away.
- Bloom shows a clip from *The Sopranos* (a character with panic attacks) — not a pure Freudian analysis (which would have the patient on a couch, not seeing a nondirective therapist), but it illustrates Freudian themes of dreams, repression, and hidden meaning.
- **Dreams** (Freud):
  - **Manifest content**: what is experienced.
  - **Latent content**: hidden implication.
  - All dreams are **wish fulfillment** — sometimes forbidden wishes.
  - Dreams have **symbolism** — the idea long predates Freud.
- **Literature, fairy tales, stories**: Freud believed they carry universal themes — aspects of unconscious struggles and preoccupations.
- **Religion**: Freud viewed the search for a singular, all-powerful God as seeking out a father figure that some of us never had during development.

### Chapter 5. Question and Answer on Freud's Theories

- **Q (non-traditional family structures)**: Freud's stages assume a child raised by a mother and a father; what about a child raised by a single parent, or never breast-fed? Bloom: Freudians have had problems with this; Freud focused on the upper-class European families he interacted with. A Freudian would presumably expect such children to be psychologically damaged by failing to go through the normal psychosexual stages.
- **Q (women and superegos)**: Do modern psychoanalysts still believe women lack superegos? Freud was notorious for claiming women were morally immature relative to men. Bloom thinks Freud would say women have superegos, just not the sturdy ones men have. Modern psychoanalytic scholars are mixed — some maintain deep sex differences, others want to jettison this aspect.
- **Q (sublimation vs displacement)**: A student asks if sublimation is a subgroup of displacement. Bloom: displacement takes anger from one target to another (you → her); projection puts your desire onto someone else; sublimation "gives up the details and keeps the energy." Example: telling your roommate they're studying so much because they haven't had sex.
- **Q (cross-cultural evidence)**: Bloom defers to the scientific-assessment section.

### Chapter 6. Controversies and Criticisms on Freud's Theories

- Bloom references *The Memory Wars* by **Frederick Crews**, "one of the strongest and most passionate critics of Freud."
- Two ways to reject a theory:
  1. It is wrong. (Example: the theory that autism is caused by mothers not loving children enough — once popular, just wrong.)
  2. It is so vague and all-encompassing it cannot be tested.
- The second is the main critique of Freud, captured by physicist **Wolfgang Pauli**'s line about another physicist: "That guy's work is crap. He's not right. He's not even wrong."
- **Karl Popper** introduced **falsifiability**: scientific predictions must make strong claims that could be proven wrong.
- Examples of falsifiable psychological claims (to be revisited in the course):
  - Damage to the hippocampus causes failures of certain sorts of memory.
  - Everywhere in the world, men on average want more sexual partners than women.
  - Exposure to violent television tends to make children more violent.
- **Astrology** as the model non-science: predictions are not wrong, they cannot be wrong. Bloom reads his web horoscope:
  - "A couple of negative aspects could make you a little finicky for the next few days."
  - "The presence of both Mars and Venus suggests you want to box everything into a neat, ordered, structured way but keeping a piece of jade or carnelian close will help you keep in touch with your fun side." (His wife gave him a piece of jade.)
  - A funnier, falsifiable horoscope from *The Onion*: "Riding in a golf cart with snow cone in hand, you'll be tackled by two police officers this week after matching a composite caricature of a suspected murderer."
- Freudian theory often fails the falsifiability test because it's too flexible.
- The "self-validation" problem in psychoanalysis:
  - Freud tells a patient, "You hate your mother." Patient agrees → Freud is right.
  - Patient denies → Freud says: "Your anger shows this idea is painful to you. You have repressed it from consciousness. I am right."
- The same dynamic plays out in scientific debate: scientists say there is little evidence for claims like "adult personality traits are shaped by psychosexual development," "all dreams are disguised wish fulfillment," "psychoanalysis is the best treatment for mental disorders." Freudian response: "Your rejection of my ideas shows they are distressing to you. This is because I am right." Often followed by: "You have deep psychological problems."
- Where Freudian-aligned researchers have made falsifiable predictions, results are bad:
  - No evidence that oral/anal characteristics (needy vs. stingy) relate in any interesting way to weaning or toilet training.
  - Cross-cultural studies of toilet training and weaning differences show no good evidence of corresponding personality differences.
  - Freud's strong claims about sexuality (why people are straight or gay) have met with very little empirical support.
  - The claim that psychoanalysis is uniquely effective at curing mental illness is "almost certainly not true" — quicker and more reliable treatments exist for most disorders. There is considerable controversy about whether the "Tony Soprano method" of insight makes any real difference for symptoms like anxiety disorders or depression.
- As a result, **Freudian psychoanalysis is almost never studied inside psychology departments** — neither cognitive, developmental, nor clinical sides — with rare exceptions, and even there usually critically. At Yale, Freud is more likely to be found in the history or literature department than in psychology.
- Despite all this, **the big idea — the importance of the dynamic unconscious — remains intact**.

### Chapter 7. Examples of the Unconscious in Modern Psychology

- **Language understanding**:
  - "John thinks that Bill likes him" → understood in a fraction of a second to mean John thinks Bill likes John.
  - "John thinks that Bill likes himself" → understood to mean John thinks Bill likes Bill.
  - This is not conscious; you don't know how you do it or that you are doing it.
- **Automatic action**:
  - Driving, chewing gum, tying shoelaces — when you are expert enough, you don't know you're doing them.
  - Anecdote: a friend hosting a party ran out of food and went to pick some up "around the corner"; an hour later they called his cell phone — he had gotten on the highway and driven to work (an hour away).
- **Social psychology — likes and dislikes due to factors we are unaware of**:
  - Harsh initiation into a club makes people like the club *more*. Hazing is illegal but "a remarkably successful tool."
  - The more you pay for something and the more pain you go through to get it, the more you like it.
  - Political campaign implication: "If you want loyal people in a political campaign, do not pay them. If you pay them, they'll like you less. If they volunteer, they'll like you more."
- **Terror management — subliminal death primes**:
  - Participants sit before a computer screen and answer questions like, "How much do you love your country?" "What do you think of Asians/Jews/blacks/vegetarians/people with different political views?"
  - Words like "corpse," "dead," "dying" are flashed too fast to consciously see (**subliminal**).
  - Effect: people become more nationalistic, more patriotic, less forgiving of others, less liking of other races and people from other countries.
- **In-class demonstration — Norbert Schwarz's research**:
  - Bloom splits the class in half. Each half is asked to think of someone they love.
  - One half lists **three** positive features of that person; the other half lists **ten**.
  - Result (not subtle): liking goes *up* in the three group and *down* in the ten group.
  - Mechanism: three features is easy, so the person seems great; ten is hard because "nobody has ten positive features" — the difficulty leads subjects to infer they don't like the person very much.
  - Subjects do not know this is why: asked why they rated the person lower, they don't say "because you told me to list ten."
- This is "a substantial and an important part of the study of psychology, and particularly... the study of racial and sexual prejudice." People have strong views about other races that they don't know about and can't control.
- Closing point: the particulars of Freud have largely been rejected, but the general idea of the dynamic unconscious has been "so successful" that Freud has become "a victim of his own success" — we underestimate his importance because his worldview is now hard to think outside of.

### Chapter 8. Further Question and Answer on Freud

- **Q (medication)**: Did Freud believe in medication? Freud began as a neuroscientist, intensely interested in the neural basis of thought and behavior, but the answer is **no** — he was convinced disorders like depression and anxiety must be cured by talk therapy and insight, not drugs. Modern therapists, including some who are not psychoanalytically defined, say drugs "mask the symptoms" — panic attacks "are actually not your real problem" — so making them go away with drugs doesn't get to the root. Both Freud and modern psychoanalysts think medications are substantially overused.
- **Q (dreams research)**: Bloom defers — will devote half a class to sleep and dreams. Some evidence that dreams relate to what you're thinking about and worrying about during the day, but the strong Freudian view of symbolism and wish fulfillment "has not been supported by the study of dreams."
- **Q (Electra complex)**: It is the penis envy story. Bloom: "Freud developed the Oedipal complex... and then it's as if somebody reminded him, 'Sigmund, there are also women.'" The Electra complex was an "add-on" to the main interest of the Oedipal complex.
- **Q (does penis envy fixate like the oral or anal stage?)**: Not a fixation in the same sense, but the woman's discovery that she lacks a penis "plays a fundamental role later on determining her allegiances in life and in fact her own sexual preferences and interests." Not just a short-period effect.
