# PSYC 110 - Lecture 2 - Foundations: This Is Your Brain

Source: https://oyc.yale.edu/psychology/psyc-110/lecture-2

## Overview

This lecture introduces the brain as the foundation of mental life and contrasts two opposing positions: dualism (the common-sense, religiously and philosophically widespread view that the mind/soul is separate from the body) and the modern scientific consensus that "the mind is what the brain does." Professor Paul Bloom opens with Francis Crick's "Astonishing Hypothesis" and Rene Descartes' arguments for dualism, then explains why scientists reject dualism in favor of a materialist account. The bulk of the lecture builds the brain from the bottom up: starting with the neuron as the basic unit, moving to neurotransmitters and how drugs alter them, then to massively parallel wiring, then to subcortical structures, the cortex, its lobes, topographic maps, and disorders. The lecture closes with hemispheric lateralization (via Michael Gazzaniga's split-brain work) and two notes of humility: the unsolved "hard problem" of consciousness, and the tension between a mechanistic conception of mind and humanist values such as free will and intrinsic value.

## Key Concepts & Definitions

- **The Astonishing Hypothesis (Francis Crick)**: "You, your joys and your sorrows, your memories and your ambitions, your sense of personal identity and free will are in fact no more than the behavior of a vast assembly of nerve cells and their associated molecules… you're nothing but a pack of neurons."
- **Dualism**: The doctrine that humans consist of two separate things — a material body and an immaterial mind/soul. Found "in every religion and in most philosophical systems throughout history"; explicit in Plato; most articulately defended by Rene Descartes.
- **Beast machines (Descartes)**: Descartes' term for non-human animals, which he claimed were merely physical robots.
- **Method of doubt (Descartes)**: Asking "What can I be sure of?" — concluding one cannot doubt that one is thinking, supporting the separability of mind from body.
- **Materialist consensus**: "The mind is what the brain does. The mind reflects the workings of the brain just like computation reflects the working of a computer."
- **Existence proof**: Modern evidence that physical objects (machines) can do complicated things like play chess, manipulate symbols, do mathematical/logical reasoning — making it possible that humans are physical machines.
- **Neuron**: The basic unit of the brain; a specialized cell with three major parts (dendrites, cell body, axon).
- **Dendrites**: Tentacle-like extensions that receive signals from other neurons; signals can be excitatory or inhibitory.
- **Excitatory signal**: Raises the likelihood the neuron will fire.
- **Inhibitory signal**: Lowers the likelihood the neuron will fire.
- **Cell body**: Sums incoming signals; if a threshold (e.g., "+60 or something") is reached the neuron fires.
- **Axon**: The output fiber along which the neuron fires; can be many feet long (e.g., spinal cord to big toe).
- **Myelin sheath**: Insulation surrounding the axon; helps firing work quicker.
- **Sensory neurons**: Take information from the world (e.g., retina to brain).
- **Motor neurons**: Tell muscles what to do (e.g., raising a hand).
- **Interneurons**: Connect sensory and motor neurons; "do the thinking."
- **All-or-nothing principle**: A neuron either fires or it doesn't, like a gun — squeezing harder/faster doesn't increase the firing.
- **Coding of intensity**: Achieved by (1) the number of neurons firing and (2) the frequency of firing.
- **Synapse**: The tiny gap (~1/10,000 of a millimeter wide) between the axon of one neuron and the dendrite of another.
- **Neurotransmitters**: Chemicals released into the synapse when a neuron fires; can excite or inhibit the next neuron.
- **Agonists**: Drugs that increase the effect of neurotransmitters — by making more, stopping cleanup, or mimicking them.
- **Antagonists**: Drugs that slow neurotransmitter activity — by destroying them, blocking creation, or pasting over dendrites so neurotransmitters can't connect.
- **Parallel distributed processing**: How the brain is wired, in contrast to a serial PC/Mac.
- **Neural networks**: Computational systems modeled after how neurons are wired together, studied in engineering and CS departments to build smart machines.
- **Subcortical structures**: Structures below/underneath the cortex (e.g., medulla, cerebellum, hypothelamus).
- **Medulla**: Responsible for heart rate and respiration; damage likely fatal.
- **Cerebellum**: Responsible for body balance and muscular coordination; contains approximately 30 billion neurons.
- **Hypothalamus**: Responsible for feeding, hunger, thirst, and to some extent sleep.
- **Cortex**: The outer, wrinkled layer; if flattened, about two feet square; ~80% of the volume of the human brain.
- **Lobes of the cortex**: Frontal, parietal, occipital, temporal.
- **Topographic maps**: Maps of the body in the brain; nearby body parts map to nearby cortical regions.
- **Motor cortex / somatosensory (sensory) cortex**: Cortical areas where body maps reside; map size reflects degree of motor command or sensory control, not real-world body size.
- **Projection areas**: The map-containing parts of cortex; less than one quarter of the cortex.
- **Lateralization**: The unequal distribution of functions across hemispheres (e.g., for right-handers: language on the left; math and music on the right).
- **Crossover**: Left visual field → right hemisphere; right visual field → left hemisphere; right hemisphere controls left body, left hemisphere controls right body.
- **Corpus callosum**: The huge web connecting the two hemispheres.
- **Hard problem of consciousness**: The puzzle of subjective experience — how a physical brain gives rise to feelings/what-it's-like-ness.
- **Mechanistic conception of mental life**: Treating the mind as an information processor / elaborate computer to be explained, rather than as something beautiful or mysterious.

## Detailed Notes

### Chapter 1. The Brain, the Mind and Dualism [00:00:00]

- The class begins with Francis Crick's "Astonishing Hypothesis": that all of mental life is the activity of nerve cells.
- Bloom predicts most students will not believe it at first; "Most people, in fact, hold a different view. Most people are dualists."
- **Dualism**: present in every religion, most philosophical systems, explicit in Plato; most articulately defended by Rene Descartes.
- Descartes asked: "Are humans merely physical machines, merely physical things?" Answer: No.
  - Animals = "beast machines" / robots.
  - Humans = immaterial souls possessing physical bodies — two separate things.
- **Descartes' two arguments for dualism**:
  1. **From human action**: Descartes lived in a sophisticated time with hydraulic robots in the French Royal Gardens (a "seventeenth-century Disneyland" — stepping on a panel made a swordsman jump out, or a bathing beauty cover herself). Bodies can be reflexive (knee-tap reflex). But humans do coordinated, creative, spontaneous things — e.g., free use of language. "Somebody says, 'How are you?' And I say, 'I am fine. How are you?' But sometimes I could say what I choose to be… 'Pretty damn good.'" Machines, Descartes argued, cannot make such choices.
  2. **Method of doubt**: He could doubt God, country, friends, family, even his body (madmen think they have extra limbs; he might be dreaming; an evil demon — the modern analog is *The Matrix* — might be deluding him). But he could not doubt that he was thinking. Thus body and mind are separable. Descartes concluded: "I knew that I was a substance, the whole essence or nature of which is to think, and that for its existence, there is no need of any place nor does it depend on any material thing. That is to say, the soul by which I am, when I am, is entirely distinct from body."

- **Common-sense dualism — five illustrations**:
  1. **Language**: We talk of "my arm, my heart, my child, my car," and also "my body" and "my brain" — as if we own and are separate from them.
  2. **Personal identity in fiction**: We easily follow stories where a teen wakes up as an older Jennifer Garner, or where a man dies and is reborn into a child's body.
  3. **Transformations**: Kafka's *The Metamorphosis* — "As Gregor Samsa woke one morning from uneasy dreams, he found himself transformed in his bed into a gigantic insect." Homer's Odyssey — Odysseus' companions stuck (by the witch) into the bodies of pigs: "They had the head and voice and bristles and body of swine but their minds remained unchanged as before, so they were penned there weeping."
  4. **Many minds, one body**: Slapstick comedy (*All of Me*, with Steve Martin and Lily Tomlin); one analysis of multiple personality disorder; exorcism beliefs across cultures.
  5. **Surviving bodily destruction**: Most cultures and religions hold this. In a Chicago survey: ~96% of Christians said they would go to Heaven; most Jewish respondents said the same (despite Judaism's less-clear afterlife story); most respondents with no religion *also* said they would go to Heaven.

### Chapter 2. Scientific Consensus against Dualism [00:12:06]

- Scientific consensus: dualism is wrong; "there is no 'you' separable or separate from your body. In particular, there is no 'you' separable from your brain."
- **The mind is what the brain does**.
- **Problems with dualism**:
  - It is "profoundly unscientific" — fails to explain how children learn language, what we find attractive, the basis of mental illness.
  - The conduit problem: Descartes couldn't explain how an immaterial soul connects to a physical body, though he knew the connection exists (banging your toe → pain; alcohol → reasoning effects).
- **Existence proof against dualism**: Descartes thought physical objects couldn't, e.g., play chess. Now we know machines can play chess, manipulate symbols, do limited math/logic, recognize things, compute. So it's at least possible humans are physical machines / brains.
- **Brain–mind correspondence**: Long known that head blows, syphilis, caffeine, and alcohol affect mental life. Newer: **CAT scans, PET, fMRI** show different parts of brain active during different mental tasks (seeing vs. hearing vs. reading vs. generating words). With fMRI we can to some extent tell whether someone is thinking about music vs. sex, or solving a moral dilemma vs. something else.
- The brain itself looks "disgusting" — "like an old meat loaf"; gray outside the head ("gray matter") but bright red inside because it's pulsing with blood. Aside: "It's good with cream sauce but everything's good with cream sauce."
- Bloom expresses sympathy for Descartes: it does seem ridiculous that such a fleshy lump produces free will, love, and consciousness. The goal of neuroscience is to make it less ridiculous.
- Plan: build up from neurons → larger structures → whole brain.

### Chapter 3. The Neuron: The Basic Building Blocks of Thought [00:19:28]

- **Basic unit**: the neuron — a specialized cell. Neurons look diverse, but a typical one has:
  - **Dendrites** — receive excitatory (+) or inhibitory (–) signals.
  - **Cell body** — sums signals; at threshold (+60 or so), the neuron fires.
  - **Axon** — output fiber; can be many feet long (spinal cord → big toe).
  - **Myelin sheath** — insulation around the axon; speeds firing.
- **Numbers**: ~1,000 billion neurons; each connected to thousands, perhaps tens of thousands of others.
- **Three flavors of neurons**:
  - Sensory neurons (e.g., retina → brain).
  - Motor neurons (brain → muscles).
  - Interneurons (do the thinking; link sensation and action).
- **Regeneration**: It was thought neurons never grow back. This is "actually not true" — some parts of the brain can regrow neurons.
- **All-or-nothing**: "A neuron is like a gun. It either fires or it doesn't."
- **Coding intensity** despite all-or-nothing firing:
  1. Number of neurons firing (more neurons → more intense).
  2. Frequency of firing (faster bangs → more intense).
- **Synaptic communication**:
  - Tiny gap (~1/10,000 mm) between axon and next dendrite = **synapse**.
  - When a neuron fires, the axon releases **neurotransmitters** across the gap; they excite or inhibit the next neuron.
- **Psychopharmacology (medical and recreational) consists of fiddling with neurotransmitters.**
  - **Agonists**: increase neurotransmitter effect (make more, block cleanup, or mimic).
  - **Antagonists**: decrease neurotransmitter effect (destroy them, prevent creation, or block dendrite receptors).

| Drug / disorder | Mechanism / effect |
|---|---|
| **Curare** | Antagonist; blocks motor neurons from affecting muscle fibers → paralysis → death (breathing depends on motor neurons). |
| **Alcohol** | Inhibitory. Paradox: drinkers feel disinhibited because alcohol *inhibits the inhibitory parts of the brain* (largely in the frontal lobes). Enough alcohol then inhibits the excitatory parts → fall down, pass out. |
| **Amphetamines** ("speed," "coke") | Increase arousal by increasing **norepinephrine**. |
| **Prozac** | Works on **serotonin**; makes serotonin more prevalent — depression involves too little serotonin. |
| **Parkinson's disease** | Involves loss of motor control; too little **dopamine**. |
| **L-DOPA** | Increases dopamine; temporarily alleviates Parkinson's symptoms. |

- **Wiring vs. computers**: The brain is *not* wired like a PC. Two reasons:
  1. **Damage-resistant**: snip a laptop with pliers and it dies, but the brain can sustain a lot of damage with preserved function; other parts can take over.
  2. **Speed paradox**: brain tissue is slow, computer wires are fast, yet the brain is extremely fast (faces recognized quickly). Solution: **massively parallel distributed processing**.
- **Neural network modeling**: Research (often outside psychology — engineering, CS) tries to build machines using neuron-like components. Caveat: this is a young field. "There is no machine yet that can recognize faces or understand sentences at the level of a two-year-old human."
- The visual cortex schematic shown is "extraordinarily simplified" — the brain is hugely more complicated than any current neural network.

### Chapter 4. The Different Parts of the Brain [00:32:58]

- **Things you don't need a brain for** (often studied in France via post-decapitation reflex testing — "kind of gruesome"):
  - Newborn sucking
  - Limb flexation / withdrawal from pain
  - Erection of the penis
  - Vomiting
- **Live demo**: Bloom drops the textbook (5th edition) onto a student's outstretched hand; the hand automatically rebounds. "This is something automatic, instinctive, and does not require your brain."

- **Subcortical structures** (below the cortex):

| Structure | Function |
|---|---|
| **Medulla** | Heart rate, respiration; damage likely lethal. |
| **Cerebellum** | Body balance and muscular coordination; ~30 billion neurons. |
| **Hypothalamus** | Feeding, hunger, thirst, somewhat sleep. |

- **Cortex**:
  - Outer, wrinkled layer; if flattened, ~2 feet square.
  - "Where all the neat stuff takes place." Fish — little; reptiles/birds — a bit; primates — a lot; humans — "a real lot."
  - ~80% of human brain volume.
  - **Lobes**: frontal, parietal, occipital, temporal.
  - Two hemispheres — mostly duplicated with subtle differences.

- **Topographic maps** in the cortex:
  - Brain surgery can be done on conscious patients because the brain has no sense organs.
  - **Dr. Penfield (McGill University)**: extending earlier dog-brain experiments, stimulated patients' cortex with a small electrical probe. Zapping various points made body parts move, or caused perceived colors, sounds, or touch.
  - Built up **maps**: motor cortex (left of diagram) and somatosensory/sensory cortex (right).
  - **Two key properties**:
    1. **Topographical**: nearby body parts map to nearby cortical regions (tongue closer to jaw than to hip — same on cortex).
    2. **Distorted by use**: map size reflects motor command / sensory innervation, not actual body part size. Tongue and face occupy huge cortical area; shoulder barely shows. The "sensory body" homunculus has huge tongue/face, tiny shoulders.
  - **Less than one quarter of the cortex** contains these projection maps; the rest handles language, reasoning, moral thought, etc.
  - As we move from rat → cat → monkey → human, less cortex is projection, more is "other things."

- **Methods to find what other parts do**:
  - **Imaging** (CAT, PET, fMRI) — see active regions during tasks like language.
  - **Brain damage studies** — lesions, tumors, strokes, injury. "Neuropsychologists love when motorcyclists drive without helmets because through their horrible accidents we gain great insights." If damage to region X disables face recognition, X may be involved in face recognition.

- **Disorders that illuminate function**:

| Disorder | What it is |
|---|---|
| **Apraxia** | Not paralysis; simple movements fine, but cannot coordinate movements (e.g., wave goodbye, light a cigarette). |
| **Agnosia** ("psychic blindness") | Not blindness; eyes are intact, but cannot recognize things. |
| **Visual agnosia** | Cannot recognize objects. |
| **Prosopagnosia** | Cannot recognize faces. |
| **Sensory neglect** | Not paralysis or blindness; lose the idea of, e.g., the left side of the body or world. |
| **Aphasia** (classic) | Discovered by **Paul Broca, 1861**: a patient with brain damage could only say "tan, tan, tan, tan." |
| **Receptive aphasia** | Speaks fluently but words don't make sense; cannot understand others. |
| **Acquired psychopathy** | Frontal-lobe damage robs the ability to tell right from wrong. |

### Chapter 5. Mechanist Conception and the Hard Problem of Consciousness [00:44:47]

- **How many minds do you have?** The brain looks symmetrical but isn't — real differences between right and left hemispheres.
- **Handedness**: ~9/10 are right-handed; right-handers have language in the **left hemisphere**. Left-handers are complicated — some have language right, some left, some elsewhere. Generalizations below assume right-handers.
- **Lateralized functions** (right-hander):
  - Left hemisphere: language.
  - Right hemisphere: math, music.
- **Crossover**:
  - Left visual field → right hemisphere; right visual field → left hemisphere.
  - Right hemisphere controls left body; left hemisphere controls right body.
- The two halves are connected by the **corpus callosum**.
- **Michael Gazzaniga** — one of the world's top neuroscientists, leading expert on the two hemispheres; lecture plays a *Scientific American* clip on split-brain patients (man whose hemispheres no longer communicate). Caveat: "people are just extremely pleased with themselves, so you have to ignore that while watching it."
- Methodological lesson: studying extreme/unusual cases (like split-brain patients) reveals how normal brains work.

- **Closing humility — two points**:

  1. **The hard problem of consciousness**: Psychology / cognitive science / neuroscience treats the mind as an information processor — find a program that solves a problem (face recognition, language, motor control, logic), then ask how it is instantiated in the brain. But subjective experience remains mysterious: a computer plays chess and recognizes numbers, "but my computer doesn't have feelings in the same sense." Illustrations shown: an old Star Trek angst image; Bloom's son Max being happy. Huxley: "How it is that anything so remarkable as a state of consciousness comes about as a result of irritating nervous tissue, is just as unaccountable as the appearance of the Djinn… when Aladdin rubs his lamp."

  2. **The mechanistic conception vs. humanist values**: Bloom is presenting a mechanistic view of mental life — explaining decisions, parental love, falling in love, etc. This may feel "repellant" because it sits uneasily with:
     - **Free will and moral/legal responsibility** (cutting someone off in traffic reflects badly; saving a life deserves praise).
     - **The intrinsic value of people**.
     - **The spiritual value of people**.

  - **Three possible responses to the tension**:
    1. Reject the scientific conception of mind; embrace dualism.
    2. Embrace the scientific worldview and reject humanist values — some philosophers/psychologists hold free will, responsibility, spiritual/intrinsic value are "pre-scientific notions that get washed away."
    3. Try to reconcile the scientific view with humanist values — a recurring theme of the course.
