# PSYC 110 - Lecture 8 - Conscious of the Present; Conscious of the Past: Vision and Memory (cont.)

Source: https://oyc.yale.edu/psychology/psyc-110/lecture-8

## Overview

This lecture continues the discussion of vision and memory, beginning with change-blindness as evidence that our perception of the world is far sparser than it feels. Professor Bloom then distinguishes short-term (working) memory from long-term memory, arguing that storage capacity, "chunking," and depth of processing determine what is retained. He covers retrieval (cues, context- and state-dependent memory, elaborative rehearsal/retrieval) and several types of memory failure: ordinary forgetting, brain damage (retrograde and anterograde amnesia, including implicit memory in amnesics), and reconstructive failures including suggestion-induced false memories. The lecture closes with hypnosis, the repressed-memory debate, flashbulb memories, and a Q&A on motor/skill memory and photographic memory. A central theme is that confidence in a memory is no guarantee of accuracy.

## Key Concepts & Definitions

- **Change blindness**: A phenomenon in which large changes in a scene go unnoticed, demonstrating that perception and memory of the world are limited and sparse.
- **Sensory memory**: "The sort of fraction of a second of sensory residue of what you're hearing and what you're seeing."
- **Working memory / Short-term memory (STM)**: Very limited memory held in consciousness; capacity is "seven, plus or minus two" (George Miller), i.e., roughly five to nine items.
- **Long-term memory (LTM)**: The "major storage system"; "like the hard drive of your computer"; huge but finite capacity (e.g., 60–80,000 English words, faces, songs, etc.).
- **Chunk** (George Miller): "A basic memory unit, something you think of as a single, individual entity." How material is chunked depends on knowledge.
- **Maintenance rehearsal**: Repeating information over and over to hold it in STM; typically does NOT get information into LTM.
- **Depth of processing**: The more deeply (meaningfully) you process something, the better it is entrenched in LTM.
- **Elaborative rehearsal**: The more you think about something (connecting it to images, jokes, explanations, counterfactuals), the easier it is to remember.
- **Elaborative retrieval**: People give up too soon when searching memory; persistent, cued searching recovers more.
- **Retrieval cues**: Things associated with what you are trying to remember that bring it back.
- **Compatibility principle**: You remember something better in the context in which you learned it. Also known as context-dependent memory and state-dependent memory.
- **Déjà vu**: A feeling that an event has happened before; worse with frontal lobe damage; one theory (per Marcia Johnson) is a "glitch" where an event is stored without a proper time/date stamp, so it feels familiar half a second later.
- **Retrograde amnesia** ("retro" for past): Loss of memory of the past, often a blackout period prior to a blow to the head because memories did not get consolidated.
- **Anterograde amnesia**: Loss of the ability to form new memories; appears in Korsakoff's syndrome, in patient H.M., and in Clive Wearing.
- **Implicit memory**: Memory that persists without conscious awareness, e.g., amnesic patients improving on the mirror-star task even though they don't remember doing it before.
- **Explicit memory**: Conscious memories you are aware of and understand.
- **Childhood amnesia**: Difficulty recovering memories from before about age three.
- **Flashbulb memories**: Vivid memories of socially relevant events (e.g., September 11, 2001), which cannot fully be trusted because they get reshaped through retelling.
- **Repressed memories**: Allegedly forgotten traumatic memories recovered later (often via therapy/hypnosis); their veracity is heavily debated.

## Detailed Notes

### Chapter 1. Distinctions between Short-Term and Long-Term Memory

- **Change-blindness demonstration (Dan Simons, at Cornell; advised by Frank Keil, now at Yale)**: A student on a college campus asks an older man for directions. Two construction workers carrying a door pass between them; the student swaps places with one of them. The conversation resumes and the older man never notices he is speaking to a different person.
- **Big moral**: Your perception of reality is "a lot more sparse, a lot more limited, than you might think it is." You think you have a full sense of the world around you, but if classmates changed positions, or the lecturer changed clothes, you wouldn't notice unless you had consciously encoded it.
- Memory and the visual system "exploit a basic fact about the universe, which is that most things stay the same most of the time." You don't need precise representations.
- Two ways the limitation becomes visible: (1) psychologists changing reality (change-blindness studies); (2) movies — continuity errors are pervasive and hard to catch.
- Review from last class: sensory memory → working memory → long-term memory; attention determines what passes through.
- **Storage differences**:
  - LTM: huge but finite; includes ~60–80,000 English words, people met, languages, faces, stories, locations, nursery rhymes, songs, TV programs. It is **not** true that you remember everything that has ever happened to you.
  - STM/working memory: very limited.
- **Number-recall demonstration**: Bloom reads 14, 59, 11, 109, 43, 58, 98, 487, 25, 389, 54. Most students get only a few. **George Miller**: STM ≈ "seven, plus or minus two" (roughly 5–9).
- **Chunks**: The unit is the chunk. Examples:
  - "L, A, M, A, I, S, O, N" = 8 chunks; broken into 4 words = 4 chunks; as French "la maison" = 1–2 chunks.
  - A binary string ("1,1,0,1,1,0,0,1,0,1,1,0") is harder for non-experts but compresses to a single number for those who know binary.
  - **Chess study**: Non-players can hardly remember a chessboard after 5 minutes of study; chess masters remember a legal/meaningful position at a glance ("Oh. It's the Fibonacci defense").
  - **Football coaches** have photographic memory for football diagrams; **architects** for floor plans — because they understand the structure.
- Storage in memory depends greatly on how much you understand the material (expertise effects).
- **Maintenance rehearsal**: Example phone number "514-688-9057." Repeating to yourself holds it as long as you keep rehearsing, but this does NOT typically get things into LTM. Like a character on *24* memorizing a message by repetition.

### Chapter 2. How Structure and Organization Affects Long-Term Memory

- **Depth-of-processing experiment**: 48 words shown to subjects (not told to memorize). Three groups:
  1. Press button for capitals vs. non-capitals (surface/visual).
  2. Press button if it rhymes with "train" (sound).
  3. Press button if it fits the sentence "The girl placed the blank on the table" (meaning).
- Result on surprise recall: looks (worst) < sound < **meaning (best)**. "If you want to remember something, the best way to remember it is to give it meaning, to give it sense."
- **Mnemonic techniques** (vivid imagery, songs, poetry):
  - The hippocampus is involved in spatial memory ("finding your way around"). Mnemonic: "The way I find my way around campus is through the hippocampus."
  - Name-memory books pair a face with a vivid image (e.g., Mr. Fish with spiky hair → fish impaled on hair).
  - To remember 10 letters, turn them into a song or "dirty poem."
  - Generate a "grand and obscene image" for arbitrary material.
- **Understanding > tricks**: At a deep level, understanding is the way to get things into memory.
- **Kite paragraph demonstration**: Bloom reads a passage ("A newspaper is better than a magazine… Finally, a rock will serve as an anchor…"). It is murderously hard to remember. Revealing that "This paragraph is about flying a kite" enables comprehension and recall. Context aids memory.

### Chapter 3. Memory Retrieval

- Getting information out: exam recall, eyewitness testimony, recalling someone's name.
- **Retrieval cues**: Associations that bring memories back (e.g., seeing a cracked window reminds you to replace it; seeing a person reminds you of the lunch date).
- **Compatibility principle / context-dependent / state-dependent memory**:
  - **Scuba diver study**: Subjects taught material either on a boat or underwater; tested later. Recall is better when tested in the same context as learning.
  - Implication: You'll do better on the final exam in the same room you learned the material in.
  - **Drug state**: People who learn while mildly stoned remember better when stoned again than when sober. ("So, if you study while you drink you should tipple a little bit before coming in to the Final exam.")
  - **Mood**: Material learned while depressed is recovered slightly better when depressed than when elated.
- **Elaborative rehearsal**: Connect a target memory to many things — images, jokes, explanations, counterfactuals — to make multiple retrieval paths.
- **Elaborative retrieval**: People give up too soon. Study with older adults trying to recall high-school classmates (verified via yearbooks): initial recall is poor, but persistent cuing ("Who was your teacher? What clubs? Sports? How did you get to school? Friends whose last name began with B, C, D?") brings more memories back over time. Some forgetting is real, but some "forgetting" is just insufficient searching.
- **Déjà vu**: Worse with frontal lobe damage. Per **Marcia Johnson** (chair of the Yale department), one theory is that a "glitch" causes an event to be stored in memory without a time/date stamp, so when the same event continues half a second later it feels strangely familiar. Not evidence of psychic powers.

### Chapter 4. Memory Failure

- **Why forgetting?** Three explanations:
  1. **Decay**: The brain is a physical thing; memory traces decay over time.
  2. **Interference**: New similar information (e.g., 114, 81, 66, 42) blocks recovery of the originals; related memories get confused.
  3. **Changes in retrieval cues**: As the world changes around you, the cues that originally accessed the memory are no longer present.
- **Childhood amnesia**: People have trouble recovering memories from before about age three. Hard to test because people fabricate plausible-sounding early memories. Trauma in children at age 1–2 is typically not later remembered accurately. Possible explanations:
  - Retrieval cues change radically (a clinical psychologist's idea: build giant tables/chairs to recreate baby's-eye view).
  - Language reformats memory; pre-linguistic memories can't be re-accessed afterward.
  - Neural maturation: memory parts of the brain develop around age 2–3.
- **Brain damage**:
  - **Retrograde amnesia**: After head trauma/loss of consciousness, memories from immediately prior do not get consolidated and are lost.
  - **Anterograde amnesia**: Inability to form new memories. Seen in **Korsakoff's syndrome**, patient **H.M.** (lives in Hartford, Connecticut), and **Clive Wearing** (film shown last class). Damage typically to the temporal lobe and hippocampus.
- **Implicit memory in amnesics**: Mirror-star tracing task. Amnesic patients each time say they've never seen the task before, yet they get steadily better at it. Implicit (procedural) memory is preserved even when explicit conscious memory is impaired.
- **Films illustrating amnesia**:
  - A "very bad" Drew Barrymore / Adam Sandler movie ("Definitely don't watch that").
  - ***Memento*** — highly recommended; the main story is told backwards, but a parallel story about "Sammy" told forwards (modeled after real experiments) dramatically illustrates what is and isn't impaired in severe memory damage.
- **Two general lessons**: (1) Normal everyday forgetting is expected. (2) Exotic brain-damage cases illuminate normal memory mechanisms.

### Chapter 5. The Power of Suggestion on Memory

- **Sam Stone / Steve Ceci children's study**:
  - Three children describe an event involving a man named Sam Stone. Students vote on whom to believe — opinions roughly evenly split.
  - 2,300 experts shown the films thought the man ripped the book, messed up the bear, tossed the book in the air — the majority believed it happened.
  - **Truth**: Nothing happened. A man named Sam Stone walked in, said "Hi," walked around, and left. The second girl was right.
  - Memories were **implanted** in the first and third children through:
    - **Stereotype**: Children were pre-told Sam Stone is clumsy, trips, breaks things, spills things. (The third child says "He always does that.")
    - **Suggestive/leading questions**: "Sam Stone came in? Did he rip a book while he was there?"
    - **Combination** over several months (the children shown were in this group).
  - The interviewer was naïve to the truth. Children weren't lying — they genuinely believed it. Police officers, child caseworkers, judges, and lawyers find such children highly believable precisely because they aren't lying.
- **False memories in adults**: Similar experiments succeed. Dramatic cases of people confessing to crimes they didn't commit — not because lying, deranged, schizophrenic, or delusional, but because they've been persuaded.
- **Filling in blanks**: If a story about a restaurant omits paying the bill, listeners later "remember" that it was paid — schemas fill gaps.
- **Elizabeth Loftus eyewitness studies**:
  - "Did you see **the** broken headlight?" vs. "Did you see **a** broken headlight?" — "the" presupposes a headlight existed; subjects later more likely to remember one.
  - Film with no school bus; asking "Did you see the children getting on the school bus?" leads people later to report seeing a school bus.
  - "How fast were the cars going when they **smashed** into each other?" vs. "**hit** each other?" — a week later, "smash" subjects more often report seeing broken glass that wasn't there.

### Chapter 6. Hypnosis, Repressed Memory and Flashbulb Memories

- **Hypnosis**: Some people are readily hypnotizable. Hypnosis does NOT magically retrieve stored memories (no "zoom in on the license plate" as in movies). It makes people eager to please the hypnotist, so they make stuff up — enthusiastically and believably.
  - **Hypnotic regression**: Asked to go back to one's 4th birthday party, a subject acts like an adult's idea of a 4-year-old, not a real 4-year-old.
  - Some subjects claim to speak languages like "ancient Egyptian"; linguists find these are just nonsense sounds from a North American imagining ancient Egyptian.
  - Hypnotism is an extreme form of what normally happens in eyewitness testimony — it "brings out the actor in you."
- **Repressed memories**: Heated debate, especially in the U.S., about adults recovering memories of childhood sexual abuse during therapy (often using hypnotic techniques).
  - Some psychologists: memories are real and Freudian-style repressed; therapy brings them out.
  - Most psychologists: such memories are untrustworthy and may be created by the therapist's actions.
  - Real legal consequences: criminal charges by women against their fathers based on possibly false memories; counter-suits against therapists for implanting memories.
  - Whether repression is ever real is controversial; that false memories *can* be implanted (without sinister intent) is not.
- **Flashbulb memories**: e.g., where you were on September 11, 2001. They feel vivid but cannot be fully trusted: people repeatedly tell and hear the story, and "stories change." Bloom notes his own memory of 9/11 may now be partly his wife's experience because he spent so much time listening to her tell it.
- **Resist the temptation**: "Being sure is no guarantee that a memory isn't false, reconstructed or even implanted."
- **Three main morals of the memory unit**:
  1. There are many types of memories (STM/LTM, implicit/explicit; possibly dedicated systems for faces, objects, spatial locations); they are dissociable.
  2. The key to remembering is organization and understanding. Intro courses (including Intro Psych) are among the hardest at Yale because the material is diverse; "highfalutin seminars" are often easier because background context makes everything understandable. "The more you understand something, the easier you'll remember it."
  3. You can't trust some of your memories.

### Chapter 7. Question and Answer on Memory

- **Q: What kind of memory is knowing how to play the piano?**
  - It is **long-term memory** (you carry it around for years) **and also implicit memory** (you can do it unconsciously). "It's not sensory but it's as if, put it crudely, that your fingers know and not your mind."
- **Q: Photographic memory?**
  - Claims of photographic memory tend not to be substantiated. Sometimes linked with savant-like skills in people with severe impairments (came up earlier in the autism discussion). Bloom is "not convinced" that true picture-taking photographic memory exists; there may be one or two case studies, but it's controversial.
