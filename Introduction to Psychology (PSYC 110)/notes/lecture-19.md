# PSYC 110 - Lecture 19 - What Happens When Things Go Wrong: Mental Illness, Part II

Source: https://oyc.yale.edu/psychology/psyc-110/lecture-19

## Overview

This lecture continues the unit on clinical psychology (also called abnormal psychology or psychopathology), framing it as a domain of tremendous scientific and personal importance. Professor Bloom argues that, although mental illness labels have historically been misused to ostracize social deviants, some conditions are real illnesses in the medical sense — they damage functioning, and treated people typically improve and do not wish to return to their illness. He walks through four major categories of disorders: schizophrenia, anxiety disorders, dissociative disorders, and personality disorders, reviewing symptoms, subtypes, prevalence, causes, and controversies. He raises the boundary problem (how to distinguish illness from eccentricity, evil, or unusual altruism) and closes by previewing therapy, noting the gruesome history of past treatments and the unresolved question of whether current therapies will look equally barbaric.

## Key Concepts & Definitions

- **Clinical psychology / abnormal psychology / psychopathology**: the study of mental illness.
- **Hallucination**: a sensory experience that isn't real (most typically auditory in schizophrenia).
- **Delusion**: a belief that isn't right / that you shouldn't be having.
- **Ideas of reference**: a delusional theme in which one believes events, conversations, or media coded messages revolve around oneself.
- **Disorganized speech**: babbling/garbled speech in schizophrenia.
- **Catatonic**: extreme disorganized motor behavior in which the person doesn't move, often freezing in a position.
- **Positive symptoms (schizophrenia)**: things present that are unusual (hallucinations, delusions, disorganized speech, disorganized behavior).
- **Negative symptoms (schizophrenia)**: absence of normal thought or affect (emotion).
- **Paranoid schizophrenia**: believes others are spying/plotting; often has delusions of grandeur.
- **Catatonic schizophrenia**: unresponsive to surroundings; often repeats what people say without generating own speech.
- **Disorganized schizophrenia**: delusions, hallucinations, babbling, unable to help self.
- **Amphetamine psychosis**: schizophrenia-like symptoms (hallucinations, delusions) induced by drugs that shoot up dopamine.
- **Anxiety disorder**: primary disturbance is anxiety — persistent, uncontrollable, unreasonable anxiety, or maladaptive behaviors to reduce it.
- **Generalized anxiety disorder**: constant worrying; ~1 in 20 people get it; can be paralyzing with physical symptoms (headaches, stomachaches, muscle tension, irritability).
- **Phobia**: intense, irrational fears focused on objects, events, or social settings.
- **Preparedness theory**: we evolved to be sensitive to certain phobic objects that were dangerous in our evolutionary history.
- **Obsession**: irrational disturbing thoughts that intrude into consciousness.
- **Compulsion**: repetitive actions performed to alleviate the obsessions.
- **Obsessive-compulsive disorder (OCD)**: affects ~2–3% of population; linked to heightened neural activity in the caudate nucleus.
- **Comorbid**: disorders that often go together.
- **Dissociative disorder**: involves dissociation of memory — unaware/separated from part of identity or history.
- **Dissociative amnesia (psychogenic amnesia)**: memory loss following a terrible event; can be selective or global.
- **Dissociative fugue (psychogenic fugue)**: global amnesia plus identity replacement; person leaves home and develops a new identity.
- **Dissociative identity disorder (formerly multiple personality disorder)**: two or more distinct people in one head.
- **Personality disorder**: a personality so bad it veers into mental illness (e.g., narcissistic, avoidant, dependent, histrionic, borderline, paranoid).
- **Antisocial personality disorder / psychopathy / sociopathy** (formerly "moral insanity"): selfish, callous, impulsive, sexually promiscuous; lacks love, loyalty, affiliation, compassion; easily bored, seeks stimulation.
- **"Going postal" / "running amok"**: terms (American; Papua, New Guinea Stone Age) for a humiliated person who tries to regain status through one act of terrible violence.

## Detailed Notes

### Chapter 1. Identifying Mental Illness

- Clinical psychology is the final topic; described as "what psychology is really about" for many.
- Personal relevance: many students currently in therapy or on medications (Prozac, Zoloft, Ambien, Wellbutrin).
- Estimate: about **one half of all college graduates** in the US will have some mental illness serious enough to require treatment.
- Even those not directly affected will see loved ones develop conditions (Alzheimer's, schizophrenia, depression, anxiety).
- Scope of "mental disorders" is broad — includes:
  - prototypical schizophrenic on the streets;
  - alcohol/cocaine and other addictions;
  - Down syndrome, autism;
  - old person losing memory;
  - teenager in deep depression;
  - severe social phobia.
- Hard cases at the boundary:
  - John Gotti / a killer without a conscience — mentally ill?
  - The Kramer character / actor on "Seinfeld" — zaniness vs. illness.
  - Extreme altruism — Batman (sleeps one hour a night, fights crime); a New Yorker case of a wealthy man with loving family who had a kidney removed to help a stranger ("I have two kidneys… I could save someone's life") whose wife called him mentally ill.
- Past/wrong views to dispense with:
  - **Demonic possession** (Gospels: Jesus exorcised demons) — not true.
  - **Social deviance view (Thomas Szasz)**: labeling someone mentally ill is a social decision to ostracize deviants and rid them of moral agency.
- Evidence that the social-deviance view has merit:
  - Dissidents in many countries labeled mentally ill and put in asylums.
  - Slaves in the US who tried to escape were described as mentally ill.
  - Until **1973**, being gay counted as mental illness in official classifications.
  - US presidents (including Bush and especially Clinton) called "psychopath," "schizophrenic," "delusional" by opponents.
- But going too far: some people are mentally ill in the same real sense as cancer — it damages functioning; ill people are not more creative/productive/vivacious; treatment makes them more competent and happier, and they don't choose to return to illness.
- Modern view: disorders are like medical disorders; schizophrenia is as much a disease as cancer.
- Prior coverage: amnesia (in memory unit), autism (in social reasoning unit).
- Sex differences in DSM categories:
  - Women: more anxiety disorders and mood disorders.
  - Men: more substance disorders, particularly alcoholism; predominantly male antisocial personality disorder.
  - Schizophrenia: roughly evenly matched.
- Today's focus: schizophrenia, anxiety disorders, dissociative disorders, personality disorders (skipping mood disorders — covered in Dr. Nolen-Hoeksema's lecture).

### Chapter 2. Schizophrenia

- **Prevalence**: about **1%** of the world's population; most common reason for being in a mental hospital due to severity.
- Called "the lepers of the twentieth century" — shunned, rejected, no known good treatment.
- Etymology: "split" + "mind" — a **split from reality**, NOT split/multiple personality. (Multiple personality is a dissociative disorder.)
- Genders roughly equally affected; strikes men earlier; can first occur in college/university age.
- **Five main symptoms** (four positive, one negative):
  1. **Hallucinations** — sensory experiences that aren't real; typically auditory (hearing voices, particularly telling them to do things). Some try to block them by humming, counting, or holding mouth open.
  2. **Delusions** — beliefs that aren't right. Often weird and clearly wrong; often believe they are famous people (many believe they're Jesus Christ). In **1959** a Michigan hospital had **three Jesus Christs** who would meet and talk. Richard Dawkins' *The God Delusion* is mentioned as a contested use of the term.
  3. **Ideas of reference** — newspapers contain coded messages for them; whispers are about them; aliens/FBI/CIA/government conspiring against them.
  4. **Disorganized speech / behavior** — babbling; odd motor movements; extreme = **catatonic** (frozen).
  5. **Negative symptom** — absence of normal thought or affect; quietness, stillness, no caring.
- **Three major subtypes** (of five):
  - **Paranoid schizophrenia** — others spying/plotting; delusions of grandeur; supernatural powers; "God" or "messiah."
  - **Catatonic schizophrenia** — unresponsive; echoes others rather than generating speech.
  - **Disorganized schizophrenia** — most stereotypically "insane"; delusions, hallucinations, babbling, dangerous, unable to function.
- **Core dysfunction**: inability to put thoughts and perceptions together, to sequence/coordinate them, to impose logical structure and realistic temporal sequence.
- **Vicious circle**: cognitive problem → loss of social contact → less reality checking → cognitive problem worsens.
- **Genetics**:
  - Strong genetic component.
  - Identical twin schizophrenic → ~**50%** odds of becoming schizophrenic.
  - Not 100%, so environment must contribute.
  - Genes create vulnerability; whether expressed depends on environmental triggers.
- **Environmental triggers**:
  - Birth trauma.
  - Viral infections — more schizophrenics born in winter (more illness in winter); epidemics/plagues correspond to jumps in schizophrenic births.
  - **Toxoplasmosis** (carried by cat feces): asked parents of schizophrenics "Did you own a cat when your child was born?" — yes responses correlated with higher odds.
  - **Stressful family environments** — but cause vs. effect uncertain (per "worst study in the world" cautions); schizophrenic children may also create stressful family environments.
- **Excess dopamine theory** (older):
  - Drugs reducing dopamine reduce symptoms.
  - Drugs that boost dopamine → **amphetamine psychosis** (hallucinations, delusions).
  - But incomplete: doesn't explain negative symptoms; brain structure differences also found (enlarged cerebral ventricles, reduced frontal lobe activity).
- **Cross-cultural mystery** (from Gray textbook): symptoms and prevalence similar everywhere, but **less industrialized countries have a better recovery rate** than industrialized countries. Three possible explanations:
  1. More latitude / less criticism in families.
  2. Less use of antipsychotic medication (which may impair recovery while helping symptoms).
  3. Cultural framing of schizophrenia as a transient disorder may make transient outcome more likely.

### Chapter 3. Anxiety Disorders

- Primary disturbance: anxiety — persistent, or maladaptive behaviors to reduce it.
- Normal to have anxiety; becomes a disorder when too much, uncontrollable, unreasonable, life-disrupting.

**Generalized Anxiety Disorder (GAD)**
- ~1 in 20 lifetime prevalence.
- Constant worry; can be paralyzing.
- Physical symptoms: headaches, stomachaches, muscle tension, irritability.
- Genetic component; related to major depression.
- Possible roots in childhood trauma → hyper-vigilance → more prone to GAD after difficult life events.

**Phobias**
- Intense irrational fears about objects, events, social settings.
- Diagram referenced (proportion afraid of each object).
- **Snakes**: ~**40%** afraid.
- Mice: many afraid ("mice are the worst things in the world").
- Cats: very few afraid.
- **Classical conditioning model is inadequate**:
  - Many snake-phobics never had bad snake experiences.
  - Many people with bad experiences (car crashes, electrocution, witnessing shootings) do not develop phobias.
- **Preparedness theory**: evolved sensitivity to evolutionarily dangerous objects.

**Obsessive-Compulsive Disorder (OCD)**
- Obsessions = intrusive irrational thoughts; compulsions = repetitive actions to alleviate them.
- ~**2–3%** of population.
- Examples: obsessed with being dirty → compulsive washing; obsessed with God's anger → compulsive prayer.
- Common themes: cleanliness, religion.
- Sufferers often know rationally the behaviors are unreasonable.
- Professor's personal example: worrying he left his door unlocked, running back to check.
- **Most common compulsions**: checking and washing.
- Neuropsychological correlate: heightened activity in the **caudate nucleus**.
- Treatable with drugs affecting **serotonin** (a neurotransmitter), despite seeming "Freudian."

### Chapter 4. Q&A on Schizophrenia and Anxiety Disorders

- **OCD vs. Tourette's**: Tourette's is a specific neurophysiological syndrome with involuntary tics, tremors, sometimes shouted obscenities/taboo words — no obsessive thoughts. OCD is broader; its behaviors are in service of thoughts.
- **Can individuals have multiple disorders?** Yes — disorders can be **comorbid**. Severe depression often co-occurs with anxiety disorders.
- **Superstition**: depends on severity. Mild ("Step on a crack, break your mother's back"; broken mirror) is harmless. When rituals escalate (must redo walk if a crack is stepped on; "must do X or terrible things happen"), it can verge into OCD; OCD often has religious/magical manifestations.
- **Are schizophrenics dangerous?** Statistically, not as a rule — more often victims than perpetrators; vulnerable; can't defend themselves. Paranoid schizophrenics could develop delusions to harm someone, but rare.
- **How permanent are medication effects?** Generally **temporary**. With mild depression, drugs (Prozac, Wellbutrin) can give time to rebuild coping skills; without coping skills relapse is likely (per Nolen-Hoeksema). Schizophrenia medication effects are also temporary. Drugs help people get out of a problem.

### Chapter 5. Dissociative Identity Disorders

- Lecture opens with a movie clip; informal poll: how many think the depicted person is faking?
- **Dissociation** = dissociation of memory; separated from parts of identity/history, except sometimes under special circumstances.
- **Normal dissociative experiences** (from Nolen-Hoeksema's abnormal psychology textbook checklist):
  - "Not sure whether one has done something or only thought about it."
  - "So involved in the fantasy that it seems real."
  - "Feeling as though one's body is not one's own."
  - Pharmaceutical experiments can produce these.
  - "Driving a car and realizing that one doesn't remember part of the trip."
  - "Talking out loud to oneself when alone."
  - "Not recognizing one's reflection in a mirror" (uncommon but still in normal range).

**Three types of dissociative disorders**:

| Disorder | Description |
|---|---|
| Dissociative amnesia (psychogenic amnesia) | Memory loss after seeing something terrible; selective or global; over half of people charged with homicide claim some degree (confounded by alcohol/drugs and possible lying). |
| Dissociative fugue (psychogenic fugue) | Global amnesia + identity replacement. Example: man's wife leaves him for another; six months later he's tending bar in Miami Beach calling himself **Martin**. When fugue wears off, old identity returns and new is forgotten. Bloom: "This is my favorite mental disorder." |
| Dissociative identity disorder (DID; formerly **multiple personality disorder**) | Two or more distinct people in one head. Example: woman shifts to a 6-year-old personality named **Donna**. |

- **DID is rare and controversial**.
- Featured in *Primal Fear* (Ed Norton's first big movie).
- Used as criminal defense — the **Hillside Strangler** claimed to be two people but was convicted (both).
- Typically starts early; mostly women; often involves recollection of torture or sexual abuse.
- Co-occurs with **PTSD**.
- Cause hypothesis: severe abuse + genetic/biological predisposition to dissociate. DID patients are unusually easy to hypnotize → may begin as **self-hypnosis** to cope.
- **Doubts about DID**:
  - In a recent poll, **less than one quarter** of psychiatrists believe DID exists.
  - **Between 1930 and 1960** — only **2 cases** in the US.
  - **In the 1980s — 20,000 cases**.
  - Largely an American phenomenon.
  - Highly therapist-dependent: some hospitals never see a case; some therapists see it in nearly every patient.
- **Suggestion hypothesis**: DID may be real to the patient but induced by therapists who, gripped by theories of repression and multiple personalities, tacitly or overtly encourage patients to develop separate personalities.
- **Continuum view**: philosopher **Dan Dennett** and psychologist **Judith Harris** note that we shift selves across contexts (friends, family, strangers). DID may be an extended/play-acted version of normal multi-self psychology, partly to please therapists/doctors.

### Chapter 6. Q&A on Dissociative Identity Disorders

- **Dissociative amnesia vs. retrograde/anterograde amnesia**: the latter result from brain damage and are long-lasting/severe. Dissociative amnesia is caused by specific life events and is often short-lived. All are brain events, but dissociative amnesia is more "psychological."
- **What happened around 1960**: the famous **case of Sybil**, made into a movie, hugely influenced beliefs that DID was real.

### Chapter 7. Personality Disorders

- Not actually covered in the Gray textbook.
- Personality = your way of dealing with the world, particularly with other people. Disorders = personalities so bad they veer into mental illness.
- Examples listed:
  - **Narcissistic** — extreme self-focus and self-praise.
  - **Avoidant**.
  - **Dependent**.
  - **Histrionic**.
  - **Borderline** — "really bad… just awful to be with."
  - **Paranoid personality disorder** — not paranoid schizophrenia (no hallucinations); just paranoid beyond normal levels.

**Violence and crime**
- Most murderers are **not mentally ill** in the medical/clinical sense — driven by normal desires (rage, jealousy, hate) taken to extremes.
- Mass murderers also not substantively different psychologically.
- Cross-cultural pattern: a humiliated male, repeatedly losing status, tries to recover it through one act of terrible violence — gains posthumous status. Called **"going postal"** (American); **"running amok"** (Stone Age Papua, New Guinea); present in every society.

**Serial killers**: many have mental illnesses, but varied:
- **Jerome Brudos** — severe foot fetish; killed young women, severed their feet, kept them.
- **Son of Sam** — paranoid schizophrenic; murders ordered by a barking dog.
- **Jeffrey Dahmer** — cannibal killer; killed to eat victims. (Joke from a colleague: "He has a severe eating disorder.")
- Other named: **Ted Bundy**, **John Wayne Gacy**, imaginary **Hannibal Lecter**.
- Many killers claim **dissociative disorders** ("it wasn't me, it was my alter ego Fred"); credibility unclear.

**Antisocial personality disorder (psychopathy / sociopathy)**
- Formerly "moral insanity."
- Bloom blends psychopath and sociopath into one category and uses "psychopath."
- Typically male.
- Traits: selfish, callous, impulsive, sexually promiscuous; lack love, loyalty, normal feelings of affiliation and compassion; easily bored, seek stimulation.
- Not necessarily unattractive: the **most famous psychopath is James Bond** ("a perfect psychopath in every regard"), as played by the original actor and Sean Connery — but Roger Moore's and Timothy Dalton's versions were not psychopaths.
- **Is it an illness?**
  - Psychopaths don't seek treatment — they don't experience a problem.
  - Many are reasonably successful.
  - The psychopaths studied by psychologists are by definition the unsuccessful ones.
  - Argument: the **successful psychopaths** "run the world" — excel in every field; cold-blooded, ambitious; conceal traits well enough not to be imprisoned.

### Chapter 8. Brief History on Therapy

- Central question: **Does therapy work?**
- History has been "gruesome and unsuccessful":
  - Madness viewed as being in league with the devil → tortured to death, burnt, sent to sea.
  - **18th century**: viewed as degenerates, sent away from society.
  - **19th century**: brief compassion — **Pinel** tried to establish mental hospitals.
  - Subsequent treatments largely unsuccessful (summarized in a video clip).
- Bloom speculates whether current therapies will, in 100 years, look as barbaric.
